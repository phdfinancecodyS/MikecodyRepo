## What This Helps With

If your brain won't stop scanning for danger — even when you're safe on your own couch — this one's for you. Not theory. Just what to actually do about it.

This version is written with LGBTQ+ life in mind, including identity stress, belonging, safety, chosen family, and the extra labor of figuring out where you can fully exhale.

## What Is Happening

Mental health problems don't land in a vacuum. If you're LGBTQ+, the issue may be sitting on top of identity stress, rejection history, minority stress, hiding, code-switching, or exhaustion from always reading whether a space is safe. That doesn't mean your identity is the problem. It means the context matters.

Your high-alert brain is scanning for threat constantly. Your threat system stays locked on even when you aren't in danger. This isn't paranoia—it's your nervous system doing what it learned to do. The problem is it doesn't turn off.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being made worse by unsafety, hiding, isolation, or being around people who don't actually let you be known.
2. Name three false alarms your brain has triggered this week.
3. Practice one grounding action when you notice the scanning (5-4-3-2-1 senses, or feet-to-ground breathing).
4. Set one anchor point in your day where you pause and check: am I actually in danger right now?

## What To Say

- I need support that doesn't make me shrink, explain myself, or scan the room first.
- Part of what makes this harder is the environment, not just me.
- My brain is in high-alert mode and I'm not in danger right now.
- I'm noticing I'm scanning and I'm going to pause and ground.
- This is an old pattern and I'm practicing a new response.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Treating identity stress like it's irrelevant background noise when it's actively shaping the problem.
- Waiting until the reaction is already big before trying to reset.
- Explaining the reaction instead of taking ownership for the impact.
- Leaving a hard moment without a return plan or repair step.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: pick one grounding move (5-4-3-2-1, cold water on your wrists, feet flat on the floor) and do it once today. Just once.
- Before bed tonight: write down three moments your brain sounded the alarm and nothing was actually wrong.
- Tomorrow: set one anchor check-in during the day — pause, ask yourself "am I actually in danger right now?" and answer honestly.
- Affirming support step: connect with one person or space where you don't have to justify or explain yourself before you can be supported — chosen family, a community group, or one person who already gets it.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- What time of day does the scanning get loudest?
- What's the last false alarm your brain triggered? What actually happened?
- Where do you feel the hyperarousal first — head, chest, gut, hands?
- What were you doing when you last felt genuinely safe?
- What's one thing that helps your body believe the danger is over?

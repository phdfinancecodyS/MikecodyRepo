## What This Helps With

You've tried everything and they won't take the help. This guide is for the helpless feeling of watching someone you love refuse to move.

This version is written with LGBTQ+ life in mind, including identity stress, belonging, safety, chosen family, and the extra labor of figuring out where you can fully exhale.

## What Is Happening

Mental health problems don't land in a vacuum. If you're LGBTQ+, the issue may be sitting on top of identity stress, rejection history, minority stress, hiding, code-switching, or exhaustion from always reading whether a space is safe. That doesn't mean your identity is the problem. It means the context matters.

When someone won't accept help, it usually means one of three things: they aren't ready to change, they don't trust your motives, or they think they should handle it alone. You can't force readiness. But you can stay connected, name what you're seeing, and keep your boundaries while their refusal plays out.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being made worse by unsafety, hiding, isolation, or being around people who don't actually let you be known.
2. Say clearly what you're seeing without judgment or argument.
3. Name one concrete help option and leave the door open.
4. Set your own boundary about what you'll and won't do, then step back.

## What To Say

- I need support that doesn't make me shrink, explain myself, or scan the room first.
- Part of what makes this harder is the environment, not just me.
- I'm noticing you're pushing back on help right now.
- I want to help, and right now you're saying no. I respect that and I'm still here.
- Here's what I can do, and here's what I can't do. That part is up to you.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Treating identity stress like it's irrelevant background noise when it's actively shaping the problem.
- Doing nothing while waiting to feel perfect.
- Trying to fix everything in one conversation.
- Skipping follow-up after the first step.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: accept that you can't force someone to accept help. Write down what that feels like.
- Before bed tonight: identify the one thing you can still do — staying present, keeping the door open, taking care of yourself.
- Tomorrow: reach out to your own support. Caring for someone who won't receive it is exhausting, and you need backup too.
- Affirming support step: connect with one person or space where you don't have to justify or explain yourself before you can be supported — chosen family, a community group, or one person who already gets it.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "What To Do When Someone Refuses Help" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

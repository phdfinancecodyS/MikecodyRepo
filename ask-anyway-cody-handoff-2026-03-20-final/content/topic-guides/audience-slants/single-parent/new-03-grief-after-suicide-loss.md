## What This Helps With

If someone you loved died by suicide and you're carrying a grief that nobody around you fully understands — this guide was written for exactly you.

This version is written for single-parent life, where mental health problems hit in the middle of responsibility, no backup, and the pressure to keep functioning even when you're depleted.

## What Is Happening

Single-parent stress changes the shape of a problem because there's less margin. You may not get to collapse, sleep in, or hand things off. That means symptoms can get hidden behind competence, and small problems can feel huge because there isn't much buffer.

Grief after suicide loss carries extra weight: shock, confusion, guilt about things unsaid, and sometimes anger at the person who left. Your brain keeps running counterfactuals—what could have changed this? Nothing you think right now will answer that question. You're in crisis-level grief and your job is survival first, meaning-making later.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask what this problem costs you as a parent, and where you need support before it becomes a crisis of exhaustion or disconnection.
2. Tell three people what happened so you aren't holding this alone.
3. Get to one grief group or therapist this week (not optional).
4. Do one physical thing every day: walk, cry, write, punch a pillow. Move the grief through your body.

## What To Say

- I'm carrying a lot with very little margin, and that changes what support needs to look like.
- I don't need judgment. I need practical help and a realistic plan.
- I lost someone to suicide. That's what happened.
- I need help carrying this and I'm not going to pretend I'm fine.
- I don't know why they made that choice and right now I just need to get through each day.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting a support plan that assumes you've extra time, extra childcare, or extra energy that you don't actually have.
- Waiting for the perfect wording while risk rises.
- Debating whether pain is serious enough before offering support.
- Putting paid resources ahead of immediate safety actions.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: let yourself feel whatever you're feeling without judging it. There's no right way to grieve this.
- Before bed tonight: say their name out loud. To yourself, to someone, to the room. They existed.
- Tomorrow: connect with one person who understands this kind of loss — a support group, a counselor, someone who's been here.
- Solo support step: identify one 5-to-10 minute window today that belongs only to you — no child need, no task list, no obligation — and protect it the same way you would protect something for your kid.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "Grief After Suicide Loss" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

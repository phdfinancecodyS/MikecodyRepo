## What This Helps With

You said something. You did something. Now there's damage. This isn't about being perfect — it's about what to do after you weren't.

This version is written for people whose work life runs hot: high demand, high stakes, low recovery, and pressure that keeps leaking into the rest of life.

## What Is Happening

When work takes a lot out of you, mental health problems often get mislabeled as personality issues or motivation issues. A lot of the strain is cumulative load. Your job may be training your nervous system to stay switched on, rush, perform, and postpone recovery.

Repair is the skill of reconnecting after damage. You said something hurtful, you missed your commitment, you showed up angry. Now the relationship feels broken. Most people try to move on without repair. Repair means you own the damage, you name what happened, and you commit to different.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this pattern gets worse after deadlines, conflict, overload, or long stretches without real recovery.
2. Say specifically what you did: I was short and harsh when you came home.
3. Say why you did it: I was overwhelmed and I didn't have capacity.
4. Say what you're going to do differently: Next time I feel overwhelmed, I'm going to say that instead of being harsh.

## What To Say

- Work pressure is shaping this more than I want to admit.
- I need support that works in real life, not advice that assumes I've unlimited downtime.
- I hurt you and I need to repair that.
- Here's what I did, why I did it, and what I'm changing.
- I know this apology is words and you'll trust it when you see the change.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Designing a recovery plan that only works on perfect weeks instead of on the weeks you actually live.
- Trying to fix the whole relationship in one conversation.
- Having the conversation when neither person is regulated enough for it.
- Skipping clear repair, boundaries, or follow-up after the hard moment.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: name the impact of what happened — not your intent, the actual impact on the other person.
- Before bed tonight: write one honest sentence you could say to them that takes ownership without defending.
- Tomorrow: say it. Not perfectly, not in a speech. Just the sentence.
- Decompression support step: build one deliberate wind-down window into today — not passive (scrolling) but active (a walk, a call, a physical task that clears your head).

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "Repair After Damage" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

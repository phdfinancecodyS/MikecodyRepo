## What This Helps With

If your brain feels like it's running through mud — slow processing, lost words, frustration — this guide is built for that exact experience.

This version is written for people whose work life runs hot: high demand, high stakes, low recovery, and pressure that keeps leaking into the rest of life.

## What Is Happening

When work takes a lot out of you, mental health problems often get mislabeled as personality issues or motivation issues. A lot of the strain is cumulative load. Your job may be training your nervous system to stay switched on, rush, perform, and postpone recovery.

Body-based symptoms can feel scary and make decision-making harder in the moment.
A short protocol helps you move from alarm to control.
You're aiming for steady function, not instant perfection.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this pattern gets worse after deadlines, conflict, overload, or long stretches without real recovery.
2. Name the symptom pattern and rate intensity from 1-10.
3. Run one grounding or pacing protocol for 90 seconds.
4. Choose one practical adjustment for the next hour.

## What To Say

- Work pressure is shaping this more than I want to admit.
- I need support that works in real life, not advice that assumes I've unlimited downtime.
- My system is spiking right now, and I'm using my reset plan.
- I need two minutes to stabilize and then I can continue.
- I'm not in danger right now; I'm in a surge and taking control steps.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Designing a recovery plan that only works on perfect weeks instead of on the weeks you actually live.
- Ignoring early symptoms until they become overwhelming.
- Making big decisions during peak body alarm.
- Skipping follow-up care when patterns repeat.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: simplify. Pick one task. Set a timer for 20 minutes. Do that task and nothing else.
- Before bed tonight: write tomorrow's schedule with as much detail as you need — times, steps, locations. Don't rely on memory.
- Tomorrow: take breaks between cognitive tasks. Your brain needs recovery windows — respect them.
- Decompression support step: build one deliberate wind-down window into today — not passive (scrolling) but active (a walk, a call, a physical task that clears your head).

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "TBI Brain Fog Plan" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

## What This Helps With

The thing you keep doing — checking, counting, cleaning, repeating — isn't random. It's a coping loop. Here's how to interrupt it without white-knuckling.

This version stays broad and practical so it works if you don't want a specific identity lens on the problem.

## What Is Happening

A lot of mental health problems get worse when people overcomplicate them or wait too long to act. The goal is to name the pattern clearly, understand it in plain language, and take one useful next step.

This topic gets easier when you catch it earlier and work one small step at a time.
You don't need a perfect plan to start.
Consistent reps beat one-time intensity.

## What To Do Now

1. Strip the problem down to what's actually happening this week, not the whole story of your life.
2. Name the pattern clearly.
3. Run a short reset.
4. Take one useful next action.

## What To Say

- This is real, and I can work with what's in front of me right now.
- I don't need a perfect plan to take the next useful step.
- I'm slowing this down so I can respond better.
- I care about this, and I'm taking one step now.
- I'll follow up after I reset.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Staying vague about the problem so long that you can't act on it.
- Doing nothing while waiting to feel perfect.
- Trying to fix everything in one conversation.
- Skipping follow-up after the first step.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: notice the urge without acting on it. Set a timer for two minutes and wait.
- Before bed tonight: write down the compulsion cycle: trigger → urge → action → temporary relief → guilt. See the loop.
- Tomorrow: when the urge comes, replace the action with a 90-second redirect — walk, breathe, text someone. The urge will still pass.
- Support step: identify one person or resource you trust — a friend, a therapist, a support line — and make one contact today, even a short one.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "Compulsion Interrupt Plan" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

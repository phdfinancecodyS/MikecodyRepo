# MODULE 3: The Words: How to Ask the Question Directly

## Why This Module Matters So Much

Module 1 got you to the place where you're willing to ask.

Module 2 taught you when and how to create the right context.

**This module gives you the exact words.**

Because here's the truth: **having the courage to ask means nothing if you don't know what to say.**

Most guides stop at "ask directly." This module doesn't. We're going to give you the words for every part of the conversation. Word for word. No improvisation required (though improvisation is fine once you know the framework).

---

## THE FUNDAMENTAL MISTAKE EVERYONE MAKES

### You ask indirectly.

**What most people do:**
- "Are you okay?"
- "You seem quiet lately."
- "Is everything alright?"
- "I'm here if you need me."

**Why these fail:**
Because they all give people an easy out. And the people who need to talk most are experts at taking the easy out.

When you ask "Are you okay?" someone who's thinking about suicide will almost always say "Yeah, I'm fine": even if they're not. They've practiced this answer. They say it to their parents, their boss, the checkout person at the grocery store.

**The conversation ends.** Not because they don't want help. Because you gave them an exit ramp.

### You need to ask directly.

Direct doesn't mean harsh. It doesn't mean clinical. It means: *leaving no room for evasion.*

The difference between indirect and direct:

| **Indirect** | **Direct** |
|---|---|
| "You seem off lately" | "I'm worried about you. I want to ask you something real" |
| "Are you okay?" | "Are you thinking about suicide?" |
| "Let me know if you need anything" | "I want to understand what's going on. Can you tell me?" |

Direct is harder to dodge. That's the whole point.

---

## THE THREE PHRASES YOU'LL USE

### PHRASE 1: Opening the Door

**What you say before you ask the direct question.**

> "I've noticed you seem [withdrawn / different / not yourself] lately, and I care about you. I want to ask you something, and I need you to know that I'm asking because I care about you, not because I'm judging you. Can I ask you something real?"

---

**Why this works:**

- **"I've noticed"** = You're not making this up. You actually saw something.
- **"and I care about you"** = This is coming from love, not curiosity or concern about your own guilt.
- **"I want to ask you something"** = Creates anticipation. Gives them a moment to brace.
- **"I'm asking because I care, not because I'm judging"** = Addresses the shame they're probably feeling. Removes the threat.
- **"Can I ask you something real?"** = Invites them to show up authentically. The word "real" signals: *This is important and I'm going to talk to you like an adult.*

---

**Variations that work equally well:**

> "I've been thinking about you. I've noticed [specific thing: you seem down, you're not answering texts like usual, you've withdrawn]. I care about you. Can I ask you something real?"

> "I notice we haven't talked in a while and you seem different. I'm worried about you. I'd like to ask you something. Is now a good time?"

> "I can tell something's going on with you. I don't think you need fixing: I think you need to talk about it. Can I ask you something direct?"

**All of these work because they:**
- Acknowledge what you noticed (you're not imagining it)
- Name that it matters to you
- Signal directness is coming (so they're not blindsided)

---

### PHRASE 2: The Direct Ask

**This is the moment of truth. This is where most people chicken out.**

> "Are you thinking about suicide?"

---

**That's it.** That's the actual question.

Not:
- "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?"
- "Would you ever...?"
- "You're not thinking about ending things, are you?"
- "Are you having suicidal thoughts?"

Those versions work too, but the direct "Are you thinking about suicide?" is the clearest.

**Why it works:**
- It's the actual word
- There's no ambiguity
- You can't misunderstand what's being asked
- It's the word they're already thinking

---

**If asking about suicide feels impossible to you, these alternatives work:**

> "Are you thinking about killing yourself?"

> "Have you thought about ending your life?"

> "Are you thinking about harming yourself?"

**Pick whichever one feels most natural in your mouth.** Practice saying it out loud (yes, actually say it) so it doesn't feel foreign in the moment.

---

**What NOT to say:**

> "You're not going to do anything stupid, are you?"

This frames suicide as "stupid" (shame), and gives them an out by asking it as a question that assumes "no."

> "You wouldn't actually hurt yourself, would you?"

Same problem. And the "actually" creates distance.

> "Promise me you won't do anything."

This is a request that can't be kept and creates false security. Also: now they're worried about disappointing you if they're having thoughts.

> "Are you thinking about committing suicide?"

"Committing" has legal/criminal connotations. Avoid it. "Dying by suicide" or "thinking about suicide" instead.

---

### PHRASE 3: What To Say If They Say Yes

**This is the most important moment of the entire conversation.**

What you say in the first 60 seconds after they confirm sets the tone for everything that follows.

> "Thank you for telling me. I'm really glad you told me. I'm not going anywhere. I want to understand more about what's been going on."

---

**Why this matters:**

- **"Thank you"** = You're honoring the vulnerability it took to tell you
- **"I'm really glad"** = You're not panicking. You're steady. You've got this.
- **"I'm not going anywhere"** = This is the reassurance they most need to hear
- **"I want to understand"** = You're shifting into listener mode. Not fixer mode.

---

**After you say that, the next thing you do is listen.**

Not fix. Not lecture. Not problem-solve.

Just listen.

Ask them to tell you more:

> "How long have you been feeling this way?"

> "What's been making you feel this way?"

> "Can you tell me what that's like?"

Then be quiet. Let them talk. This is their turn.

---

## What To Do If They Say "No, I'm Fine" (But Something Feels Off)

**Sometimes people will deny it even when they're struggling.**

Not because they don't trust you. But because:
- The shame is overwhelming
- They think you'll think less of them
- They're not ready to admit it
- They want to protect you from the burden

**You're not done yet.** You note the feeling, but you don't push hard.

Say something like:

> "Okay. I hear you. But I'm noticing you [seem really withdrawn / haven't been yourself / don't seem happy]. I'm going to be around. And I care about you no matter what. If things change or you want to talk, I'm here. No judgment."

Then you:
- Stay in the relationship
- Check in (not obsessively, but genuinely)
- Watch for changes
- Try again if things get worse

**Sometimes asking once isn't enough. And that's okay.** You planted a seed that says: *This person notices me. This person cares.*

---

## What To Do If They Deflect or Get Uncomfortable

**Sometimes they'll change the subject or get defensive.**

"Are you thinking about suicide?"

"What? No. Why would you even ask me that? That's weird."

**This is normal.** Shame makes people defensive.

You can acknowledge their discomfort without backing down:

> "I know that's a weird thing to ask. And I wouldn't ask if I didn't care about you. I've noticed [specific thing], and I care about you enough to ask the hard question. So... are you?"

Then be quiet. Let them answer.

---

## The Different Responses You'll Get

### Response #1: "No, I'm not."

**Accept this.** But note it.

If their body language or tone says otherwise, you can say:

> "Okay. I believe you. But I've noticed [specific thing], so I want to keep checking in. Is that okay?"

**Then follow up.** Not in a creepy way. Just real check-ins.

---

### Response #2: "I don't know" or "Maybe" or "I've thought about it but..."

**These are the people who need you most.**

This is not "no." This is active uncertainty. This is: *I'm thinking about it, I'm not sure, I'm scared, I'm considering.*

This is where you move into the next phase: *understanding the seriousness of it.*

Transition with:

> "Thank you for being honest. I want to understand what that means. Can you tell me more?"

---

### Response #3: "Yes, I am."

**This is where everything changes.** And this is covered in detail in Part 2 of the program (Module 4: When They Say Yes).

For now, your job is:

1. **Stay calm**
2. **Stay present**
3. **Don't panic and call 911 immediately** (unless they're in active danger right now)
4. **Listen**
5. **You'll learn the next steps in the next module**

---

## Practice: Saying It Out Loud

**This is crucial. You cannot skip this.**

The reason people fumble in the actual moment is because they've never said the words out loud.

Your brain will freeze if you're trying to figure out what to say at the same time you're trying to say it.

### WORKSHEET 3: Say It Out Loud

**Your assignment:**

1. **Write out the three phrases:**
   - Your opening (customize it with actual observations about the person)
   - Your direct ask (pick the version that feels most natural)
   - Your response if they say yes

2. **Say them out loud.** Yes, actually speak them. Multiple times. In the car. In the shower. In front of a mirror.

3. **Notice what feels wrong.** If a word doesn't feel natural, change it. Make it yours.

4. **Do this for at least 3 people in your life you're concerned about.** Different people will need slightly different language based on your relationship and their personality.

5. **Practice the listening part too.** After you ask, practice being quiet. Count to five before you say anything else.

**Why this works:**

Muscle memory is real. When you're nervous in the actual moment, your brain will return to what you practiced. If you practiced fumbling, you'll fumble. If you practiced clarity, you'll be clear.

## The Most Common Mistakes

### Mistake #1: Asking too many questions at once

**Don't do this:**
> "Are you thinking about suicide? Do you have a plan? Are you going to do something? When?"

**Do this:**
> "Are you thinking about suicide?"

*Wait for answer.*

> "How long have you been feeling this way?"

*Then the rest comes in Part 3 (the risk assessment).*

---

### Mistake #2: Segueing into advice immediately

**Don't do this:**
> "Are you thinking about suicide? Because you should really try therapy. And exercise helps. And you need to stop isolating yourself."

**Do this:**
> "Are you thinking about suicide?"

*Listen. Just listen. Advice comes later, if at all.*

---

### Mistake #3: Asking and then disappearing

**Don't do this:**
> "Are you thinking about suicide?"

*They say yes.*

> "Oh my God. Okay. I don't know what to say. I have to go."

**Do this:**
> "Are you thinking about suicide?"

*They say yes.*

> "Thank you for telling me. I'm not going anywhere."

*And you actually stay (or come back later that same day).*

---

### Mistake #4: Getting mad or disappointed

**Don't do this:**
> "Are you thinking about suicide?"

*They say yes.*

> "Why would you think like that? What's wrong with you? You have everything!"

**Do this:**
> "Are you thinking about suicide?"

*They say yes.*

> "Thank you for telling me. That took courage. I care about you."

*And you mean it.*

---

## The Neuroscience of Why Direct Works

When you ask indirectly, the other person's brain has to do translation work. They have to figure out what you're really asking. And if they don't want to hear what you're asking, the translation is easy: *Oh, they just want to make sure I'm okay. I'm fine. Moving on.*

When you ask directly, there's no translation work. There's no out. The question lands exactly as it was meant to land.

This doesn't mean they'll answer yes. But they'll answer honestly. Because there's nowhere to hide.

---

## After You've Asked

**If they say no:** Respect that, but stay attentive.

**If they say yes or "maybe":** You move into the next part of the program. Module 4 (When They Say Yes) walks you through exactly what happens next.

**If they get defensive:** Understand it as shame, not rejection. And try again later if you can.

---

## The Hardest Part

The hardest part isn't saying the words. It's believing you have the right to ask.

You do.

When you care about someone and you notice they're struggling, you don't just get to notice. You get to *ask*. That's what love is. That's what showing up looks like.

The person who doesn't ask because they're scared is still someone who cares. But the person who asks despite being scared? **That's the person who changes things.**

---

## One More Thing

**You don't have to be perfect at this.**

You can ask awkwardly. You can stumble over the words. You can have this conversation imperfectly.

The people it helps will know you tried. Will know you cared enough to try.

That's what matters.

Now go practice the words. Out loud. Multiple times.

Because one day, someone you care about will be thinking about suicide. And because you practiced, you'll be ready.

# "Ask Anyway": Lead Magnet Content
## What to Say When You're Worried Someone Might Be Struggling

---

## PAGE 1: Opening Letter

### This is from both of us.

I'm a Licensed Clinical Social Worker. I've had the conversation about suicide hundreds of times: with clients, with colleagues, with family and friends.

But here's the thing nobody tells you: having clinical training doesn't make it easier. It just means I know *why* the hard conversations matter.

My co-author lost his best friend to suicide. He didn't know what to say before it happened. Now he's spent years learning what he wishes he'd known.

So this guide isn't from someone in an ivory tower. It's from someone who does this work clinically, and someone who lived through the deepest regret about not knowing what to say.

**We wrote this because the world is full of people who care deeply but feel completely frozen around this one conversation.** That's not a character flaw. It's a skill gap. And skills can be learned.

This guide gives you the 5 things you actually need. Nothing more.

---

## PAGE 2: The Problem (Everyone Knows This)

### You notice something's off.

Maybe your friend has been canceling plans. Maybe your brother seems hollowed out. Maybe your coworker made a joke that didn't sound like a joke. Maybe your kid has changed.

Your gut says: *Something's wrong.*

And then your brain says: *But what if I ask and make it worse? What if I say the wrong thing? What if I'm overreacting? Maybe it's not my place.*

And so you don't ask.

You tell yourself you'll bring it up later. Or you drop a vague text. Or you mention it to someone else instead of him.

**Here's what we know:** In almost every case where someone dies by suicide, people around them saw warning signs they didn't know how to approach.

Not because they didn't care. Because they didn't know *how*.

---

## PAGE 3: Here's What You're Probably Doing Wrong (The Opening)

### You're being too indirect.

When we're scared, we soften the conversation. We hope the other person will pick up what we're putting down. We ask "Are you okay?" and hope they'll volunteer: "Actually, no, I'm thinking about suicide."

*That's not how this works.*

People who are struggling are expert at saying "I'm fine." They've practiced it. They say it to their parents, their boss, their doctor. It's the default away-message for pain.

So when you ask "Are you okay?" and they say "Yeah, I'm good": even if they're not: **the conversation is over.**

The indirect approach gives people an out. And the people who need to talk most are often the most likely to take it.

**You need a different strategy. You need to ask directly.**

---

## PAGE 4: The Words (Ready to Use)

### You'll use three sets of words. Here they are.

**OPENING THE DOOR**
*Create space for a real conversation. Find a private moment (not with an audience). Sit. Look at them.*

> "I've noticed you seem [different / withdrawn / not yourself] lately, and I care about you. I want to ask you something, and I need you to know I'm asking because I care, not because I'm judging. Can I ask you something real?"

*Wait for them to nod or say yes.*

---

**THE DIRECT ASK**
*Just say it. Directly. Use these exact words or close to them.*

> "Are you thinking about suicide?"

Or if that feels impossible, these work too:
- "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?"
- "Have you thought about ending your life?"

**Do NOT say:**
- "You're not thinking about doing anything stupid, are you?"
- "You wouldn't actually hurt yourself, right?"
- "Please don't do anything crazy."

Those versions give people room to say "No, I wouldn't ACTUALLY do it": which lets them off the hook and closes the conversation.

---

**IF THEY SAY YES**
*Your only job in this moment is to not end the conversation. Stay.*

> "Thank you for telling me. I'm really glad you did. I'm not going anywhere. Can you tell me more about what's been going on?"

Then listen. Don't try to fix. Don't argue. Don't say "you have so much to live for." Just listen and stay present.

---

## PAGE 5: What Kills the Conversation (And How to Stay in It)

### Here's what NOT to say (and why it backfires):

| What you might say | Why it bombs | What actually works |
|---|---|---|
| "You have so much to live for" | Minimizes their pain. They've heard this. It doesn't land. | "Tell me what's been making you feel like this" |
| "You should just talk to someone" | Dismissive. Feels like you're routing them away from you. | "I'm here. And I think finding professional support would help too. Can we figure that out together?" |
| "Other people have it worse" | Invalidation. Their pain is still their pain. | Say nothing. Just listen. |
| "I had no idea you felt this way" | Centers your feelings, not theirs. | "I'm really glad you told me." |
| "Promise me you won't do anything" | Doesn't work. Cannot be enforced. Creates false security. | "I want to help you stay safe. Can we talk about that?" |
| "Maybe you just need to think positive" | Toxic. Pain isn't fixed by mindset. | Stay silent and listen. |

**The pattern:** The things that feel helpful are often the things that stop the conversation.

The things that help are: staying present, validating their experience (not agreeing with their pain), and listening without trying to solve it immediately.

---

## PAGE 6: If They Confirm (Your Next 3 Steps)

### What happens after they say "yes"?

**STEP 1: Stay and listen: don't transfer responsibility.** 

Your job is NOT to be their therapist. Your job is to not end the conversation and to help them connect to professional support. There's a difference.

Ask them: "How long have you been feeling this way? What's been happening?" Let them talk.

---

**STEP 2: Understand how serious it is.** 

You don't need to diagnose anything. You just need to know: Is this active crisis (they're planning to act today/tonight) or is this ongoing struggle?

Ask: 
- "Do you have a plan for how you would do it?" (If they have a specific plan, that's more serious.)
- "Do you have access to [the means]?" (If yes: firearms, pills, etc.: that's more serious.)
- "Are you planning to act on this soon?" (Today? This week?)

Their answers tell you whether this is "I need to get you professional help today" or "I need to help you get professional support this week."

---

**STEP 3: Connect them to help.**

**If this is active crisis (they're planning something imminent):**
- Call 988 (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline) together, from your phone
- Or go to the nearest ER together
- Do NOT leave them alone

**If this is ongoing struggle:**
- Have them call their therapist/doctor if they already have one
- Help them text 988 if they don't have professional support yet
- Stay in touch. Don't ghost after this conversation.
- Check in tomorrow. Check in next week.

**The "warm handoff" matters.** Don't just say "go call this number." Actually help them make the call or appointment.

---

## PAGE 7: You're Not Responsible for Saving Them (But This Conversation Matters)

### You did something really hard.

You asked. You stayed. You listened. You helped them connect to help.

That took courage. Especially if you were terrified.

**One important thing:** You are not responsible for making them better. That's not your job. Your job was to have a real conversation and help them connect to professional support. You did that.

The rest is their journey: and the professionals' job.

But your conversation mattered. From here, the actual healing work with a therapist/counselor can begin.

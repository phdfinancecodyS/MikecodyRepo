## What This Helps With

You're trying to be a good parent while running on fumes. If you feel like you're failing at the thing that matters most, this guide is honest about what helps.
This version is written for people whose faith, spirituality, or sacred practices matter and should be respected as part of healing without assuming a specifically Christian frame.

## What Is Happening

When faith matters to you, mental health problems often carry spiritual questions too: meaning, suffering, discipline, belonging, trust, and how to stay connected without bypassing what is real. The goal isn't to flatten faith into generic wellness or turn everything into doctrine. It's to use what is true and usable.

Parenting on empty means you're out of fuel. Patience is gone. Presence is impossible. You're in survival mode. Your kids read your absence and they act out more because they feel the disconnection. You feel guilt about not being the parent you want to be.

## What To Do Now

1. Separate what needs grounding, action, and support now from the bigger spiritual questions that may take longer to work through.
2. Pick one non-negotiable parenting anchor (bedtime, one meal together, morning check-in) and protect only that.
3. Release guilt about everything else. You can't do all things when you're depleted.
4. Get help with the non-anchors (school pickup, supper prep, bedtime on your off-nights).

## What To Say

- My faith matters here, and I also need practical help in the real world.
- I'm allowed to honor what is sacred to me without pretending that's the only thing I need.
- I'm running on empty and I can only show up for [one specific thing].
- I need help with [specific tasks] so I can protect my presence for what matters most.
- When I come back to capacity, I'll be more available. Right now, this is what I can do.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using spiritual practice alone as a substitute for honest problem-solving, support, or safety planning.
- Trying to parent perfectly while running on no fuel: depletion isn't a character flaw, it's a resource problem.
- Feeling guilty about needing a break instead of recognizing that breaks make you a better parent.
- Saying yes to everything for the kids while saying no to your own basic needs.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: give yourself one 15-minute break today. Not when the kids are asleep: before that. Let something be messy.
- Before bed tonight: name one thing you did right as a parent today, even if it was small.
- Tomorrow: ask for one specific help from one specific person. Not "I need help": "can you take them for an hour?"
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits faith beyond christian life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot where the depletion is hitting your parenting hardest.

Prompts:
- What time of day is hardest with the kids? What's happening in your body at that point?
- Which parenting task drains you most: and which one still feels manageable?
- What does your kid see when you're running on empty? Be honest.
- What are you trying to do perfectly that you could do "good enough" instead?
- When's the last time you had 30 minutes completely to yourself? What happened?
- What parts of your faith or spiritual life make you feel steadier, and what parts make you feel pressure right now?

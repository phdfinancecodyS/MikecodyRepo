## What This Helps With

You're tired in a way that sleep doesn't fix. If you've been running on empty so long it feels normal, this is the guide that meets you there.
This version is written for educators dealing with emotional labor, performance pressure, burnout, care overload, and the reality of trying to keep showing up while running low.

## What Is Happening

Education work asks for constant emotional output, patience, attention, and management of other people’s needs. That can make mental health problems feel invisible because you're still performing. But being able to teach or lead a room doesn't mean you're okay.

Chronic sleep debt isn't about missing sleep one night. It's the accumulated exhaustion of weeks or months of poor sleep. Your body can't catch up with random long sleeps. Your brain is degraded. Your mood is wrecked. Recovery takes consistent repair, not heroic one-time catches.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this pattern is tied to the emotional labor of always being on, available, and responsible for the tone of the room.
2. Pick one anchor point (7am wake or 11pm sleep) and protect it ruthlessly for 2 weeks.
3. Use the same wind-down 30 minutes before bed every night (no negotiating).
4. In week 2, add one more anchor point. Don't try to fix everything at once.

## What To Say

- I can still do the job and still be struggling.
- I need support that accounts for emotional labor, not just workload.
- My sleep debt is real and catching up takes time, not one long sleep.
- I'm protecting my sleep anchors because that's how I get out of this.
- I'm going to feel worse before I feel better, and that's part of recovery.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using your ability to stay functional in front of others as proof that nothing needs attention.
- Trying a completely different sleep fix every night.
- Using screens, scrolling, or stimulation as your wind-down plan.
- Expecting one good night to repair long-term sleep strain.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: set a hard stop time for screens tonight: one hour before you want to be asleep.
- Before bed tonight: do one boring thing for 15 minutes (fold laundry, read something dull). Let your brain wind down.
- Tomorrow: move your wake-up time 15 minutes earlier and resist the snooze. Start small.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits educators life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the sleep pattern: when it breaks, what's driving it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- How long has the sleep debt been building? Weeks, months, or longer?
- What keeps you up: racing thoughts, screens, pain, noise, or habit?
- What does the exhaustion cost you during the day: mood, focus, patience, safety?
- What have you tried that helped even slightly? What made things worse?
- What time do you actually fall asleep vs. what time you get in bed?
- Which moments in teaching or school life are most linked to this issue right now?

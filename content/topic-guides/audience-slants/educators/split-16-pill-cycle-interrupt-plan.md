## What This Helps With

The pills help. Then the pills become the problem. If you're caught in that cycle, this guide meets you where you actually are.
This version is written for educators dealing with emotional labor, performance pressure, burnout, care overload, and the reality of trying to keep showing up while running low.

## What Is Happening

Education work asks for constant emotional output, patience, attention, and management of other people’s needs. That can make mental health problems feel invisible because you're still performing. But being able to teach or lead a room doesn't mean you're okay.

The pills started as a solution and became part of the problem. The cycle usually looks like this: take as prescribed, notice it doesn't work as well, take a little more, run out early, scramble for a refill, feel guilty, repeat. This isn't about being weak: it's about tolerance, dependence, and the difference between using medication to function and using it to disappear.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this pattern is tied to the emotional labor of always being on, available, and responsible for the tone of the room.
2. Take your medication exactly as prescribed today. Not more, not less.
3. Write down the last time you took more than prescribed and what was happening that day.
4. Tell one person: your prescriber, a sponsor, a trusted friend: what the pattern actually looks like right now.

## What To Say

- I can still do the job and still be struggling.
- I need support that accounts for emotional labor, not just workload.
- I think my medication use has shifted from managing symptoms to managing feelings. I need to talk about it.
- I've been taking more than prescribed and I need help figuring out what to do next.
- I'm not looking for judgment: I'm looking for a plan.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using your ability to stay functional in front of others as proof that nothing needs attention.
- Taking more than prescribed because the original dose "isn't working anymore" without telling your prescriber.
- Doctor-shopping or stockpiling without admitting what that behavior means.
- Confusing physical dependence with addiction: they're different, and both deserve attention.
- Using medication to numb instead of treat. If you're taking it to disappear, that's a different conversation than taking it to function.
- Stopping abruptly without medical supervision. Some medications require a taper: quitting cold can be dangerous.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: take your medication exactly as prescribed. Not more and not less.
- Before bed tonight: write down the last time you took more than prescribed and what was going on that day.
- Tomorrow: tell one person: your doctor, a sponsor, a trusted friend: what the pattern looks like right now.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits educators life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the cycle: when you take more than prescribed and what you're chasing.

Prompts:
- What medication are you using more than prescribed? How much more?
- What triggers the extra dose: pain, anxiety, insomnia, emotional overwhelm, or habit?
- What does the extra dose give you? (Relief, calm, numbness, sleep, escape?)
- What does the cycle look like: overuse, run out early, scramble for refill, guilt, repeat?
- Who knows about this pattern? Who would you be most afraid to tell?
- Which moments in teaching or school life are most linked to this issue right now?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Take one honest step toward breaking the pill cycle.

Prompts:
- Today I'll take my medication exactly as prescribed. The hardest moment will be ______.
- When the urge to take more hits, I'll do ______ for 10 minutes first.
- One person I'll tell about this pattern (doctor, sponsor, partner, therapist):
- The question I'll ask my doctor: "Is there a safer way to manage ______?"
- By this weekend, I'll know this worked if:

## What This Helps With

You said something. You did something. Now there's damage. This isn't about being perfect: it's about what to do after you weren't.
This version is written for educators dealing with emotional labor, performance pressure, burnout, care overload, and the reality of trying to keep showing up while running low.

## What Is Happening

Education work asks for constant emotional output, patience, attention, and management of other people’s needs. That can make mental health problems feel invisible because you're still performing. But being able to teach or lead a room doesn't mean you're okay.

Repair is the skill of reconnecting after damage. You said something hurtful, you missed your commitment, you showed up angry. Now the relationship feels broken. Most people try to move on without repair. Repair means you own the damage, you name what happened, and you commit to different.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this pattern is tied to the emotional labor of always being on, available, and responsible for the tone of the room.
2. Say specifically what you did: I was short and harsh when you came home.
3. Say why you did it: I was overwhelmed and I didn't have capacity.
4. Say what you're going to do differently: Next time I feel overwhelmed, I'm going to say that instead of being harsh.

## What To Say

- I can still do the job and still be struggling.
- I need support that accounts for emotional labor, not just workload.
- I hurt you and I need to repair that.
- Here's what I did, why I did it, and what I'm changing.
- I know this apology is words and you'll trust it when you see the change.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using your ability to stay functional in front of others as proof that nothing needs attention.
- Trying to fix the whole relationship in one conversation.
- Having the conversation when neither person is regulated enough for it.
- Skipping clear repair, boundaries, or follow-up after the hard moment.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: name the impact of what happened: not your intent, the actual impact on the other person.
- Before bed tonight: write one honest sentence you could say to them that takes ownership without defending.
- Tomorrow: say it. Not perfectly, not in a speech. Just the sentence.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits educators life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Name the damage clearly before you try to repair it.

Prompts:
- What did you do or say? Be specific: not the story, the action.
- What was the impact on the other person? Not your intent: their actual experience.
- What were you feeling underneath when it happened (overwhelmed, cornered, exhausted, triggered)?
- What's your pattern: do you explode, withdraw, dismiss, or shut down? Which one showed up?
- What would repair look like to them, not to you?
- Which moments in teaching or school life are most linked to this issue right now?

## What This Helps With

Someone you care about is in crisis. You don't need to be a therapist. You need to know what to say and what to do right now. Here it is.
This version is written for educators dealing with emotional labor, performance pressure, burnout, care overload, and the reality of trying to keep showing up while running low.

## What Is Happening

Education work asks for constant emotional output, patience, attention, and management of other people’s needs. That can make mental health problems feel invisible because you're still performing. But being able to teach or lead a room doesn't mean you're okay.

This topic sits in the safety-first lane: when pain gets loud, your first job is connection and immediate support.
People often wait for certainty before acting. In this lane, you act early and keep someone connected.
You aren't trying to be perfect. You're trying to keep the next hour safer.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this pattern is tied to the emotional labor of always being on, available, and responsible for the tone of the room.
2. Name what you're noticing with direct and calm language.
3. Ask one clear safety question and stay present for the answer.
4. Connect to crisis support now (988/741741/911 as needed) and don't leave them isolated.

## What To Say

- I can still do the job and still be struggling.
- I need support that accounts for emotional labor, not just workload.
- I care about you, and I want to ask something directly so we can keep you safe.
- Are you thinking about suicide right now?
- We can take one step together right now. I'm staying with you while we reach support.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using your ability to stay functional in front of others as proof that nothing needs attention.
- Waiting for the "right moment" to ask someone if they're thinking about suicide. There is no right moment: ask now.
- Talking around it with vague language like "You're not thinking of doing anything crazy, are you?" Be direct: "Are you thinking about killing yourself?"
- Promising to keep it secret. If someone is in danger, safety outranks secrecy every time.
- Trying to fix their pain or argue them out of it. Your job is to be present and get them connected to help: not to solve the problem in one conversation.
- Freezing because you're afraid of making it worse. Asking about suicide doesn't plant the idea. Silence is the real danger.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: identify the person you're worried about and decide: am I going to ask today or not?
- Before bed tonight: practice your opening sentence out loud. "I care about you. Can I ask you something real?"
- Tomorrow: ask. Not perfectly. Not with a script. Just ask.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits educators life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Prepare for the conversation: name what you're seeing and what's stopping you from asking.

Prompts:
- Who are you worried about? What specific behavior or change made you notice?
- What are you most afraid will happen if you ask directly about suicide?
- What are you afraid will happen if you don't ask?
- Have you had a crisis conversation before? What happened?
- What's stopping you from asking today: fear of making it worse, not knowing the words, or something else?
- Which moments in teaching or school life are most linked to this issue right now?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Plan the conversation: when, where, and what you'll say.

Prompts:
- The person I'll talk to: ______. When: ______. Where: ______.

- If they say yes, I'll say: "Thank you for telling me. Let's get you connected to support right now."

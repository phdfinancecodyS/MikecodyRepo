## What This Helps With

You know you need help. You can't make yourself ask. This guide is for the gap between knowing and doing: because that gap is where most people get stuck.
This version is written with racial stress, cultural pressure, belonging, vigilance, and the impact of systemic harm in mind.

## What Is Happening

Mental health problems can get heavier when they sit on top of racism, code-switching, cultural pressure, isolation, or the constant question of whether a space is safe. That doesn't mean everything is about race. It means race and racial stress may be increasing the load in ways that matter.

Asking for help feels like admitting defeat if you were taught to be independent or strong. But asking is actually advanced function: it means you know your limits, you trust others, and you take your needs seriously. People who ask for help recover faster and stay connected better.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask where this problem gets worse because you're carrying extra vigilance, pressure to hold it together, or lack of safe understanding.
2. Identify one specific help need (not vague rescue).
3. Pick one person who has said yes to you in the past.
4. Ask for exactly what you need, and give them an out by showing you've got a backup plan.

## What To Say

- Part of this is personal, and part of this is the environment I'm trying to function inside.
- I need support that sees the full context, not just my symptoms.
- I need one specific help with [exact thing]. Can you do that?
- If not, that's okay: I've got another option. But if you can, I would be grateful.
- This matters to me and I'm asking because I trust you.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Treating systemic stress like it's irrelevant background instead of part of the real load you're carrying.
- Hinting instead of asking. "It would be nice if someone..." isn't asking. Name what you need, name who you're asking, and say it directly.
- Asking everyone at once and getting nowhere. Pick one person. One ask. One specific need.
- Apologizing for needing help. "Sorry to bother you" frames your need as a burden. Try "I could use your help with something."
- Waiting until you're in crisis to ask. By then, the ask is bigger and scarier. Practice small asks now.
- Interpreting a "no" as proof you shouldn't have asked. A no means that person can't right now. It doesn't mean you don't deserve help.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: name the one thing you need help with most. Be specific.
- Before bed tonight: pick one person who's said yes to you before. They're your ask.
- Tomorrow: ask. Say exactly what you need and give them an out if they can't. Clarity is the antidote to awkwardness.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits bipoc / racial trauma life.

## Worksheet 1: Help-Asking Barrier Inventory

Goal: Name what's actually stopping you from asking so you can work around it.

Prompts:
- What's the specific thing you need help with right now? Be concrete: not "I need support" but "I need someone to watch the kids Tuesday so I can go to my appointment."
- What's the story in your head about what it means to ask? ("I should handle this myself," "They'll think less of me," "I'll owe them.")
- When's the last time someone asked YOU for help? How did you feel about it? (Probably not what you fear they'll feel.)
- Who are three people who have said yes to you before? What did they help with? How did it go?
- What's the smallest ask you could make today that would take 5% of the weight off?
- Where do you feel most pressure to stay composed, guarded, or unreadable?

## Worksheet 2: Ask Script Builder

Goal: Write your exact ask so you can deliver it without overthinking.

Prompts:
- The person I'm going to ask is: ___. I chose them because: ___.
- My ask will sound like: "I need help with ___. Can you ___ by ___? If you can't, no pressure: I've got a backup plan."
- I'll ask them by: ___ (in person, text, call). The best time is: ___.
- If they say no, I'll say: "No problem. Thanks for being honest." Then I'll ask: ___ as my backup.
- After they help, I'll follow up with: ___ (thank you text, returned favor, acknowledgment). Closing the loop matters.

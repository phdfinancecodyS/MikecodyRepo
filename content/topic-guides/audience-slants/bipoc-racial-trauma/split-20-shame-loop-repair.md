## What This Helps With

Shame did something → felt terrible → hid from it → shame got louder. If that cycle sounds familiar, here's how to break out of it.
This version is written with racial stress, cultural pressure, belonging, vigilance, and the impact of systemic harm in mind.

## What Is Happening

Mental health problems can get heavier when they sit on top of racism, code-switching, cultural pressure, isolation, or the constant question of whether a space is safe. That doesn't mean everything is about race. It means race and racial stress may be increasing the load in ways that matter.

Shame is a loop, not a fact. Here's how it runs: you did something (or believe you did), you feel terrible about it, the feeling is so unbearable that you hide, the hiding creates more shame, and the loop tightens. Shame tells you "I am bad": not "I did something bad." That distinction is the whole game.

Shame thrives in secrecy. The less you talk about it, the louder it gets. Most shame loops aren't running on fresh events: they're replaying things from years ago with the volume cranked.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask where this problem gets worse because you're carrying extra vigilance, pressure to hold it together, or lack of safe understanding.
2. Say the shame thought out loud: to yourself, to the wall, to paper. Get it outside your head.
3. Separate the behavior from the identity: "I did ______" is different from "I am ______."
4. Tell one safe person one piece of what you're carrying. Shame can't survive being spoken.

## What To Say

- Part of this is personal, and part of this is the environment I'm trying to function inside.
- I need support that sees the full context, not just my symptoms.
- I've been carrying something that makes me feel like a terrible person. Can I tell you about it?
- I did something I'm ashamed of and the shame is eating me alive.
- I need you to listen, not fix it. I just need someone to know.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Treating systemic stress like it's irrelevant background instead of part of the real load you're carrying.
- Confessing to the wrong person and getting shamed deeper instead of supported.
- Using self-punishment as atonement: suffering doesn't repair anything.
- Believing that if people "really knew" you, they'd leave: shame lies about that constantly.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: say the shame thought out loud. Hear how it sounds outside your head.
- Before bed tonight: write down one thing you're proud of from this week. Even if it feels small.
- Tomorrow: tell one person one thing you've been ashamed about. Shame loses power when it's shared.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits bipoc / racial trauma life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Catch the shame loop: what it replays, what it says about you, and what it costs.

Prompts:
- What's the shame loop replaying right now? Write the exact thought.
- Is this about something you actually did, or something shame convinced you that you are?
- When did this shame start? Is it recent or has it been running for years?
- What do you do when the shame gets loud: hide, overfunction, self-punish, use, shut down?
- If you told the right person this story, what's the worst that could happen? What's the best?
- Where do you feel most pressure to stay composed, guarded, or unreadable?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Break the loop with one honest disclosure and one identity correction.

Prompts:
- The shame thought I'll say out loud (to myself, on paper, to someone safe): "______."
- The correction: "I did ______. That doesn't make me a ______."

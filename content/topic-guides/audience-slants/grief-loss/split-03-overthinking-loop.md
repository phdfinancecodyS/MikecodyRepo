## What This Helps With

The same thought, on repeat, for hours. You can't stop analyzing, replaying, or planning for things that haven't happened. Here's how to break the loop.
This version is written for people carrying grief, fresh loss, old loss that is still active, or the kind of missing that changes how every other problem lands.

## What Is Happening

Grief changes the shape of everything. It lowers capacity, intensifies loneliness, scrambles concentration, and can make normal stress feel enormous. It also doesn't move in a straight line. The goal isn't to rush you past grief. It's to help you function while grief is still part of the room.

Your brain is stuck in rumination: replaying, analyzing, rehearsing, catastrophizing. It feels like problem-solving, but nothing is getting solved. The loop runs because your nervous system is activated and your brain is trying to regain control by thinking harder. But thinking harder about an emotional problem is like pressing the gas while the car is in park. You burn fuel and go nowhere.

The loop usually intensifies at night, during downtime, or after conflict. The brain hates unresolved threat: so it keeps circling the same runway.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this problem is being amplified by grief load, anniversaries, reminders, or the effort of trying to function while carrying absence.
2. Name the loop out loud: "I'm overthinking _______ again." Naming it creates distance.
3. Set a 5-minute worry timer. Think as hard as you want. When it rings, move your body.
4. Write the loop down word for word. On paper, it shrinks. In your head, it multiplies.

## What To Say

- This problem isn't happening separate from grief. Grief is part of the load right now.
- I don't need to be over this to take a useful next step.
- I've been in my head about this for hours and it's not getting clearer.
- I need to say this out loud to someone instead of running it on repeat.
- The loop is telling me _______, but I don't have to keep solving it tonight.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Judging yourself for reduced capacity without accounting for the real weight of loss.
- Trying to "think your way out" of a feeling: the loop IS the problem, not the solution.
- Asking everyone for reassurance, which resets the loop instead of breaking it.
- Treating the loop like truth instead of recognizing it as an anxious pattern.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: set a timer for 5 minutes. Think about the thing on loop as hard as you can. When the timer goes off, move your body.
- Before bed tonight: write the thought loop down word for word. Seeing it on paper shrinks it.
- Tomorrow: when the loop starts, name it: "there's the loop": and redirect to one physical task.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits grief / loss life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Catch the loop: what it replays, when it starts, and what it steals from you.

Prompts:
- What's the thought on repeat right now? Write the exact sentence your brain keeps running.
- When did the loop start today? What triggered it: a conversation, a memory, uncertainty, silence?
- Does the loop lean toward the past (replaying) or the future (catastrophizing)?
- How many hours have you spent in the loop today? What didn't get done because of it?
- What would happen if you just let the thought be wrong and moved on?
- How is grief changing your energy, concentration, relationships, or stress tolerance right now?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Break the loop with one externalization and one physical redirect.

Prompts:
- I'll write the loop thought down right now: "______". There. It's out of my head.
- My loop-interrupt action when I catch it (move my body, call someone, set a timer): ______.
- The sentence I'll say: "That's the loop. I've already thought about this for ______ hours. Time to stop."
- One decision I'll make or one thing I'll accept so the loop loses its fuel:
- By tomorrow, I'll know this worked if:

## What This Helps With

You can't finish a thought, hold a plan, or follow through on anything. Your brain feels like it's in 47 tabs. Here's how to close a few.
This version is written for people carrying grief, fresh loss, old loss that is still active, or the kind of missing that changes how every other problem lands.

## What Is Happening

Grief changes the shape of everything. It lowers capacity, intensifies loneliness, scrambles concentration, and can make normal stress feel enormous. It also doesn't move in a straight line. The goal isn't to rush you past grief. It's to help you function while grief is still part of the room.

Your brain isn't broken: it's flooded. Too many inputs, too many open loops, too much unresolved stress. The executive function center (prefrontal cortex) shuts down when it's overloaded, and what's left is scattered attention, half-finished tasks, and the frustrating feeling that you can't hold onto anything.

Stress, sleep debt, and unprocessed emotion all make this worse. Your brain is trying to track 47 things and completing zero of them.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this problem is being amplified by grief load, anniversaries, reminders, or the effort of trying to function while carrying absence.
2. Close everything. Literally: tabs, apps, notifications. Reduce input to one channel.
3. Pick one task. Set a timer for 20 minutes. Do only that.
4. Write tomorrow's top three priorities tonight so your brain can stop holding them.

## What To Say

- This problem isn't happening separate from grief. Grief is part of the load right now.
- I don't need to be over this to take a useful next step.
- I can't track anything right now and I need to simplify before I try to be useful.
- My brain is scattered. I'm going to focus on one thing and come back to the rest.
- I'm not blowing you off: I literally can't hold it all right now.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Judging yourself for reduced capacity without accounting for the real weight of loss.
- Trying to multitask your way out of scatter: multitasking IS the scatter.
- Adding more systems, apps, or lists when the real problem is overload.
- Beating yourself up for not being able to focus, which makes the scatter worse.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: close every tab, app, and notification that isn't the one thing you need to do next.
- Before bed tonight: write down the three most important things for tomorrow. Nothing else makes the list.
- Tomorrow: do the first thing on the list before you check your phone.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits grief / loss life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the scatter: what's overloading your brain and what's getting dropped.

Prompts:
- How many things are you trying to track right now? List them.
- What's getting dropped: tasks, relationships, self-care, basics?
- When is the scatter worst: morning, afternoon, after conflict, after scrolling?
- Is this new or has your brain always been like this? What's different now?
- What helps when you do manage to focus: quiet, deadlines, movement, someone else's structure?
- How is grief changing your energy, concentration, relationships, or stress tolerance right now?

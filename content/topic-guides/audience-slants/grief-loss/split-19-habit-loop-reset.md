## What This Helps With

You keep doing the thing you said you'd stop. Not because you're weak: because the loop is wired. Here's how to rewire it, one cycle at a time.
This version is written for people carrying grief, fresh loss, old loss that is still active, or the kind of missing that changes how every other problem lands.

## What Is Happening

Grief changes the shape of everything. It lowers capacity, intensifies loneliness, scrambles concentration, and can make normal stress feel enormous. It also doesn't move in a straight line. The goal isn't to rush you past grief. It's to help you function while grief is still part of the room.

Habit loops feel automatic because cue, relief, and repeat happen fast.
The break point is usually right after the trigger, before the old routine starts.
Replacement routines work better than pure willpower.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this problem is being amplified by grief load, anniversaries, reminders, or the effort of trying to function while carrying absence.
2. Name the cue and where it usually hits.
3. Insert one replacement action for 5-10 minutes.
4. Track one win and one miss without shame.

## What To Say

- This problem isn't happening separate from grief. Grief is part of the load right now.
- I don't need to be over this to take a useful next step.
- I can feel the old loop starting, so I'm switching routines now.
- I'm choosing a smaller, safer action for the next ten minutes.
- Progress is reps, not perfection.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Judging yourself for reduced capacity without accounting for the real weight of loss.
- Trying to break a habit without understanding what it's doing for you. Every loop serves a function: boredom relief, anxiety management, numbness. Find the function first.
- Relying on willpower to override a cue-routine-reward pattern that's been reinforced for months or years.
- Replacing one bad habit with another one just as harmful but easier to hide.
- Making the plan too complicated. One cue, one new response, one reward: that's the whole thing.
- Beating yourself up after a slip and using shame as a reason to quit trying. Slips are data, not proof of failure.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: identify the cue that starts the loop. What happens right before the habit kicks in?
- Before bed tonight: write down: cue → routine → reward. Once you see the loop, you can break it.
- Tomorrow: when the cue hits, replace the routine with one different action. Same cue, new response.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits grief / loss life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Dissect the loop: identify the cue, the routine, and the reward your brain is chasing.

Prompts:
- What's the habit? Name it plainly, no softening.
- What's the cue: a time, place, emotion, person, or preceding action that starts it?
- What reward does the habit deliver? (relief, numbness, stimulation, comfort, control?)
- What have you tried to quit or change it? What happened?
- What does the habit cost you: time, health, relationships, money, self-respect?
- How is grief changing your energy, concentration, relationships, or stress tolerance right now?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Build one replacement routine for one trigger.

Prompts:
- The cue I'll target first: ______. My replacement action for 10 minutes: ______.
- What I'll track this week (number of times the cue hits, how many times I used the replacement):
- My zero-shame rule: if I slip, I'll ______ instead of calling myself a failure.
- One person I'll tell about the loop so it's not just in my head:
- By this weekend, I'll know this worked if:

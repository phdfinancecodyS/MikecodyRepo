## What This Helps With

You're doomscrolling and you know it. You can't stop. The phone is the escape and the problem at the same time. Here's the interrupt.
This version is written with neurodivergent life in mind, including overwhelm, sensory load, executive function strain, rejection sensitivity, masking, and burnout.

## What Is Happening

A lot of mental health advice quietly assumes a nervous system and executive function style that you may not have. If you're neurodivergent, the same problem can hit harder because of sensory overload, task switching, time blindness, social effort, or the cost of masking. That doesn't mean you're doing it wrong. It means the plan has to fit your brain.

Habit loops feel automatic because cue, relief, and repeat happen fast.
The break point is usually right after the trigger, before the old routine starts.
Replacement routines work better than pure willpower.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being intensified by sensory overload, masking, executive friction, or the cost of trying to function in a way that doesn't fit you.
2. Name the cue and where it usually hits.
3. Insert one replacement action for 5-10 minutes.
4. Track one win and one miss without shame.

## What To Say

- I need a plan that fits how my brain actually works, not advice built for someone else’s system.
- Some of this isn't laziness or avoidance. It's executive and sensory load.
- I can feel the old loop starting, so I'm switching routines now.
- I'm choosing a smaller, safer action for the next ten minutes.
- Progress is reps, not perfection.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using shame to force yourself into systems, routines, or expectations that are already failing your nervous system.
- Deleting all your apps and reinstalling them 48 hours later. That's a binge-purge cycle, not a strategy.
- Blaming the phone instead of understanding what you're using it to avoid. The phone isn't the problem: it's the most convenient escape route.
- Setting limits you know you'll override. If Screen Time nags and you tap "Ignore," the limit isn't working: change the approach.
- Replacing scrolling with another passive numbing behavior (binge-watching, eating, shopping). The goal is a real break, not a lateral move.
- Going all-or-nothing ("digital detox weekend!") instead of building a sustainable daily boundary.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: put your phone in another room for 30 minutes. Set a timer if you need to.
- Before bed tonight: turn off non-essential notifications. Not all of them: just the ones that pull you back in.
- Tomorrow: before you pick up your phone, do one thing first. Brush your teeth, make coffee, step outside. Break the first-touch habit.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits neurodivergent life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the scroll loop: when you pick up the phone, what you're avoiding, and how long you lose.

Prompts:
- When do you reach for the phone: boredom, anxiety, avoidance, habit, or loneliness?
- How long is a typical scroll session? How long does it feel like?
- What are you usually feeling before you pick it up? What are you feeling after?
- What are you not doing because you're scrolling: sleeping, connecting, working, living?
- If you couldn't scroll for 24 hours, what feeling would you have to sit with?
- What parts of this problem are really about overload, task friction, masking, or recovery time?

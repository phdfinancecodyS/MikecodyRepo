## What This Helps With

Your teammate is struggling and you want to say something but you don't know how. This gives you the words: not clinical ones, real ones.
This version is written with neurodivergent life in mind, including overwhelm, sensory load, executive function strain, rejection sensitivity, masking, and burnout.

## What Is Happening

A lot of mental health advice quietly assumes a nervous system and executive function style that you may not have. If you're neurodivergent, the same problem can hit harder because of sensory overload, task switching, time blindness, social effort, or the cost of masking. That doesn't mean you're doing it wrong. It means the plan has to fit your brain.

Peer support means you aren't a therapist and you aren't responsible for fixing them. Your job is to listen, name what you see, and connect them to actual help. Many peer supporters burn out because they absorb the emotional weight of the problem.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being intensified by sensory overload, masking, executive friction, or the cost of trying to function in a way that doesn't fit you.
2. Listen to what they say without trying to solve it in one conversation.
3. Name back what you heard so they feel seen.
4. Name one concrete resource and leave it with them rather than trying to make them use it.

## What To Say

- I need a plan that fits how my brain actually works, not advice built for someone else’s system.
- Some of this isn't laziness or avoidance. It's executive and sensory load.
- I hear you. That sounds really hard.
- Here's what I see: [what they're up against]. And here's where you get actual professional help.
- I'm here to check in with you, and I'm also not the person who fixes this.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using shame to force yourself into systems, routines, or expectations that are already failing your nervous system.
- Trying to be their therapist. You're a teammate, not a clinician. Your job is to notice, name it, and connect them to help.
- Opening with "Are you okay?": it's too easy to answer "I'm fine." Ask something specific: "I noticed you've been quiet since that call."
- Ambushing them in front of people. Find a private moment. Hallway, parking lot, text. Not the break room.
- Giving advice instead of listening. The most powerful thing you can say is "I see you": not "Here's what you should do."
- Checking in once and then dropping it if they say they're fine. Follow up. The first ask rarely gets honesty.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: think about the teammate you're worried about. Decide if you're going to approach them today.
- Before bed tonight: practice your opener: "I've noticed something and I want to check on you. Can we talk?"
- Tomorrow: find a private moment and ask. You're not their therapist: you're their teammate showing up.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits neurodivergent life.

## Worksheet 1: Teammate Check-In Planner

Goal: Get specific about who you're worried about and what you've noticed so the conversation actually lands.

Prompts:
- Who's the teammate you're concerned about? What specifically have you noticed? (behavior changes, withdrawal, irritability, performance dips, dark humor?)
- How long has this been going on? Was there a specific incident that triggered the change?
- What's your relationship with this person like? How much trust is there? What's the best setting to talk to them?
- What's held you back from saying something so far? (Afraid of overstepping, not sure what to say, worried they'll be angry?)
- What's the worst that happens if you say something? What's the worst that happens if you don't?
- What parts of this problem are really about overload, task friction, masking, or recovery time?

## Worksheet 2: Peer Support Conversation Script Builder

Goal: Build your exact opening and follow-up plan so you don't freeze in the moment.

Prompts:
- My opening line will be: "I've noticed ___ and I wanted to check on you. Can we talk for a few minutes?"
- If they say "I'm fine": I'll follow up with: "Okay. I'm here if that changes. I'm going to check back with you on ___."
- If they open up: I'll listen and then say: "Thank you for telling me. What do you need right now: someone to listen, or help finding resources?"
- The resource I'll have ready to share: ___ (EAP number, peer support program, 988, therapist name).
- I'll follow up in ___ days regardless of how the first conversation goes. My follow-up will sound like: "Hey, just checking in. Still here."

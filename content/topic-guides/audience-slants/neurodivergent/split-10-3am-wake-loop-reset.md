## What This Helps With

You wake up at 3am every night and your brain immediately goes to work. Here's how to break the cycle without fighting it.
This version is written with neurodivergent life in mind, including overwhelm, sensory load, executive function strain, rejection sensitivity, masking, and burnout.

## What Is Happening

A lot of mental health advice quietly assumes a nervous system and executive function style that you may not have. If you're neurodivergent, the same problem can hit harder because of sensory overload, task switching, time blindness, social effort, or the cost of masking. That doesn't mean you're doing it wrong. It means the plan has to fit your brain.

Sleep disruption turns up reactivity, worry, and decision fatigue fast.
Most people chase a perfect night. The better move is a repeatable wind-down pattern.
Small consistency beats occasional heroic fixes.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being intensified by sensory overload, masking, executive friction, or the cost of trying to function in a way that doesn't fit you.
2. Pick one fixed anchor time for wind-down or wake-up.
3. Run a 20-minute downshift routine without screens.
4. Write tomorrow's first task so your brain has a landing point.

## What To Say

- I need a plan that fits how my brain actually works, not advice built for someone else’s system.
- Some of this isn't laziness or avoidance. It's executive and sensory load.
- I'm running on low sleep and I need to simplify tonight.
- I'm doing my reset routine now so tomorrow is easier.
- I'm not fixing everything tonight; I'm building one better night.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using shame to force yourself into systems, routines, or expectations that are already failing your nervous system.
- Staying in bed and staring at the ceiling trying to will yourself back to sleep. That trains your brain to associate bed with frustration.
- Checking your phone "just to see the time" and then scrolling for 45 minutes. Put the phone across the room or face-down.
- Trying to solve the thought that woke you up instead of writing it down and shelving it until morning.
- Changing your entire sleep routine every night. Consistency matters more than perfection: pick one anchor and hold it.
- Ignoring that the 3am wake might be tied to alcohol, caffeine, blood sugar, or anxiety patterns. Track what happened that day before assuming it's random.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: set a notepad by the bed. When you wake at 3am, write the thought down instead of solving it.
- Before bed tonight: avoid screens after 10pm and do one boring thing for 20 minutes.
- Tomorrow: review the 3am notes. Handle the real ones during the day. Let the rest go.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits neurodivergent life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the 3am pattern: what wakes you and what your brain does next.

Prompts:
- What time do you wake up? Is it the same time every night?
- What's the first thought when your eyes open? Write it exactly.
- Does the waking feel physical (pain, heart racing, temperature) or mental (thoughts, dread, planning)?
- What do you reach for: phone, food, substances, nothing? What happens after?
- How many nights per week does this happen? What's it costing you?
- What parts of this problem are really about overload, task friction, masking, or recovery time?

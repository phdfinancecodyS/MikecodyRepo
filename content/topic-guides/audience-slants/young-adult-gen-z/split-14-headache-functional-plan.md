## What This Helps With

When the headache won't quit and you still have to function: this isn't medical advice. It's a plan for getting through the day.
This version is written for young adult life, where identity, money, work, relationships, social comparison, and figuring out who you are all collide at once.

## What Is Happening

Young adult problems often get minimized as a phase, but the load is real. You may be building identity, trying to stay afloat financially, dealing with unstable work, comparing yourself online, and making adult decisions without much margin. That changes how stress, shame, and mental health hit.

Chronic or recurring headaches steal your function in slow motion. The pain sits there: sometimes dull, sometimes crushing: and you're expected to keep going. Your brain gets stuck between powering through and shutting down. Most people wait too long to intervene, treat every headache the same way, and lose track of the patterns that could actually help them manage it.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask where this issue is tied to uncertainty about identity, stability, belonging, or comparison pressure rather than just personal weakness.
2. Intervene early: hydrate, lower the lights, take prescribed medication now: not after the headache hits a 7.
3. Identify today's likely trigger: sleep, dehydration, stress, screens, jaw clenching, skipped meals.
4. If you can't stop working, reduce inputs: close extra tabs, lower the volume, take 5-minute breaks every 30 minutes.

## What To Say

- I'm not behind because I'm struggling. I'm carrying a lot while building a life.
- I need help that works in real life, not advice from someone pretending this stage is simple.
- I have a headache building and I need to intervene now before it gets worse.
- I can keep going but I need reduced stimulation for the next hour.
- This has been a pattern. I need to track it and probably talk to my doctor.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Treating social comparison and instability like they're minor background issues when they're actively driving stress.
- Ignoring headaches as "just stress" until they become debilitating. Track the pattern before it tracks you.
- Taking OTC painkillers daily without realizing you're building a medication overuse headache. More than 15 days a month is the threshold.
- Waiting until the headache is a 7/10 before intervening. Early action: hydration, dark room, medication: works better than rescue at peak.
- Skipping meals, sleep, and water and then wondering why your head is splitting. Basics aren't optional.
- Blaming yourself for having headaches. This is a physiological pattern, not a character flaw.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: lower the lights, drink a full glass of water, and take any prescribed medication. Don't power through: manage.
- Before bed tonight: eat something and set a consistent sleep time.
- Tomorrow: if it's still there, track what's different today: hydration, sleep, stress, caffeine: and start building the pattern.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits young adults / gen z life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Track the headache pattern: frequency, triggers, and what you're doing (or not doing) about it.

Prompts:
- How often are the headaches hitting? Daily, weekly, random?
- What's the pattern: tension (band around head), migraine (one side, light sensitivity), or pressure?
- What triggers them: stress, screen time, sleep, dehydration, jaw clenching, skipped meals?
- When a headache hits, what do you do? Power through, hide, medicate, nothing?
- What would you need to change to cut the frequency in half? Be honest.
- Where do comparison, instability, or identity pressure show up most strongly in this issue?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Build a headache management plan so you stop losing days.

Prompts:
- My headache first-aid kit (medication, ice, dark room, caffeine, hydration):
- One trigger I'll address this week (water intake, sleep schedule, screen breaks, jaw tension):
- The sentence I'll say at work or home when a headache hits: "I need to ______ for ______ minutes."
- When I should see a doctor (frequency ______, severity ______, new symptoms ______):
- By this weekend, I'll know this worked if:

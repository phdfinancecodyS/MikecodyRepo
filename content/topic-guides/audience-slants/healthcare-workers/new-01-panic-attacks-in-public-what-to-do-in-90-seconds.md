## What This Helps With

Your chest is tight, you can't breathe, and you're in a grocery store. This is the 90-second plan. Not later. Right now.
This version is written for healthcare workers who carry clinical pressure, moral stress, compassion fatigue, shift strain, and the weird tension of caring for everybody while neglecting yourself.

## What Is Happening

Healthcare work teaches you to override your own body, emotions, and limits in order to care for other people. That can become a problem fast. You may be good at triaging everyone else while ignoring what is happening in you until it spills over.

Panic in public feels catastrophic because you think everyone is watching and something is medically wrong with you. Neither is true. Your nervous system got a false alarm signal and now it's running a full threat response. You've 90 seconds to interrupt the spiral before it feeds itself.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether you're applying professional competence to everyone except yourself, and what it's costing you.
2. Move to a less-crowded spot if you can, or stay and plant your feet.
3. Do one grounding move: feel your feet on the ground, name five things you see, or do box breathing for 30 seconds.
4. Drink water and text one safe person that you're handling this now.

## What To Say

- I know how to care for people. I'm not doing a good job of applying that same honesty to myself right now.
- This isn't just stress. It's accumulation, moral strain, and not enough recovery.
- My nervous system thinks I'm in danger but I'm not. This will pass.
- I'm using my reset right now and I'm safe.
- I'm in the middle of this and I'll be okay in a few minutes.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using clinical knowledge to explain your own problem while still avoiding the practical steps you would recommend to someone else.
- Trying to reason your way out mid-episode. Logic is offline: your body needs a physical reset first.
- Going to the ER every time but never following up with someone who treats panic specifically.
- Avoiding the places where panic happened. Avoidance shrinks your world and strengthens the cycle.
- Telling yourself to "just calm down." That phrase has never once stopped a panic attack.
- Breathing too fast or too deep during an episode. Slow exhale through pursed lips: out longer than in.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: practice the 90-second plan: feet flat, cold object in your hand, breathe in for 4 out for 6: in a safe space so it's ready when you need it.
- Before bed tonight: write a one-sentence card for your wallet: "This is a panic attack. It will pass. Feet, cold, breathe."
- Tomorrow: go somewhere mildly uncomfortable with an exit plan. Practice being in it and leaving when you need to.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits healthcare workers life.

## Worksheet 1: Panic Pattern Mapper

Goal: Map exactly what happens before, during, and after public panic so you can interrupt the cycle earlier.

Prompts:
- Describe your last public panic episode. Where were you? What were you doing? How crowded was it?
- What was the very first signal before the full wave hit? (chest tightness, tingling hands, hot flash, tunnel vision, racing thoughts?)
- How long did the episode last? What did you do during it? What finally helped it pass?
- List three places or situations you now avoid because of panic. How has that avoidance changed your daily life?
- What's the story you tell yourself when panic hits in public? ("Everyone sees me," "I'm dying," "I can't escape.") Write the exact thought.
- What parts of clinical work or patient care are most connected to this pattern lately?

## Worksheet 2: 90-Second Panic Plan Builder

Goal: Build a concrete plan you can execute on autopilot when the next public episode starts.

Prompts:
- My first panic signal is usually: ___. When I notice that signal, I'll immediately do: ___ (feet flat, cold object, slow exhale 4-7-8).
- The one sentence I'll say to myself mid-episode: "This is adrenaline. It peaks in 90 seconds. I've survived every one."
- One person who knows about my panic and I can text a code word to: ___. The code word is: ___.
- One public place I've been avoiding that I'll re-visit this week with an exit plan: ___. My exit plan is: ___.
- After the episode passes, I'll recover by doing this instead of going straight home: ___ (walk one block, get water, sit on a bench for five minutes).

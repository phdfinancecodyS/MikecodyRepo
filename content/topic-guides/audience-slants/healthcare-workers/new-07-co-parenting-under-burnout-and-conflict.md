## What This Helps With

You're both exhausted, you disagree on everything, and the kids are watching. Here's how to co-parent when you can barely co-exist.
This version is written for healthcare workers who carry clinical pressure, moral stress, compassion fatigue, shift strain, and the weird tension of caring for everybody while neglecting yourself.

## What Is Happening

Healthcare work teaches you to override your own body, emotions, and limits in order to care for other people. That can become a problem fast. You may be good at triaging everyone else while ignoring what is happening in you until it spills over.

Co-parenting while burned out or in conflict usually means you're managing two crises at once: your own depletion and the conflict with the other parent. Kids read both. Your job isn't to fix the co-parent or the conflict instantly: it's to keep yourself functioning and give kids one stable parent.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether you're applying professional competence to everyone except yourself, and what it's costing you.
2. Pick one parenting commitment you'll protect no matter what (bedtime, one meal, one check-in).
3. Take that off the table from conflict negotiations.
4. Keep co-parent communication to logistics only, not emotions.

## What To Say

- I know how to care for people. I'm not doing a good job of applying that same honesty to myself right now.
- This isn't just stress. It's accumulation, moral strain, and not enough recovery.
- I'm running on empty right now and I need to protect my capacity with the kids.
- On this topic, here's what I'm doing regardless. We can talk about the rest when I'm more stable.
- Let's stick to [logistics topic] and I'll keep you in the loop.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using clinical knowledge to explain your own problem while still avoiding the practical steps you would recommend to someone else.
- Using the kids as messengers or leverage. Even when it feels justified, the kids absorb every bit of it.
- Trying to co-parent well when you're deep in burnout. You can't give the kids your best when you haven't slept or eaten.
- Re-litigating the relationship during a co-parenting conversation. Separate the channels: kid stuff in one lane, old wounds in another.
- Expecting your co-parent to change their whole approach because you've changed yours. Manage your side of the street.
- Saying "fine" when it isn't. Resentment that doesn't get spoken leaks sideways: onto the kids, onto your next relationship.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: decide one thing you and your co-parent can agree on this week. Just one.
- Before bed tonight: write down what the kids need most from both of you right now: not from the best version, the actual version.
- Tomorrow: have one conversation with your co-parent about the kids that stays on the kids. Not the past. The kids.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits healthcare workers life.

## Worksheet 1: Co-Parenting Pressure Point Map

Goal: Identify the specific friction points so you can fix the pattern, not just react to each fight.

Prompts:
- What's the single biggest co-parenting conflict right now? Describe the last time it happened.
- When conflict starts, what happens in your body? (jaw clenches, chest tightens, shut down, rage?) What do you do next?
- What does your co-parent do that triggers you most? Be honest: not what they should do, what actually sets you off.
- How are the kids reacting to the tension? What are you noticing in their behavior that worries you?
- What's one thing you and your co-parent actually agree on about the kids? Start there.
- What parts of clinical work or patient care are most connected to this pattern lately?

## Worksheet 2: Co-Parenting Under Burnout: One-Week Plan

Goal: Build one week of functional co-parenting when you're running on empty.

Prompts:
- The one co-parenting conversation I need to have this week is about: ___. I'll keep it to: ___ minutes.
- Before that conversation, I'll do this to regulate first: ___ (walk, breathe, call a friend, eat something).
- The sentence I'll use if the conversation starts going sideways: "I want to stay on the kids. Can we come back to that?"
- One thing I'll do for myself this week so I have something left for the kids: ___.
- If things escalate beyond what I can handle, my plan is: ___ (end the conversation, text instead of call, involve a mediator).

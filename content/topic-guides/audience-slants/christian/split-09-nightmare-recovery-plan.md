## What This Helps With

You woke up shaking, sweating, heart pounding: and now you're afraid to go back to sleep. This is the plan for right now, tonight.
This version is written for people who want practical mental health help that can sit alongside Christian faith, prayer, and church life without turning into a sermon.

## What Is Happening

When your faith matters to you, pain often hits two places at once: your nervous system and your beliefs about God, hope, suffering, and support. The goal here isn't to over-spiritualize the problem or ignore faith. It's to tell the truth about what's happening and use support that fits your real life.

Nightmares after trauma aren't just bad dreams: they're your brain replaying threat at full volume while your body acts like it's real. Your heart races, cortisol surges, and you wake up flooded with the same fear as the original event. Then you dread going back to sleep, and the sleep deprivation makes everything worse the next day.

## What To Do Now

1. Name what's a mental health problem, what's a faith question, and where the two are overlapping so you don't blur them into one knot.
2. When you wake from one: lights on, feet on the floor, cold water on your face. Orient to the room: name what you see.
3. Don't try to analyze the nightmare right now. Your job is to calm your body first.
4. If you're afraid to go back to sleep, move to a different spot (couch, chair) and do something low-key for 20 minutes.

## What To Say

- I'm struggling, and I don't need a polished answer right now. I need honest support.
- Prayer can be part of this, and I also need practical help.
- I had a nightmare and my body is still reacting. I'm safe and I'm grounding myself.
- I'm afraid to go back to sleep and that's normal after what I just experienced.
- I need to talk to someone about what's happening at night: it's getting in the way.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using spiritual language to avoid the actual problem, the actual action step, or the actual support you need.
- Fighting the nightmare mid-dream or trying to "control" it before you've processed what's behind it.
- Avoiding sleep entirely because the nightmares are so bad. Sleep deprivation makes the nightmares worse, not better.
- Using alcohol or medication to knock yourself out. That suppresses REM but doesn't stop the pattern.
- Replaying the nightmare in vivid detail right after waking and flooding yourself all over again. Orient first, process later.
- Assuming nightmares will stop on their own. If they've been going more than a few weeks, talk to someone who treats trauma-related sleep disruption.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: if you just woke up from one: lights on, feet on the floor, cold water on your face. Orient to the room.
- Before bed tonight: write down one safe image you can redirect to when you wake from a nightmare. Practice visualizing it.
- Tomorrow: tell one person what's happening at night. Nightmares lose power when they're spoken.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits christian life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Track the nightmare pattern: what's happening at night and what it's doing to your days.

Prompts:
- Describe the most frequent nightmare. What happens? How does it end?
- Does the nightmare replay something real, or is it a new scenario built from old feelings?
- What does your body do when you wake up? (racing heart, sweating, frozen, crying, can't move)
- How long does it take you to come back to the present? What helps?
- What are you doing to avoid sleep because of the nightmares?
- How does faith language help this problem, and where does it make you hide instead of tell the truth?

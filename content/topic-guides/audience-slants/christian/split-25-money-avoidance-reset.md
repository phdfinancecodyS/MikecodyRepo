## What This Helps With

You're avoiding the bank account, the bills, the conversation. Money stress doesn't get better by not looking. Here's how to start looking without spiraling.
This version is written for people who want practical mental health help that can sit alongside Christian faith, prayer, and church life without turning into a sermon.

## What Is Happening

When your faith matters to you, pain often hits two places at once: your nervous system and your beliefs about God, hope, suffering, and support. The goal here isn't to over-spiritualize the problem or ignore faith. It's to tell the truth about what's happening and use support that fits your real life.

Money avoidance is a stress response, not a personality flaw. When finances feel overwhelming, your brain treats the bank balance like a threat: so you don't look. Bills pile up, late fees compound, and the gap between what you owe and what you know grows wider. The anxiety that made you avoid looking is now bigger because you avoided looking. Breaking the cycle starts with one honest glance.

## What To Do Now

1. Name what's a mental health problem, what's a faith question, and where the two are overlapping so you don't blur them into one knot.
2. Open your bank account or one bill. Just look. You don't have to fix anything yet.
3. Write down the number you're most afraid to see and the number that's actually there.
4. Pick one action: pay one bill, make one call, or set up one auto-payment. One. Not all.

## What To Say

- I'm struggling, and I don't need a polished answer right now. I need honest support.
- Prayer can be part of this, and I also need practical help.
- I've been avoiding my finances and I need to start looking at the real numbers.
- I'm behind on money stuff and I'm taking one step today instead of doing nothing.
- I need help with this: can you sit with me while I open this up?

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using spiritual language to avoid the actual problem, the actual action step, or the actual support you need.
- Avoiding looking at your bank account because the number might confirm what you already fear. Not looking doesn't slow the problem: it accelerates it.
- Making a detailed budget during a panic and abandoning it by day three. Start with one number: what goes out this week.
- Using online shopping as a stress reliever while telling yourself the financial stress is about income. Spending is part of the equation.
- Refusing to talk to anyone about money because it feels shameful. Money shame thrives in silence.
- Treating every financial problem as equally urgent and doing nothing because you're overwhelmed. Pick one bill. One call. One action.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: open your bank account or your bills. Just look. You don't have to fix anything yet.
- Before bed tonight: write down the number you're most afraid to see and the number that's actually there.
- Tomorrow: make one call, send one email, or pay one bill. One step out of avoidance.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits christian life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Name the avoidance: what you're not looking at and why.

Prompts:
- What's the money thing you're avoiding right now: bills, balance, debt, a conversation?
- What happens in your body when you think about looking at it? (chest tightens, stomach drops, brain goes blank?)
- What's the story you tell yourself about why you can't deal with it right now?
- What has avoidance cost you so far: late fees, credit damage, relationship strain, shame?
- What would you feel if you looked and it was actually manageable?
- How does faith language help this problem, and where does it make you hide instead of tell the truth?

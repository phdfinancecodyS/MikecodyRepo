## What This Helps With

You learned to do everything alone. It kept you alive. But it's costing you now. This guide is about letting people back in without losing yourself.
This version is written for people whose mental health load is tangled with symptoms, flare cycles, pain, fatigue, uncertainty, and the extra work of living in a body that takes more management.

## What Is Happening

Chronic illness and chronic pain change the rules. Energy is less predictable, symptoms can wipe out capacity fast, and a lot of advice doesn't account for the real cost of simply getting through a day. Your mental health is affected by both the condition and the life restriction around it.

Lone wolf to team member is learning to trust a team when your self-reliance has been your survival mechanism. Being part of something means vulnerability. It means depending on others. Your hypervigilance makes team trust feel impossible. But you're learning that operation is better with team.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask where this problem gets sharper during flares, fatigue, pain spikes, or the frustration of not being able to do what you want at the pace you want.
2. Start with one person on the team. Practice vulnerability in small doses (ask for help, admit you don't know, say you struggled).
3. Notice what happens: The team usually responds with stronger connection, not judgment.
4. Gradually expand: More people, more vulnerability, more trust.

## What To Say

- My body is part of this problem, not just the backdrop.
- I need a plan that respects energy limits and symptom reality.
- I'm a lone wolf and team trust doesn't come easy to me.
- I'm practicing asking for help and admitting when I'm struggling.
- I'm learning that depending on a team doesn't make me weak.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting expectations that ignore pain, fatigue, flare cycles, or the recovery cost of everyday tasks.
- Treating self-reliance like a personality trait instead of a survival pattern that's outlived its usefulness.
- Trusting the whole team at once instead of one person at a time: trust scales slowly.
- Interpreting needing help as weakness when it's actually the next skill to build.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: think about the last time someone offered help and you said no. What would've happened if you'd said yes?
- Before bed tonight: name one thing you're carrying alone that you don't actually have to.
- Tomorrow: ask one person for one specific thing. "Can you handle this today?": and let them.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits chronic illness / chronic pain life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Understand the lone wolf pattern: what it protects and what it costs.

Prompts:
- When did you start doing everything yourself? What happened that made depending on others feel unsafe?
- What does self-reliance protect you from: disappointment, vulnerability, being a burden, being controlled?
- When someone offers help, what's the automatic thought? ("I don't need it," "They'll let me down," "It's weakness")
- What has the lone wolf cost you: relationships, health, sleep, joy?
- Is there one person you've almost let in? What stopped you?
- What symptoms, limits, or energy patterns are most connected to this issue?

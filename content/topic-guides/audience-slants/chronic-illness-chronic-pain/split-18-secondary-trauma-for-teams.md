## What This Helps With

Your team absorbs hard things together. This is about what happens when the weight hits the group: and what to do before it tears the team apart.
This version is written for people whose mental health load is tangled with symptoms, flare cycles, pain, fatigue, uncertainty, and the extra work of living in a body that takes more management.

## What Is Happening

Chronic illness and chronic pain change the rules. Energy is less predictable, symptoms can wipe out capacity fast, and a lot of advice doesn't account for the real cost of simply getting through a day. Your mental health is affected by both the condition and the life restriction around it.

Your team absorbs hard things together: calls, cases, scenes, patients: and nobody processes it because there's always another one coming. Over time, the weight becomes the water you swim in. Cynicism replaces compassion. Dark humor replaces feeling. And someone on the team is quietly drowning while everyone pretends the culture is fine.

Secondary traumatic stress hits teams differently than individuals: it spreads. One person's burnout becomes everyone's irritability, turnover, and silence.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask where this problem gets sharper during flares, fatigue, pain spikes, or the frustration of not being able to do what you want at the pace you want.
2. Check in with one teammate: not about work, about them. "How are you actually doing?"
3. Name one thing the team has absorbed recently that nobody has said out loud.
4. Create five minutes of space for the team to name what they're carrying. No fixing. Just naming.

## What To Say

- My body is part of this problem, not just the backdrop.
- I need a plan that respects energy limits and symptom reality.
- We carry a lot and we don't talk about it enough. I want to change that.
- That last one hit me. Did it hit you too?
- If anyone on this team is struggling, I want them to know it's okay to say so.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting expectations that ignore pain, fatigue, flare cycles, or the recovery cost of everyday tasks.
- Treating debrief as a mandatory box to check instead of a genuine space to process.
- Using "we're all fine" as the team's default because nobody wants to be the first to break.
- Ignoring the teammate who went quiet because you're all "used to hard calls."

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: check in with one teammate: not about work, about them. "How are you actually doing?"
- Before bed tonight: think about what the team has absorbed recently and whether anyone has said it out loud.
- Tomorrow: create one space: even five minutes: for the team to name what they're carrying. No fixing. Just naming.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits chronic illness / chronic pain life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Assess the team's weight: what you're all carrying and who's struggling.

Prompts:
- What's the heaviest thing the team has absorbed recently? Has anyone named it?
- Who on the team has gone quieter, more cynical, or more detached lately?
- What does the team do with hard calls/cases/shifts: debrief, joke, drink, or nothing?
- What's the team's unspoken rule about showing struggle? ("We don't talk about it," "Suck it up," "Only the new people struggle")
- If someone on the team was in crisis, would you know? Would they tell anyone?
- What symptoms, limits, or energy patterns are most connected to this issue?

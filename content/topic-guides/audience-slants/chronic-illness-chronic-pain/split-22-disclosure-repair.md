## What This Helps With

You told someone something hard and it didn't go well. Or you're thinking about telling and you're terrified. Either way, this helps.
This version is written for people whose mental health load is tangled with symptoms, flare cycles, pain, fatigue, uncertainty, and the extra work of living in a body that takes more management.

## What Is Happening

Chronic illness and chronic pain change the rules. Energy is less predictable, symptoms can wipe out capacity fast, and a lot of advice doesn't account for the real cost of simply getting through a day. Your mental health is affected by both the condition and the life restriction around it.

Disclosure is one of the hardest things a person can do. You're telling someone something that could change how they see you. And sometimes the person you told wasn't ready, or they reacted badly, or it broke something you didn't expect to break. Now you're carrying the original thing PLUS the fallout from telling.

Or maybe you haven't told yet and the fear is paralyzing you. Both situations live here.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask where this problem gets sharper during flares, fatigue, pain spikes, or the frustration of not being able to do what you want at the pace you want.
2. If you already disclosed and it went badly: give it 48 hours before you try to repair. Reactions settle.
3. If you're preparing to disclose: write down exactly what you want them to know, and exactly what you need from them.
4. Either way: decide what outcome you can live with and what you'll do if it doesn't go that well.

## What To Say

- My body is part of this problem, not just the backdrop.
- I need a plan that respects energy limits and symptom reality.
- I told you something hard and I know it landed heavy. Can we talk about it now that we've both had time?
- I have something I need to tell you. I don't need you to fix it: I need you to hear it.
- I'm scared to tell you this, and I'm telling you anyway because keeping it inside is worse.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting expectations that ignore pain, fatigue, flare cycles, or the recovery cost of everyday tasks.
- Disclosing in the middle of a fight when emotions are already high.
- Expecting the other person to react exactly the way you need: they're processing too.
- Deciding the relationship is over because the first reaction wasn't perfect.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: write down what you want them to know and why it matters.
- Before bed tonight: decide who you want to tell and what you need from them when you do.
- Tomorrow: if you're ready, say it. If you're not, that's okay: bring it to your next session or write them a letter.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits chronic illness / chronic pain life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the disclosure: what you said (or need to say), to whom, and what happened (or might happen).

Prompts:
- What did you disclose (or need to disclose)? Write it in one sentence.
- Who did/will you tell? Why that person?
- If you already told them: what was their reaction? What did you need that you didn't get?
- If you haven't told them yet: what's the fear? What's the worst case? What's the likely case?
- What do you need from them: understanding, space, action, or just acknowledgment?
- What symptoms, limits, or energy patterns are most connected to this issue?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Either repair a disclosure that went badly or prepare for one that hasn't happened yet.

Prompts:
- If repairing: "What I needed you to hear was ______. What I need now is ______." I'll say this by: ______.
- If preparing: I'll tell ______ on ______. My opening sentence will be: ______.
- What I'll do if it doesn't go well (give space, seek other support, journal, therapist):
- One person besides the disclosure target who knows what I'm going through:
- By next week, I'll know this worked if:

## What This Helps With

You've tried everything and they won't take the help. This guide is for the helpless feeling of watching someone you love refuse to move.
This version is written for first responder life, where repeated exposure, shift strain, public pressure, dark humor, and post-incident carryover change how stress works.

## What Is Happening

First responder culture teaches you to function under pressure, suppress what's inconvenient, and keep moving. That helps on scene. It doesn't always help off scene. The same skills that keep you effective at work can make home life, sleep, relationships, and emotional recovery a lot harder.

When someone won't accept help, it usually means one of three things: they aren't ready to change, they don't trust your motives, or they think they should handle it alone. You can't force readiness. But you can stay connected, name what you're seeing, and keep your boundaries while their refusal plays out.

## What To Do Now

1. Separate on-scene function from off-scene recovery. What works in the job may be exactly what's breaking things at home.
2. Say clearly what you're seeing without judgment or argument.
3. Name one concrete help option and leave the door open.
4. Set your own boundary about what you'll and won't do, then step back.

## What To Say

- I know how to perform under pressure. I'm still learning how to come down from it.
- The fact that I can do the job doesn't mean the job isn't affecting me.
- I'm noticing you're pushing back on help right now.
- I want to help, and right now you're saying no. I respect that and I'm still here.
- Here's what I can do, and here's what I can't do. That part is up to you.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using competence at work as proof that the stress isn't costing you anything outside the job.
- Escalating your approach when they push back. More pressure doesn't equal more openness: it equals more walls.
- Taking their refusal personally. Their "no" is about their fear, their shame, or their readiness: not about your worthiness.
- Giving an ultimatum you can't follow through on. "If you don't get help I'm done" only works if you mean it and even then, it rarely opens doors.
- Assuming they don't know they need help. Most people who refuse help know exactly what's going on. They're refusing for their own reasons.
- Burning yourself out trying to save someone who hasn't asked to be saved. You matter in this equation too.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: accept that you can't force someone to accept help. Write down what that feels like.
- Before bed tonight: identify the one thing you can still do: staying present, keeping the door open, taking care of yourself.
- Tomorrow: reach out to your own support. Caring for someone who won't receive it is exhausting, and you need backup too.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits first responder life.

## Worksheet 1: When Someone Refuses Help: What You're Carrying

Goal: Get honest about what their refusal is doing to YOU so you can sustain the long game.

Prompts:
- Who is the person refusing help? What's your relationship? How long has this been going on?
- What have you tried so far? List everything: conversations, resources, ultimatums, begging, backing off.
- How are you feeling about their refusal right now? (frustrated, scared, guilty, angry, helpless, exhausted?) Name all of it.
- What's the fear underneath your urgency? What are you afraid will happen if they don't get help?
- How is their refusal affecting your own mental health, sleep, relationships, and daily functioning?
- What parts of the job follow you home most often: irritability, numbness, sleep disruption, scanning, or disconnection?

## Worksheet 2: Sustainable Support Plan

Goal: Build a plan you can maintain without destroying yourself in the process.

Prompts:
- The boundary I need to set with this person is: ___. In plain language: "I love you and I can't ___ anymore."
- The door I'll keep open: "Whenever you're ready, I'm here. Here's what I can offer: ___." (Be specific and realistic.)
- My own support person for THIS burden is: ___. I'll talk to them this week.
- The one thing I will stop doing that isn't working: ___.
- I'll check in with myself about this situation on: ___ (weekly). Questions I'll ask: Am I okay? Is my approach sustainable? Do I need to change anything?

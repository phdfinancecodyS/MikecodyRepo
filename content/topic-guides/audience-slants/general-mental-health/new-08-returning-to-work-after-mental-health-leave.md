## What This Helps With

Going back is harder than leaving. If you're dreading the return: the looks, the questions, the performance pressure: this guide walks you through it.
This version stays broad and practical so it works if you don't want a specific identity lens on the problem.

## What Is Happening

A lot of mental health problems get worse when people overcomplicate them or wait too long to act. The goal is to name the pattern clearly, understand it in plain language, and take one useful next step.

Returning to work after mental health leave is re-entry shock. Your brain got used to a different pace, and now you're back in the high-demand environment that contributed to your leaving. You're also under extra scrutiny in your own mind, terrified you'll fall apart again.

## What To Do Now

1. Strip the problem down to what's actually happening this week, not the whole story of your life.
2. Return to your light duty or part-time first if you can: don't go full intensity.
3. Name one work situation that was part of the original problem and plan one new response.
4. Schedule one check-in with your therapist or support person for mid-week.

## What To Say

- This is real, and I can work with what's in front of me right now.
- I don't need a perfect plan to take the next useful step.
- I'm back and taking this slow. I know what led to my leave and I'm managing it differently now.
- I'll be transparent if I need adjustments, and I'm committed to this role.
- I'm still in recovery and I'm working.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Staying vague about the problem so long that you can't act on it.
- Going back at full speed on day one to prove you're "fine." Ramp up gradually: your nervous system needs transition time.
- Announcing your diagnosis or reason for leave to everyone. Share on your terms, with people you trust, when you're ready.
- Interpreting a hard first week as proof you came back too soon. The first week is rough for everyone. Give it at least two weeks before deciding.
- Pretending nothing happened and hoping nobody asks. Have a prepared one-sentence answer that you're comfortable with.
- Neglecting the supports that stabilized you during leave (therapy, medication, routine) because you're "back to normal now."

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: write down your three biggest fears about going back. Name them.
- Before bed tonight: identify one person at work you trust and decide to check in with them first.
- Tomorrow: plan your first day with margins: don't stack meetings, don't overperform, don't try to prove you're fine.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits general mental health life.

## Worksheet 1: Return-to-Work Readiness Check

Goal: Get honest about where you actually are: not where you think you should be: before walking back in.

Prompts:
- What's the biggest fear you have about going back? Name it specifically. (People knowing, not being able to handle it, being treated differently, falling apart again?)
- How are you sleeping, eating, and functioning right now? Scale of 1–10 for each. Be honest.
- What does your therapist/doctor say about your readiness? Do you agree with them? Where do you disagree?
- What do your coworkers know about why you were gone? What story are you telling? What do you wish you could say?
- What's the one part of the job you actually miss? What's the one part you dread?
- What part of this problem is most active in your life right now?

## Worksheet 2: First-Week-Back Survival Plan

Goal: Build a concrete plan for your first week so you're not making it up on the fly while your nervous system is screaming.

Prompts:
- Day 1: My plan is to arrive at ___, leave at ___, and talk to ___ people max. My exit plan if I hit a wall: ___.
- The sentence I'll use when someone asks where I've been: "___." (Practice saying it out loud three times right now.)
- My regulation tools I'll keep accessible at work: ___ (headphones, walk break, breathing app, cold water, texting my person).
- My check-in person for this week is: ___. I'll text them at ___ each day with a one-word status.
- If I realize I came back too soon, my plan is: ___ (talk to HR, call my therapist, take a half day). Coming back too soon isn't failure: it's information.

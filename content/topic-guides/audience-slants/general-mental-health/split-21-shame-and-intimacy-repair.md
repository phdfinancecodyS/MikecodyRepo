## What This Helps With

When shame makes intimacy feel impossible: when you can't be seen or let someone close: this is the guide for that stuck place.
This version stays broad and practical so it works if you don't want a specific identity lens on the problem.

## What Is Happening

A lot of mental health problems get worse when people overcomplicate them or wait too long to act. The goal is to name the pattern clearly, understand it in plain language, and take one useful next step.

Shame doesn't just live in your head: it lives in your body, and it shows up loudest when someone tries to get close. Intimacy requires being seen, and shame says being seen will destroy you. So your body shuts down, pulls away, dissociates, or picks a fight right before closeness happens. It's not that you don't want connection. It's that your nervous system treats vulnerability like a threat. The wall goes up before you even decide to build it.

## What To Do Now

1. Strip the problem down to what's actually happening this week, not the whole story of your life.
2. Name the shame pattern out loud to yourself: "When closeness starts, I ______ (shut down, pull away, freeze, get angry)."
3. Tell your partner one honest thing about what happens inside you when intimacy gets close: not the whole story, just one piece.
4. Practice one moment of being seen without performing: eye contact, a pause, an honest sentence.

## What To Say

- This is real, and I can work with what's in front of me right now.
- I don't need a perfect plan to take the next useful step.
- When we get close, something in me shuts down. It's not about you: it's shame.
- I want to be here with you and my body is fighting it. Bear with me.
- I don't need you to fix this. I need you to know it's happening so we can move through it together.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Staying vague about the problem so long that you can't act on it.
- Avoiding intimacy entirely and calling it "not being in the mood" when it's actually shame.
- Performing closeness instead of being honest about the wall that's up.
- Waiting until shame is gone before trying: shame shrinks with exposure, not avoidance.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: notice where shame lives in your body when you think about intimacy. Name the sensation.
- Before bed tonight: write down what you need from your partner that you haven't asked for.
- Tomorrow: start with one sentence. "I need you to know that..." You don't have to finish the whole conversation.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits general mental health life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the shame-intimacy pattern: when it shows up, what triggers it, and what your body does.

Prompts:
- What happens in your body when intimacy gets close: do you freeze, pull away, shut down, or get angry?
- What does shame tell you about yourself in those moments? Write it word for word.
- When's the last time you felt genuinely safe being seen by your partner? What was different?
- What's the earliest memory of learning that being seen was dangerous?
- What does your partner see when shame takes over: and what do they not see?
- What part of this problem is most active in your life right now?

## What This Helps With

If your brain feels like it's running through mud: slow processing, lost words, frustration: this guide is built for that exact experience.
This version stays broad and practical so it works if you don't want a specific identity lens on the problem.

## What Is Happening

A lot of mental health problems get worse when people overcomplicate them or wait too long to act. The goal is to name the pattern clearly, understand it in plain language, and take one useful next step.

TBI brain fog isn't laziness or distraction: it's your injured brain running out of processing capacity. After a concussion or TBI, cognitive demands that used to be easy now drain you fast. Words get lost, decisions stall, conversations exhaust you, and multitasking becomes impossible. The fog usually worsens with overstimulation and improves with rest and structure.

## What To Do Now

1. Strip the problem down to what's actually happening this week, not the whole story of your life.
2. Simplify right now: one task, one screen, one input at a time. Reduce the cognitive load.
3. Set a timer for 20 minutes of focused work, then take a 10-minute rest break. Repeat.
4. Write things down. Don't rely on your memory: externalize it onto paper, lists, or alarms.

## What To Say

- This is real, and I can work with what's in front of me right now.
- I don't need a perfect plan to take the next useful step.
- My brain is fogged right now. I need to slow down and do one thing at a time.
- I'm not being lazy: I'm working within what my brain can handle today.
- I need things repeated, written down, or simplified. That's how my recovery works right now.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Staying vague about the problem so long that you can't act on it.
- Powering through fog like it's laziness. Brain fog after TBI is neurological, not motivational: pushing harder makes it worse.
- Expecting linear recovery. TBI recovery has good days and bad days for months. One bad day doesn't mean you're regressing.
- Overloading your brain with screens, noise, and multitasking when it's already struggling. Reduce input before adding demands.
- Comparing yourself to pre-injury function. That benchmark isn't fair right now: work with today's brain.
- Skipping rest breaks because you feel "fine for now." Cognitive fatigue is cumulative and hits hard later.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: simplify. Pick one task. Set a timer for 20 minutes. Do that task and nothing else.
- Before bed tonight: write tomorrow's schedule with as much detail as you need: times, steps, locations. Don't rely on memory.
- Tomorrow: take breaks between cognitive tasks. Your brain needs recovery windows: respect them.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits general mental health life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the fog: when it's worst, what makes it better, and what you've stopped doing because of it.

Prompts:
- What does brain fog feel like for you: slow processing, lost words, blank spaces, confusion?
- What time of day is worst? What makes the fog thicker (noise, multitasking, screens, fatigue)?
- What have you given up because your brain doesn't work the way it used to?
- What do people around you notice: and do they get it or dismiss it?
- What helps: even a little: when the fog lifts? (Rest, quiet, routine, breaks?)
- What part of this problem is most active in your life right now?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Build one fog-day protocol that works with your brain instead of against it.

Prompts:
- My cognitive peak time is ______. I'll schedule my hardest task during that window.
- One tool I'll use to externalize my memory (written schedule, alarms, lists, whiteboard):
- My fog-day rule: after ______ minutes of focus, I take a ______ minute break.
- The sentence I'll say when fog hits: "My brain needs a break. I'll come back to this at ______." 
- By this weekend, I'll know this worked if:

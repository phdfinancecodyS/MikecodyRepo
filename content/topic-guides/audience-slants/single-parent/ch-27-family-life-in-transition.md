## What This Helps With

Everything's shifting: schedules, roles, expectations: and nobody handed you a playbook. Here's how to keep your family intact while the ground moves.
This version is written for single-parent life, where mental health problems hit in the middle of responsibility, no backup, and the pressure to keep functioning even when you're depleted.

## What Is Happening

Single-parent stress changes the shape of a problem because there's less margin. You may not get to collapse, sleep in, or hand things off. That means symptoms can get hidden behind competence, and small problems can feel huge because there isn't much buffer.

Family life in transition is the shock of changing your family structure (separation, retirement, kids leaving, remarriage). The routines that held you're gone. New roles are confusing. Everyone is adjusting at different speeds. The family you knew isn't the family you've now.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask what this problem costs you as a parent, and where you need support before it becomes a crisis of exhaustion or disconnection.
2. Name the transition explicitly: We're in a new family structure and everyone is figuring out their role.
3. Establish one new anchor that replaces the old routine (new breakfast time, new weekly check-in).
4. Normalize that adjustment takes time and people will struggle during the transition.

## What To Say

- I'm carrying a lot with very little margin, and that changes what support needs to look like.
- I don't need judgment. I need practical help and a realistic plan.
- This is a family transition and it doesn't have to feel normal right away.
- Our new rhythm is [new routine] and I'm learning it too.
- Let's be patient with each other as we figure out how this family works now.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting a support plan that assumes you have extra time, extra childcare, or extra energy that you don't actually have.
- Expecting the family to snap into a new normal overnight when everyone adjusts at different speeds.
- Treating the transition like a problem to solve instead of a process to live through.
- Letting guilt about the change stop you from building new structure that the family actually needs.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: name the one thing that changed most recently and how it's affecting the family: be specific.
- Before bed tonight: talk to one family member about the transition. Not to fix it, just to name it together.
- Tomorrow: pick one new routine or anchor point that gives the family something predictable to land on.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits single parent life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Name the transition clearly and see who's struggling with what.

Prompts:
- What changed: separation, retirement, move, new household structure, role shift?
- Who in the family is adjusting fastest? Who's having the hardest time?
- What old routine disappeared that the family hasn't replaced yet?
- What's the biggest source of friction in the new arrangement?
- What does each family member need most right now that they aren't getting?
- When does this problem hit hardest in the rhythm of parenting, and what is happening around it?

## What This Helps With

You went quiet. Checked out. Shut the door. And now you don't know how to come back. Here's how to reconnect without it being a whole thing.
This version is written for single-parent life, where mental health problems hit in the middle of responsibility, no backup, and the pressure to keep functioning even when you're depleted.

## What Is Happening

Single-parent stress changes the shape of a problem because there's less margin. You may not get to collapse, sleep in, or hand things off. That means symptoms can get hidden behind competence, and small problems can feel huge because there isn't much buffer.

Shutdown is your nervous system pulling the emergency brake. When the input gets too loud: too much conflict, too much feeling, too much pressure: your brain doesn't fight or flee. It shuts the whole system down. You go flat. You go quiet. You disappear inside yourself even though your body is still in the room.

This isn't laziness. It's not the silent treatment. It's a dorsal vagal response: your nervous system deciding that disconnection is safer than engagement. The problem is, the people around you can't tell the difference between "I'm protecting myself" and "I don't care."

## What To Do Now

1. Ask what this problem costs you as a parent, and where you need support before it becomes a crisis of exhaustion or disconnection.
2. Notice you've shut down. Name it. "I'm shut down right now": even just to yourself.
3. Do one thing that moves your body out of freeze: splash cold water on your face, stand up, press your feet into the floor.
4. Re-enter with one small action: a text, eye contact, one sentence. You don't need a speech.

## What To Say

- I'm carrying a lot with very little margin, and that changes what support needs to look like.
- I don't need judgment. I need practical help and a realistic plan.
- I shut down. I'm not ignoring you: I went somewhere else for a minute.
- I want to come back to this. I just need my brain to come back online first.
- I'm here. I'm trying.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting a support plan that assumes you have extra time, extra childcare, or extra energy that you don't actually have.
- Waiting until you feel "ready" to reconnect: you won't. Action comes first, feeling follows.
- Disappearing for days without any signal and expecting the other person to just wait.
- Confusing shutdown with needing space: space has a return plan, shutdown doesn't.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: notice that you've checked out and name it. "I'm shut down right now."
- Before bed tonight: text one person and say: "I went quiet. I'm still here."
- Tomorrow: re-enter one conversation or relationship you pulled away from. You don't need a reason: just show back up.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits single parent life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the shutdown pattern: what triggers it, how deep it goes, and what it costs.

Prompts:
- What usually triggers the shutdown: conflict, overwhelm, criticism, emotion, noise?
- When you shut down, what does it look like from the outside? What's happening inside?
- How long do you usually stay shut down: minutes, hours, days?
- What does the other person usually do when you go quiet? Does it help or make it worse?
- What would coming back look like if it didn't have to be a big thing?
- When does this problem hit hardest in the rhythm of parenting, and what is happening around it?

## What This Helps With

Most hard conversations fail because both people are in the wrong zone. Red means stop. Yellow means slow down. Green means go. Here's how to read it in real time.
This version is written for single-parent life, where mental health problems hit in the middle of responsibility, no backup, and the pressure to keep functioning even when you're depleted.

## What Is Happening

Single-parent stress changes the shape of a problem because there's less margin. You may not get to collapse, sleep in, or hand things off. That means symptoms can get hidden behind competence, and small problems can feel huge because there isn't much buffer.

Red/yellow/green communication is mapping where people are emotionally and how safe they're to approach. Red means disconnected, dangerous, or shut down. Yellow means present but unstable. Green means engaged and accessible. Most people in relationships don't explicitly read and respond to these zones.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask what this problem costs you as a parent, and where you need support before it becomes a crisis of exhaustion or disconnection.
2. Learn to notice your own and your partner's color (red/yellow/green) in real time.
3. When someone is red, don't push conversation. Let them be red and stay safe yourself.
4. Only have important conversations when both people are green.

## What To Say

- I'm carrying a lot with very little margin, and that changes what support needs to look like.
- I don't need judgment. I need practical help and a realistic plan.
- I see that you're in red right now so I'm not going to push conversation.
- I'm in yellow today and I need some space before we talk about important stuff.
- When we're both green, we can talk about [topic]. Right now neither of us are ready.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting a support plan that assumes you have extra time, extra childcare, or extra energy that you don't actually have.
- Trying to fix the whole relationship in one conversation.
- Having the conversation when neither person is regulated enough for it.
- Skipping clear repair, boundaries, or follow-up after the hard moment.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: honestly assess your color right now. Red, yellow, or green? Write it down.
- Before bed tonight: think about the last hard conversation that went wrong. What color were you in? What color were they?
- Tomorrow: before starting any important conversation, check both colors first. If either person is red, don't start.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits single parent life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Learn to read emotional zones: yours and theirs: before starting hard conversations.

Prompts:
- What color are you usually in when fights start: red, yellow, or green?
- What does your partner's red look like? What about their yellow?
- What triggers push you from green to yellow fastest?
- When's the last time you had a conversation where both of you were green? What was different?
- What do you usually do when someone is in red: push harder, withdraw, or match their energy?
- When does this problem hit hardest in the rhythm of parenting, and what is happening around it?

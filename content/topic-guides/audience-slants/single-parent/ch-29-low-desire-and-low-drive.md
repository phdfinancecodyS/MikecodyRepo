## What This Helps With

When you've lost interest in things that used to matter: including the person next to you: that's worth paying attention to. No pressure, just honesty.
This version is written for single-parent life, where mental health problems hit in the middle of responsibility, no backup, and the pressure to keep functioning even when you're depleted.

## What Is Happening

Single-parent stress changes the shape of a problem because there's less margin. You may not get to collapse, sleep in, or hand things off. That means symptoms can get hidden behind competence, and small problems can feel huge because there isn't much buffer.

Low desire and low drive often trace back to nervous system depletion and fear. Your body isn't in safe-enough mode for pleasure and connection. You're touched-out, overstimulated, or carrying so much hypervigilance that surrender feels impossible.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask what this problem costs you as a parent, and where you need support before it becomes a crisis of exhaustion or disconnection.
2. Start with non-sexual touch (hand-holding, back scratch, sitting close) with zero expectation of progress.
3. Tell your partner: My low drive isn't about you. I'm dysregulated and reconnecting with my body.
4. Take pressure off the 'should' and practice one small reconnection daily.

## What To Say

- I'm carrying a lot with very little margin, and that changes what support needs to look like.
- I don't need judgment. I need practical help and a realistic plan.
- My desire is low because my nervous system isn't in a safe-enough place for pleasure.
- I want connection and my body is taking time to trust.
- I'm rebuilding my capacity for intimacy and I need your patience, not performance.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting a support plan that assumes you have extra time, extra childcare, or extra energy that you don't actually have.
- Adding pressure when the real need is safety, pacing, or clarity.
- Assuming avoidance means you don't care about connection.
- Trying to force closeness before the body feels safe enough for it.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: name one thing that used to interest you and notice what comes up when you think about it.
- Before bed tonight: be honest with yourself about whether this is stress, disconnection, or something medical. Write it down.
- Tomorrow: take one small step toward connection: doesn't have to be physical. A conversation, a shared meal, a walk together.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits single parent life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Understand what's driving the low desire: stress, disconnection, body, or safety.

Prompts:
- When did desire start dropping? What changed around that time?
- Is the low drive about your body, your relationship, your stress level, or all of it?
- What does your partner think is happening? What haven't you told them?
- When you think about intimacy, what shows up: guilt, pressure, nothing, dread?
- What's one kind of closeness that still feels possible, even if it's not sexual?
- When does this problem hit hardest in the rhythm of parenting, and what is happening around it?

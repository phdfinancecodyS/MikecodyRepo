## What This Helps With

You need people you can call at 2am. Not a lot of people: just the right ones. Here's how to build that list and actually use it.
This version is written for single-parent life, where mental health problems hit in the middle of responsibility, no backup, and the pressure to keep functioning even when you're depleted.

## What Is Happening

Single-parent stress changes the shape of a problem because there's less margin. You may not get to collapse, sleep in, or hand things off. That means symptoms can get hidden behind competence, and small problems can feel huge because there isn't much buffer.

A safety network isn't just crisis contacts. It's 3-5 specific people for different needs: someone for practical help, someone for emotional support, someone for crisis, someone you can be honest with. Most people have none of these named in advance, so they spin when they need help.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask what this problem costs you as a parent, and where you need support before it becomes a crisis of exhaustion or disconnection.
2. Name three real people you trust with different types of needs.
3. Text each person one specific help question to gauge response.
4. Write their names and what they're good for in your phone.

## What To Say

- I'm carrying a lot with very little margin, and that changes what support needs to look like.
- I don't need judgment. I need practical help and a realistic plan.
- I'm building a support list. Can I count on you for [specific type of help]?
- I would check in with you before things get urgent.
- This helps me know I've someone real to call, not just a crisis number.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting a support plan that assumes you have extra time, extra childcare, or extra energy that you don't actually have.
- Assuming you'll figure out who to call when you're in crisis. In crisis, your brain narrows: the plan has to be made before you need it.
- Putting everyone in the same tier. Your 2 AM person is different from your "grab coffee" person. Name who does what.
- Listing people but never testing the network. Reach out during a small hard moment: see who shows up before you need them for the big one.
- Having a network of zero because asking feels like weakness. A safety network isn't a sign you're failing: it's infrastructure.
- Assuming professionals are enough. Therapists and crisis lines matter, but you also need people who know your name and your life.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: write down three people you could call at 2am if you needed to. If you can't name three, that's important information.
- Before bed tonight: text one of them and say: "You're someone I trust. I want you to know that."
- Tomorrow: add 988 and Crisis Text Line (text HOME to 741741) to your phone contacts. Build the net before you need it.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits single parent life.

## Worksheet 1: Safety Network Inventory

Goal: Map who's actually in your network right now: and where the gaps are.

Prompts:
- Who would you call at 2 AM if you were in a dark place? If no one comes to mind, that's the gap this worksheet is here to fill.
- List up to five people who know you're struggling or have struggled. What role does each one play? (listener, practical help, distraction, professional?)
- Which of those people have you actually tested? Meaning: have you reached out during a hard moment and they showed up?
- Where are the gaps? Do you have someone for crisis but nobody for daily check-ins? Someone for logistics but nobody for emotional support?
- What's one barrier that stops you from reaching out to the people on your list? (Pride, fear of burdening them, past bad experiences?)
- When does this problem hit hardest in the rhythm of parenting, and what is happening around it?

## Worksheet 2: Safety Network Builder

Goal: Turn your map into a usable plan with real names, real numbers, and real agreements.

Prompts:
- My Tier 1 (daily/weekly check-in) person is: ___. Our check-in plan: ___ (text every morning, call on Sundays, walk together Wednesdays).
- My Tier 2 (bad-day call) person is: ___. I'll tell them: "If I reach out and say ___, that means I need you to ___."
- My Tier 3 (crisis) contact is: ___. Professional backup: ___ (therapist name/number, 988, Crisis Text Line).
- One person I want to add to my network but haven't asked yet: ___. I'll approach them this week by saying: "I'm building a support network and I'd like you to be part of it."
- I'll review and update this network on: ___ (first of every month, after any major event, when something changes).

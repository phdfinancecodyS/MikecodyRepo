## What This Helps With

The first year out is the hardest. Nobody prepares you for the identity loss, the boredom, and the grief of leaving. This guide walks through it month by month.
This version is written for single-parent life, where mental health problems hit in the middle of responsibility, no backup, and the pressure to keep functioning even when you're depleted.

## What Is Happening

Single-parent stress changes the shape of a problem because there's less margin. You may not get to collapse, sleep in, or hand things off. That means symptoms can get hidden behind competence, and small problems can feel huge because there isn't much buffer.

Leaving the job (through separation or retirement) is an identity loss. Your routine vanishes, your sense of purpose gets muddled, and suddenly you've space and no structure in it. The first year is disorientation, not failure. You're rebuilding how you see yourself outside the role.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask what this problem costs you as a parent, and where you need support before it becomes a crisis of exhaustion or disconnection.
2. Don't search for a replacement identity in year one: just stabilize the basics (sleep, eat, move).
3. Keep one connection to the community (coffee with someone from work, volunteer in adjacent field, something).
4. Don't fill all the time instantly; boredom is actually part of how your nervous system recalibrates.

## What To Say

- I'm carrying a lot with very little margin, and that changes what support needs to look like.
- I don't need judgment. I need practical help and a realistic plan.
- I'm no longer in the role and I'm figuring out who I'm now.
- This isn't laziness or depression: this is transition and it takes time.
- I'm staying connected to the community in smaller ways while I settle.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting a support plan that assumes you have extra time, extra childcare, or extra energy that you don't actually have.
- Rushing to replace the identity. Taking a new job, starting a business, or making a big life change in the first six months is almost always premature. Stabilize first.
- Drinking more because there's no one watching. Without the structure of the job, substances fill the vacuum fast.
- Interpreting the disorientation as depression. It might be depression: or it might be normal transition grief. Get assessed before you decide.
- Cutting off every connection to the old life. You need some thread to who you were. One coffee a month with someone from the work can be enough.
- Filling every hour because empty space feels wrong. Boredom is actually your nervous system coming down from years of hypervigilance. Let it happen.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: write down three things you miss about the old life and three things you'd like to build in the new one.
- Before bed tonight: connect with one person who's been through this transition ahead of you.
- Tomorrow: do one thing that belongs to the new chapter. Sign up for something, explore something, start something small.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits single parent life.

## Worksheet 1: Year-One Transition Inventory

Goal: Take stock of where you actually are in the transition: not where you think you should be.

Prompts:
- How long have you been out of the role? How are you spending your days right now?
- What do you miss most about the job? What do you miss least? Be specific.
- How's your body doing? (sleep, appetite, energy, aches, restlessness?) Rate each 1–10.
- What's the hardest part of the transition that nobody warned you about?
- Who are you now that you're not [the job title]? If you can't answer that yet, write: "I don't know yet and that's where I am."
- When does this problem hit hardest in the rhythm of parenting, and what is happening around it?

## Worksheet 2: Year-One Stabilization Plan

Goal: Build a basic structure for the first year that keeps you connected, functional, and honest about how you're doing.

Prompts:
- Three basics I'll protect daily: sleep by ___, eat ___, move ___ (walk counts). These aren't goals: they're life support.
- One connection to the old community I'll maintain: ___ (monthly coffee, volunteer role, group text). With: ___.
- One new thing I'll explore: not commit to, just explore: ___. I'll try it by: ___.
- My check-in person for this year is: ___. We'll connect ___ (weekly, monthly). What I'll tell them: "If you notice me ___ing, say something."
- If I'm not stabilizing by month ___: I'll take this step: ___ (see a therapist who works with career transitions, call the veteran/retiree line, talk to my doctor).

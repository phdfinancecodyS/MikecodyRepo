## What This Helps With

Yesterday was bad. Today you feel like you got hit by a truck. This is the day-after plan: because nobody talks about what to do the morning after a hard one.
This version is written for single-parent life, where mental health problems hit in the middle of responsibility, no backup, and the pressure to keep functioning even when you're depleted.

## What Is Happening

Single-parent stress changes the shape of a problem because there's less margin. You may not get to collapse, sleep in, or hand things off. That means symptoms can get hidden behind competence, and small problems can feel huge because there isn't much buffer.

The day after an incident, your nervous system crashes. Adrenaline is gone, emotions hit, and your body feels wrecked. This isn't weakness: this is what happens after intense activation. The crash is part of the cycle and managing it well prevents worse spirals later.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask what this problem costs you as a parent, and where you need support before it becomes a crisis of exhaustion or disconnection.
2. Move slowly and don't schedule anything heavy for the next 24 hours.
3. Eat and hydrate like you're recovering from the flu.
4. Lower your bar for today; one small win is enough.

## What To Say

- I'm carrying a lot with very little margin, and that changes what support needs to look like.
- I don't need judgment. I need practical help and a realistic plan.
- Today I'm recovering, not performing.
- My body is telling me it needs downtime and I'm listening.
- I made it through yesterday. Today is about stability.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting a support plan that assumes you have extra time, extra childcare, or extra energy that you don't actually have.
- Debriefing too soon. The day after isn't the time for a full emotional deep-dive: your nervous system is still in shock.
- Drinking to take the edge off the night after a bad call. Alcohol suppresses REM sleep and makes the crash worse.
- Isolating because you think nobody else is affected. They are: they're just hiding it too.
- Pressuring yourself to "bounce back" by next shift. Recovery from high-impact incidents takes days, not hours.
- Replaying the call looking for what you did wrong before your body has even stabilized. Process later: stabilize first.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: hydrate, eat something, and do the minimum. Today is a recovery day, not a performance day.
- Before bed tonight: tell one person what happened yesterday: not the whole story, just enough to break the silence.
- Tomorrow: check in with yourself. If you're still in the crash, that's okay. If you're surfacing, pick up one thing.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits single parent life.

## Worksheet 1: Post-Incident Crash Tracker

Goal: Map what happens to your body and mind in the 24–72 hours after high-impact incidents so you can recognize the crash pattern.

Prompts:
- Describe the last incident that hit you hard. What happened? What role did you play?
- What did the crash look like the day after? (flat affect, couldn't sleep, couldn't eat, rage, crying, numb, wired?)
- How long did the crash last before you felt somewhat functional again? Days? A week? Longer?
- What did you do during the crash? What helped and what made it worse?
- Who checked on you afterward? Who didn't? What did you wish someone had said or done?
- When does this problem hit hardest in the rhythm of parenting, and what is happening around it?

## Worksheet 2: Day-After Recovery Protocol

Goal: Build your personal post-incident recovery plan so you don't have to figure it out while you're in the crash.

Prompts:
- My post-incident crash pattern usually includes these three signs: ___, ___, ___.
- In the first 24 hours after a bad call, I will do these three things: ___ (sleep, eat, move: be specific).
- I will NOT do these things in the first 24 hours: ___ (drink, isolate, replay the call, make big decisions).
- The one person I'll text or call the day after: ___. What I'll say: "Yesterday was rough. Can we [walk/eat/sit together]?"
- If the crash lasts more than 72 hours, I'll take this step: ___ (call EAP, talk to peer support, see my therapist).

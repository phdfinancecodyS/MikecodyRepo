## What This Helps With

You love someone who carries heavy things. And now you're carrying them too. This guide is for the person beside the person: because you matter in this.
This version is written for single-parent life, where mental health problems hit in the middle of responsibility, no backup, and the pressure to keep functioning even when you're depleted.

## What Is Happening

Single-parent stress changes the shape of a problem because there's less margin. You may not get to collapse, sleep in, or hand things off. That means symptoms can get hidden behind competence, and small problems can feel huge because there isn't much buffer.

You're absorbing your partner's trauma like a sponge, and nobody told you it would change you too. Their nightmares are keeping you awake. Their hypervigilance is making you walk on eggshells. Their flat affect is making you feel invisible. And slowly, their symptoms are becoming yours: the anxiety, the irritability, the emotional exhaustion.

This is secondary traumatic stress. You're not weak for feeling it. You're human and you've been holding someone else's pain longer than any nervous system is designed to.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask what this problem costs you as a parent, and where you need support before it becomes a crisis of exhaustion or disconnection.
2. Check in with yourself: not them, you. How are you actually doing right now?
3. Name one thing you've been absorbing that isn't yours to carry.
4. Do one thing today that is exclusively for you. Not for them. Not for the family.

## What To Say

- I'm carrying a lot with very little margin, and that changes what support needs to look like.
- I don't need judgment. I need practical help and a realistic plan.
- I love you and I'm tired. Both things are true.
- I need some time that's just for me tonight and that doesn't mean I'm pulling away.
- I'm carrying more than I can hold. I need to talk to someone about my stuff, not just yours.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting a support plan that assumes you have extra time, extra childcare, or extra energy that you don't actually have.
- Making your partner's recovery your full-time job and losing yourself in the process.
- Feeling guilty about needing your own support: you can't pour from an empty cup and you know it.
- Waiting until you're completely burned out to ask for help.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: check in with yourself. Not them: you. How are you actually doing right now?
- Before bed tonight: name one thing you've been absorbing from your partner's stress that isn't yours to carry.
- Tomorrow: do one thing that's exclusively for you. Not for them, not for the family. Just you.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits single parent life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Track what you've absorbed: separate your partner's pain from yours.

Prompts:
- What symptoms of theirs have become yours? (Anxiety, hypervigilance, sleep problems, irritability?)
- When did you stop taking care of yourself? What was happening with them at that time?
- What do you pretend is fine that isn't?
- What would you say to a friend in your position? Why is it harder to say it to yourself?
- When's the last time you did something just because it made you happy?
- When does this problem hit hardest in the rhythm of parenting, and what is happening around it?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Reclaim one piece of your own life this week.

Prompts:
- One thing I'll do this week that's exclusively for me: ______.
- One boundary I need to set with my partner's struggle: "I can't ______ anymore, but I can ______." 
- One person I'll talk to about MY stuff (friend, therapist, support group): ______.
- One thing I'll stop apologizing for needing (sleep, space, fun, help):

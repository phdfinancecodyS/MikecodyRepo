## What This Helps With

If your body doesn't look or feel the way it used to: scars, weight, injury: and that's affecting how you show up in intimacy, this is for you.
This version is written with LGBTQ+ life in mind, including identity stress, belonging, safety, chosen family, and the extra labor of figuring out where you can fully exhale.

## What Is Happening

Mental health problems don't land in a vacuum. If you are LGBTQ+, the issue may be sitting on top of identity stress, rejection history, minority stress, hiding, code-switching, or exhaustion from always reading whether a space is safe. That doesn't mean your identity is the problem. It means the context matters.

Body image issues after trauma often trace to how your body was treated or how you survived. Scars, weight changes, asymmetry: your body carries the story. Sexual confidence gets tangled in appearance anxiety and safety fears mixed together.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being made worse by unsafety, hiding, isolation, or being around people who don't actually let you be known.
2. Separate body image (appearance) from body safety (does this body keep me safe?).
3. Rebuild relationship with one body part that troubles you: What does it do? How did it help me survive?
4. Practice one appearance-neutral intimacy action (shower together, manual stimulation, closeness without mirrors).

## What To Say

- I need support that doesn't make me shrink, explain myself, or scan the room first.
- Part of what makes this harder is the environment, not just me.
- My body image anxiety is mixed with safety anxiety and they're separate things.
- My body carries scars and they tell stories of survival, not failure.
- I can rebuild confidence in what my body does, separate from what it looks like.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Treating identity stress like it's irrelevant background noise when it's actively shaping the problem.
- Adding pressure when the real need is safety, pacing, or clarity.
- Assuming avoidance means you don't care about connection.
- Trying to force closeness before the body feels safe enough for it.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: look at yourself in a mirror for 30 seconds without narrating what's wrong. Just look.
- Before bed tonight: write down one thing your body has done for you that has nothing to do with how it looks.
- Tomorrow: wear something that makes you feel like you: not someone else's version of okay.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits lgbtq+ life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Separate appearance from safety: understand how body image is affecting intimacy.

Prompts:
- What part of your body are you most self-conscious about? What story does your brain tell about it?
- Do you avoid being seen (lights off, covered up, certain positions): and is that about appearance or safety?
- What did your body go through that changed how it looks? What did it survive?
- When does body image anxiety spike hardest: mirrors, intimacy, photos, comparison?
- What would it take for you to believe that your body is enough as it is right now?
- Where do you feel most defended, hidden, or on alert in your current environment?

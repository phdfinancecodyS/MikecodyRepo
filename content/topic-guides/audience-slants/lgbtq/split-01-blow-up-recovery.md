## What This Helps With

You blew up. It happened. Now what? This is the after plan: not a lecture, just how to come back from it and make the repair that actually matters.
This version is written with LGBTQ+ life in mind, including identity stress, belonging, safety, chosen family, and the extra labor of figuring out where you can fully exhale.

## What Is Happening

Mental health problems don't land in a vacuum. If you are LGBTQ+, the issue may be sitting on top of identity stress, rejection history, minority stress, hiding, code-switching, or exhaustion from always reading whether a space is safe. That doesn't mean your identity is the problem. It means the context matters.

This pattern usually starts in your body before it shows up in your words.
When intensity jumps fast, your best move is early interruption, not long explanation.
Repair is part of the plan, not a side note.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being made worse by unsafety, hiding, isolation, or being around people who don't actually let you be known.
2. Catch the earliest body cue and call a short reset.
3. Use one grounding action for 60-90 seconds.
4. Return with one repair sentence and one clear next step.

## What To Say

- I need support that doesn't make me shrink, explain myself, or scan the room first.
- Part of what makes this harder is the environment, not just me.
- I'm getting overloaded and I don't want to make this worse.
- Give me a short reset and I'll come back ready to continue.
- I care about this conversation, and I'm ready to restart better.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Treating identity stress like it's irrelevant background noise when it's actively shaping the problem.
- Explaining your behavior before owning the impact. "I was stressed" isn't a repair: "I lost control and I'm sorry" is.
- Taking distance with no return time. Saying "I need space" and disappearing for two days isn't a reset: it's abandonment with extra steps.
- Skipping the repair because it feels awkward. Awkward doesn't mean optional. The longer you wait, the harder it gets.
- Apologizing for how it made them feel instead of what you did. "I'm sorry you felt hurt" is a dodge. "I'm sorry I yelled" is ownership.
- Blowing up and then acting like nothing happened the next day. Everyone remembers. Pretending it didn't happen makes them trust you less, not more.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: take 90 seconds and cool your body down. Cold water on your wrists, walk outside, deep breath.
- Before bed tonight: write one sentence that takes ownership for the impact without defending your intent.
- Tomorrow: say that sentence to the person it was aimed at. Then ask what they need.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits lgbtq+ life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the blowup: what lit the fuse, what it looked like, and who caught the shrapnel.

Prompts:
- What happened right before the blowup? What were the first 30 seconds like in your body?
- Who was the target? Was it the person you were actually angry at?
- What's the sentence you said (or almost said) that you regret most?
- How did the other person look when it happened? What did their face do?
- How long does it usually take you to come back from one? Hours? Days?
- Where do you feel most defended, hidden, or on alert in your current environment?

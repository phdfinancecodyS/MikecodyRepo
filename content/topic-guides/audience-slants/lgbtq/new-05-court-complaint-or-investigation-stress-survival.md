## What This Helps With

You're under investigation, facing a complaint, or headed to court. The stress is different from anything else. Here's how to survive the process.
This version is written with LGBTQ+ life in mind, including identity stress, belonging, safety, chosen family, and the extra labor of figuring out where you can fully exhale.

## What Is Happening

Mental health problems don't land in a vacuum. If you are LGBTQ+, the issue may be sitting on top of identity stress, rejection history, minority stress, hiding, code-switching, or exhaustion from always reading whether a space is safe. That doesn't mean your identity is the problem. It means the context matters.

An investigation or complaint against you triggers threat detection in your body: your job status is threatened, your reputation is on the line, and you're probably scared. This isn't time for trying to feel normal. This is time for protecting yourself, following your union or legal guidance exactly, and managing the actual crisis, not your feelings about it.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being made worse by unsafety, hiding, isolation, or being around people who don't actually let you be known.
2. Get legal representation or union help immediately: don't skip this step.
3. Follow their guidance exactly. Don't try to handle this alone.
4. Set a time limit for daily worry (say, 20 minutes) and discipline yourself to it.

## What To Say

- I need support that doesn't make me shrink, explain myself, or scan the room first.
- Part of what makes this harder is the environment, not just me.
- I'm in an investigation and I need my representative at every step.
- I'm not answering questions without guidance because I'm protecting myself appropriately.
- This is temporary and stressful and I'm doing the right things.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Treating identity stress like it's irrelevant background noise when it's actively shaping the problem.
- Checking your phone every five minutes for updates. Set a designated check-in time and stick to it.
- Talking to everyone at work about the situation, hoping for reassurance. That usually makes it bigger, not smaller.
- Making catastrophic career decisions while the investigation is active. Don't quit, don't post, don't confront: stabilize first.
- Treating legal advice from coworkers as actual legal advice. Get a real attorney or union rep if you need one.
- Letting the anxiety run your sleep, eating, and relationships unchecked while telling yourself you're "handling it."

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: separate today from the outcome. You can't control the result. You can control how you get through today.
- Before bed tonight: write down what you're most afraid of. Getting it out of your head takes power away from it.
- Tomorrow: talk to your rep, your attorney, or a trusted supporter. Don't carry the legal stress alone.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits lgbtq+ life.

## Worksheet 1: Investigation Stress Map

Goal: Get clear on what the investigation stress is actually doing to you so you can manage it instead of spiraling.

Prompts:
- What's the investigation, complaint, or legal situation you're facing? Describe it in two sentences.
- What's the worst-case scenario your brain keeps running? Write it out: the full catastrophic version.
- Now write what's actually happening today. What's the real status right now?
- How is the stress showing up in your body and behavior? (sleep, appetite, irritability, avoidance, checking your phone compulsively?)
- Who knows about this? Who have you told? Who are you hiding it from, and why?
- Where do you feel most defended, hidden, or on alert in your current environment?

## Worksheet 2: Investigation Survival Plan

Goal: Build a concrete plan for getting through the wait without it consuming you.

Prompts:
- The one thing I can control today about this situation is: ___.
- The one thing I will STOP doing that's making the anxiety worse: ___ (refreshing email, asking coworkers for intel, reading worst-case forums).
- My designated "worry window" will be from ___ to ___ each day. Outside that window, I redirect.
- The person I'll lean on during this process: ___. What I need from them: ___ (listening, checking in, distraction, practical help).
- If I get news: good or bad: my plan is to: ___ before reacting. (Call someone, take a walk, wait 24 hours before making decisions.)

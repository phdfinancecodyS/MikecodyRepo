## What This Helps With

If someone you loved died by suicide and you're carrying a grief that nobody around you fully understands: this guide was written for exactly you.
This version is written with LGBTQ+ life in mind, including identity stress, belonging, safety, chosen family, and the extra labor of figuring out where you can fully exhale.

## What Is Happening

Mental health problems don't land in a vacuum. If you are LGBTQ+, the issue may be sitting on top of identity stress, rejection history, minority stress, hiding, code-switching, or exhaustion from always reading whether a space is safe. That doesn't mean your identity is the problem. It means the context matters.

Grief after suicide loss carries extra weight: shock, confusion, guilt about things unsaid, and sometimes anger at the person who left. Your brain keeps running counterfactuals: what could have changed this? Nothing you think right now will answer that question. You're in crisis-level grief and your job is survival first, meaning-making later.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being made worse by unsafety, hiding, isolation, or being around people who don't actually let you be known.
2. Tell three people what happened so you aren't holding this alone.
3. Get to one grief group or therapist this week (not optional).
4. Do one physical thing every day: walk, cry, write, punch a pillow. Move the grief through your body.

## What To Say

- I need support that doesn't make me shrink, explain myself, or scan the room first.
- Part of what makes this harder is the environment, not just me.
- I lost someone to suicide. That's what happened.
- I need help carrying this and I'm not going to pretend I'm fine.
- I don't know why they made that choice and right now I just need to get through each day.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Treating identity stress like it's irrelevant background noise when it's actively shaping the problem.
- Searching for the reason. You won't find one that makes it make sense, and the search keeps the wound open.
- Comparing your grief timeline to anyone else's. Suicide grief has its own clock: guilt, anger, and "what if" waves can come years later.
- Protecting everyone else from your grief while you drown in it. You're allowed to need support too.
- Avoiding talking about the person who died because you're afraid it'll upset others. Say their name.
- Blaming yourself for not seeing it. Hindsight creates a false clarity that wasn't available in real time.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: let yourself feel whatever you're feeling without judging it. There's no right way to grieve this.
- Before bed tonight: say their name out loud. To yourself, to someone, to the room. They existed.
- Tomorrow: connect with one person who understands this kind of loss: a support group, a counselor, someone who's been here.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits lgbtq+ life.

## Worksheet 1: Grief After Suicide Loss: What You're Carrying

Goal: Name the specific weight of suicide grief so you can begin to set some of it down.

Prompts:
- Write the name of the person you lost. When did they die? What was your relationship?
- What's the hardest thought you carry about their death? The one that loops? Write it out exactly.
- What emotion hits hardest: guilt, anger, confusion, abandonment, relief, or something else? No wrong answers.
- What have people said to you about the loss that made it worse? What do you wish they'd said instead?
- What part of your daily life changed most after the loss? What's still different?
- Where do you feel most defended, hidden, or on alert in your current environment?

## Worksheet 2: Grief After Suicide Loss: One Step Forward

Goal: Build one small, concrete action toward processing this grief without forcing resolution.

Prompts:
- One thing I need to say to the person I lost: even though they can't hear it: ___.
- One person in my life who I can talk to about this without being managed or fixed: ___. I'll reach out by: ___.
- The guilt thought I carry most is: ___. The truth I know underneath it is: ___.
- One way I'll honor their memory this week that feels right to me (not performative): ___.
- If the grief gets bigger than I can manage alone, I'll take this step: ___ (call a grief counselor, attend a survivors of suicide loss group, call 988).

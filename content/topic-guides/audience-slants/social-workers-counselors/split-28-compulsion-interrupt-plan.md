## What This Helps With

The thing you keep doing: checking, counting, cleaning, repeating: isn't random. It's a coping loop. Here's how to interrupt it without white-knuckling.
This version is written for helpers whose job is to hold pain, track systems, stay regulated for others, and still have a private human nervous system of their own.

## What Is Happening

Helping professions make it easy to intellectualize your own distress, normalize overload, or keep functioning because you know how to sound okay. But insight isn't the same as relief. Secondary trauma, compassion fatigue, and system strain can make your own symptoms harder to admit.

Compulsions are anxiety's escape hatch. The brain sends a "something is wrong" signal, and the compulsion temporarily turns it off: checking, counting, cleaning, repeating, arranging. The relief is real but short-lived, and each cycle of action-and-relief wires the loop tighter.

This isn't about willpower. Your nervous system has learned that the compulsion = safety. The interrupt isn't stopping the behavior: it's teaching your nervous system that not doing the thing is survivable.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask where your helper training is helping and where it's actually making it easier to bypass your own needs.
2. Notice the urge without acting on it. Set a timer for 2 minutes and sit with the discomfort.
3. Rate the anxiety from 1–10 before you give in. Then wait. Watch it peak and drop without doing the thing.
4. Replace with one redirect: walk, breathe, text someone, change rooms.

## What To Say

- I know the language of this, and I still need real support for my own life.
- Insight isn't enough. I need action and recovery too.
- I can feel the urge and I'm going to wait 2 minutes before I decide to act on it.
- The anxiety wants me to ______ but I know the relief is temporary.
- I'm not crazy. This is a pattern and I'm learning to interrupt it.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Confusing professional self-awareness with actual personal recovery.
- Going cold turkey on all compulsions at once: target one at a time.
- Thinking the anxiety will keep climbing forever if you don't do the thing: it peaks and drops.
- White-knuckling it alone instead of getting professional support (ERP therapy works for this).

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: notice the urge without acting on it. Set a timer for two minutes and wait.
- Before bed tonight: write down the compulsion cycle: trigger → urge → action → temporary relief → guilt. See the loop.
- Tomorrow: when the urge comes, replace the action with a 90-second redirect: walk, breathe, text someone. The urge will still pass.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits social workers / counselors life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the compulsion cycle: trigger, urge, behavior, relief, repeat.

Prompts:
- What's the compulsion: checking, counting, cleaning, repeating, arranging, something else?
- What triggers it: a specific thought, situation, feeling, or "something just feels wrong"?
- What happens if you don't do it? What does your brain tell you will happen?
- How many times do you do it before the anxiety drops? Is it getting more or less?
- What has the compulsion cost you (time, relationships, energy, sanity)?
- How does your role as a helper make this pattern easier to hide or explain away?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Practice one delay and one redirect.

Prompts:
- The compulsion I'll target first: ______. My delay: ______ minutes before acting.
- My redirect when the urge hits (walk, breathe, call someone, change rooms): ______.
- My anxiety rating before waiting: ______/10. After 5 minutes of waiting: ______/10.
- One step toward professional support if this is ruling my day (ERP therapist, OCD specialist):
- By this weekend, I'll know this worked if:

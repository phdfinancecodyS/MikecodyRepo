## What This Helps With

It's 2am and your brain just opened every file it has. If the nighttime spiral is stealing your sleep and your sanity, this is the interrupt plan.
This version is written for helpers whose job is to hold pain, track systems, stay regulated for others, and still have a private human nervous system of their own.

## What Is Happening

Helping professions make it easy to intellectualize your own distress, normalize overload, or keep functioning because you know how to sound okay. But insight isn't the same as relief. Secondary trauma, compassion fatigue, and system strain can make your own symptoms harder to admit.

Sleep disruption turns up reactivity, worry, and decision fatigue fast.
Most people chase a perfect night. The better move is a repeatable wind-down pattern.
Small consistency beats occasional heroic fixes.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask where your helper training is helping and where it's actually making it easier to bypass your own needs.
2. Pick one fixed anchor time for wind-down or wake-up.
3. Run a 20-minute downshift routine without screens.
4. Write tomorrow's first task so your brain has a landing point.

## What To Say

- I know the language of this, and I still need real support for my own life.
- Insight isn't enough. I need action and recovery too.
- I'm running on low sleep and I need to simplify tonight.
- I'm doing my reset routine now so tomorrow is easier.
- I'm not fixing everything tonight; I'm building one better night.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Confusing professional self-awareness with actual personal recovery.
- Using late-night scrolling as your off switch. Screens keep your brain in input mode and push the spiral later, not away.
- Trying to solve the thoughts at 2am. Your brain doesn't have access to perspective or proportion at that hour. Write it down and revisit in daylight.
- Changing your whole routine every night hoping one will stick. Pick one wind-down sequence and repeat it for a week before judging it.
- Lying in bed fighting the spiral for hours. If you're not asleep in 20 minutes, get up, go somewhere dim, and do something boring until drowsiness returns.
- Ignoring caffeine timing. If your last cup is after 2pm, that's a likely contributor. Caffeine's half-life is 5–7 hours.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: put a pen and paper next to your bed. When the spiral starts tonight, write the thoughts down instead of chasing them.
- Before bed tonight: set a hard screen cutoff 60 minutes before sleep.
- Tomorrow: review what you wrote. Most of it won't look as urgent in daylight: and that's the point.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits social workers / counselors life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the spiral: what your brain opens at 2am and why it won't close.

Prompts:
- What's the recurring thought that shows up after dark? Write the exact loop.
- When the spiral starts, does it land on worry, regret, planning, or dread?
- What time does it usually start? What were you doing right before?
- What have you tried to stop it? (scrolling, drinking, counting, fighting it) What actually worked?
- How many hours of sleep are you averaging? What does the next day feel like?
- How does your role as a helper make this pattern easier to hide or explain away?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Build a night protocol that interrupts the cycle before your brain takes over.

Prompts:
- One thing I'll put by the bed to dump the thoughts (journal, notepad, voice memo):
- My screen cutoff time tonight ______. My replacement activity: ______.
- The sentence I'll say when the spiral starts: "That's the spiral. I'll deal with this at ______ tomorrow."
- One wind-down action I'll do every night for 7 nights (shower, stretch, tea, breathing):
- By this weekend, I'll know this worked if:

## What This Helps With

You're not drinking to celebrate. You're drinking to stop feeling. This isn't a sobriety lecture: it's an honest look at the pattern and one way to interrupt it.
This version is written for military and veteran life, where mission, hypervigilance, identity, loss of tribe, and carrying the past all shape how a problem lands.

## What Is Happening

Military life trains your body and mind around vigilance, control, performance, and mission. Even after service, those patterns don't disappear just because the environment changed. A lot of the struggle isn't weakness. It's training, injury, loss, and identity still showing up in civilian life.

You're not drinking because you enjoy it: you're drinking because something hurts and alcohol makes it quieter. The pattern usually starts with a feeling you don't want to sit with: stress, emptiness, anger, sadness. The drink turns the volume down. But the thing that hurts is still there in the morning, plus now you feel worse. The cycle isn't about willpower. It's about what the alcohol is managing for you.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this reaction belongs to your current environment or to training and survival patterns that are still firing after the mission changed.
2. Before the next drink, pause and name what you're actually feeling right now. Not "stressed": the real thing underneath.
3. Decide: am I drinking to enjoy this, or to stop feeling something?
4. Replace the first drink with one other action for 10 minutes: a walk, a call, a glass of water. Then decide.

## What To Say

- Part of this is service-related, and I'm not going to shame myself for the fact that it still shows up.
- I need support that respects the way training and loss changed me.
- I think I'm drinking to numb something and I want to be honest about that.
- I'm going to pause before this one and see what I'm actually feeling.
- I need to talk to someone about this pattern. It's getting harder to manage alone.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Expecting civilian routines, relationships, or workplaces to make sense without accounting for service, transition, or loss of tribe.
- Quitting cold turkey with no plan for what to do with the feelings that surface. Alcohol was managing something: you need a replacement, not just a void.
- Setting a "never drink again" rule on a bad morning, then breaking it by Friday and calling yourself a failure.
- Drinking alone and convincing yourself it's different from drinking with people. It's not: it's quieter, which makes it easier to hide.
- Waiting until it's "bad enough" to count as a real problem. If you're reading this, it's already costing you something.
- Using willpower as your only strategy. Willpower is finite. Structure, routine, and honesty with one other person work better.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: pour the first one and pause. Ask yourself: am I drinking to enjoy this or to stop feeling something?
- Before bed tonight: write down what you were trying to numb. Be honest.
- Tomorrow: replace the numbing time with one other activity: a walk, a call, anything that isn't the bottle.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits military / veteran life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the numbing pattern: when you drink, what you're avoiding, and what it costs.

Prompts:
- When do you reach for the drink: after work, after conflict, after the kids are down, when you're alone?
- What feeling are you trying to turn off? (Anxiety, anger, sadness, boredom, emptiness?)
- How much are you drinking now vs. six months ago? Be honest.
- What does the next morning feel like: and what do you tell yourself about it?
- Who has noticed? What did they say? What did you say back?
- Where does training still help you, and where is it creating friction in civilian life or close relationships?

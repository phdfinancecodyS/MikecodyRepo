## What This Helps With

If your brain is running the same three worst moments on loop and telling you that you're the problem: read this. Shame lies, and it's loud. Here's how to turn the volume down.
This version is written for military and veteran life, where mission, hypervigilance, identity, loss of tribe, and carrying the past all shape how a problem lands.

## What Is Happening

Military life trains your body and mind around vigilance, control, performance, and mission. Even after service, those patterns don't disappear just because the environment changed. A lot of the struggle isn't weakness. It's training, injury, loss, and identity still showing up in civilian life.

Shame doesn't feel like emotion. It feels like fact. You're the problem. You're bad at the core. Everything you did wrong proves it. Your brain runs the same three memories on loop, each one confirming you're fundamentally flawed. This isn't truth; this is a thought-feeling trap.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this reaction belongs to your current environment or to training and survival patterns that are still firing after the mission changed.
2. Write down the three shame memories that loop most.
3. For each one, write what a person who loves you would say about what actually happened vs. what your shame tells you.
4. When shame loops, say out loud: This is shame talking, not fact.

## What To Say

- Part of this is service-related, and I'm not going to shame myself for the fact that it still shows up.
- I need support that respects the way training and loss changed me.
- I'm in a shame spiral and I need to break the loop.
- I know this feeling means I did something I regret, and I'm not a bad person because of it.
- I'm going to tell [trusted person] what happened because shame dies in isolation.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Expecting civilian routines, relationships, or workplaces to make sense without accounting for service, transition, or loss of tribe.
- Treating shame like proof instead of one part of the story.
- Isolating with guilt instead of saying the truth out loud somewhere safe.
- Trying to rush forgiveness before honest accountability and repair.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: write down the three shame memories your brain keeps looping. Get them out of your head and onto paper.
- Before bed tonight: for each one, write what someone who loves you would actually say about what happened.
- Tomorrow: when the loop starts, say out loud: "That's shame talking, not fact." Then text one person and tell them one true thing.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits military / veteran life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern: when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- What are the three memories your brain loops most when shame takes over?
- What does shame tell you about yourself? Write it word for word.
- Who do you avoid when you're in the spiral?
- What's the difference between guilt (I did a bad thing) and shame (I am bad) in your head?
- When's the last time you told someone the truth about what you're carrying?
- Where does training still help you, and where is it creating friction in civilian life or close relationships?

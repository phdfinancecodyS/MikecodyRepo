## What This Helps With

The house is a wreck and it feels like proof that you're failing. It's not. Here's how to reset the space without needing a full life makeover.
This version is written for military and veteran life, where mission, hypervigilance, identity, loss of tribe, and carrying the past all shape how a problem lands.

## What Is Happening

Military life trains your body and mind around vigilance, control, performance, and mission. Even after service, those patterns don't disappear just because the environment changed. A lot of the struggle isn't weakness. It's training, injury, loss, and identity still showing up in civilian life.

The state of your space is a mirror of what's happening inside you. When the house is chaos, it's usually not about the dishes: it's about depletion, overwhelm, depression, or a system that stopped working months ago. The mess creates shame, the shame creates avoidance, the avoidance makes the mess worse.

You don't need a deep clean. You need one clear surface and the permission to start small.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this reaction belongs to your current environment or to training and survival patterns that are still firing after the mission changed.
2. Pick one surface: a counter, a table, a desk: and clear it. That's it.
3. Put three things back where they belong. Not a deep clean. Just three things.
4. Tomorrow: one room, 15 minutes. Timer on, timer off.

## What To Say

- Part of this is service-related, and I'm not going to shame myself for the fact that it still shows up.
- I need support that respects the way training and loss changed me.
- The house is a wreck and I can't do it all right now. I'm starting with this one thing.
- I need help. Can you handle ______ while I handle ______?
- The mess isn't me. I'm just behind right now.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Expecting civilian routines, relationships, or workplaces to make sense without accounting for service, transition, or loss of tribe.
- Trying to clean the whole house in one burst and burning out by room two.
- Using the mess as proof that you're a failure: it's a symptom, not a verdict.
- Waiting for motivation to clean when motivation comes AFTER starting, not before.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: pick one surface: a counter, a table, a desk: and clear it. That's it.
- Before bed tonight: put three things back where they belong. Not a deep clean: just three things.
- Tomorrow: do one room for 15 minutes. Timer on, timer off. Stop when it rings.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits military / veteran life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Understand the connection between the chaos outside and what's happening inside.

Prompts:
- How bad is it right now: honestly? What areas are worst?
- When did the house start slipping? What else was happening in your life at that time?
- Is the mess about laziness (probably not) or depletion (probably yes)?
- What does the mess make you feel: shame, overwhelm, numbness, anger, nothing?
- What would one clean room do for your mental state right now?
- Where does training still help you, and where is it creating friction in civilian life or close relationships?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Clear one space. Feel the difference.

Prompts:
- The one surface I'll clear right now: ______.
- Tomorrow's 15-minute room: ______. Timer starts at ______.
- One system I'll put in place (evening reset, dish rule, laundry schedule): ______.
- Help I'll ask for: "Can you handle ______ this week while I handle ______?"
- By this weekend, I'll know this worked if:

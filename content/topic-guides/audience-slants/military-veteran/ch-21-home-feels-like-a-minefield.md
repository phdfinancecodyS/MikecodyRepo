## What This Helps With

When your own home doesn't feel safe: when you're walking on eggshells or the tension never lifts: here's what to do. Not everything. Just the next thing.
This version is written for military and veteran life, where mission, hypervigilance, identity, loss of tribe, and carrying the past all shape how a problem lands.

## What Is Happening

Military life trains your body and mind around vigilance, control, performance, and mission. Even after service, those patterns don't disappear just because the environment changed. A lot of the struggle isn't weakness. It's training, injury, loss, and identity still showing up in civilian life.

Your home is a minefield because you're hypervigilant about everyone's emotional state, tone of voice, and energy. You're reading micro-expressions and anticipating conflict before it starts. You feel responsible for everyone's mood. Living here's exhausting.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this reaction belongs to your current environment or to training and survival patterns that are still firing after the mission changed.
2. Pick one person or family member and practice: I don't control their emotional state.
3. Set a boundary: If there's yelling or threat, I'm leaving the room for 20 minutes.
4. Create one safe zone in your home where you can go to reset without negotiating.

## What To Say

- Part of this is service-related, and I'm not going to shame myself for the fact that it still shows up.
- I need support that respects the way training and loss changed me.
- I'm reading threat here and I need to pause and check reality.
- I'm not responsible for managing everyone's emotions.
- When I feel the minefield response coming, I'm taking 20 minutes in [safe space].

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Expecting civilian routines, relationships, or workplaces to make sense without accounting for service, transition, or loss of tribe.
- Walking on eggshells and calling it "keeping the peace" when it's actually hypervigilance.
- Taking responsibility for everyone else's emotional state instead of managing your own.
- Changing everything about the environment instead of teaching your nervous system that home is different from the field.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: identify one room or corner that feels safest and spend 10 minutes there doing nothing.
- Before bed tonight: write down the three biggest tension points in the house right now. Just name them.
- Tomorrow: pick one tension point and address it: not all of it, just the first sentence of the conversation.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits military / veteran life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the minefield: what triggers you at home and what you're actually scanning for.

Prompts:
- Which room or situation at home creates the most tension?
- What are you scanning for: someone's mood, tone of voice, footsteps, silence?
- How does your body feel when you walk through the door? Tight, braced, exhausted?
- Who in the home do you monitor most? What are you afraid will happen?
- When's the last time you fully relaxed at home without being on alert?
- Where does training still help you, and where is it creating friction in civilian life or close relationships?

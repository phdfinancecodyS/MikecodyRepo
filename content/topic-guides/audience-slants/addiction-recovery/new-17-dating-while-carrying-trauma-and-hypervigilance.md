## What This Helps With

You want to date but your nervous system treats every new person like a threat assessment. Here's how to show up without armor: or at least with less of it.
This version is written for people dealing with addiction, compulsive coping, sobriety, recovery, relapse fear, secrecy, and the work of building a life that doesn't depend on numbing.

## What Is Happening

Addiction and recovery change how mental health problems feel because the urge to numb, escape, control, or disappear is always close by. Even if substances or compulsions aren't the main issue today, recovery thinking, shame, secrecy, and relapse fear can still shape the problem hard.

Trauma and hypervigilance make dating harder because you're reading threat in normal relationship moments. You may isolate, push people away, or move too fast seeking safety. You aren't broken for this. You need to pace differently and name what's happening so your person can understand.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this issue is waking up urges to numb, isolate, hide, or tell yourself one slip doesn't matter.
2. Tell the person early and clearly: I've been through something difficult and I'm managing some hypervigilance.
3. Name one specific way it shows up for you when stressed (withdrawing, testing, asking for reassurance a lot).
4. Ask them to be patient with the pattern and tell you when they notice it.

## What To Say

- This is the kind of moment that can push me toward old coping, so I need to move early.
- I don't need to handle this alone or in secret.
- I carry some trauma and sometimes that shows up as [specific pattern]. It isn't about you.
- When I do this, here's what helps: [what actually helps]. Here's what makes it worse: [what triggers].
- I want this to work and I'm managing my stuff. Bear with me.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Waiting until the urge is loud before using support, structure, or accountability.
- Testing people without telling them you're testing. If you set traps to see if they'll leave, they eventually will: and it won't be proof you're unlovable, it'll be proof the test worked.
- Dumping your entire trauma history on date two. Disclosure should be paced. Share what's relevant when trust has been earned, not as a way to fast-track intimacy.
- Moving too fast because connection feels like safety. Trauma makes intensity feel like love. Slow down: real safety is boring at first.
- Isolating instead of dating because it's "easier." Avoiding vulnerability protects you from pain, but it also blocks connection.
- Assuming every partner will eventually hurt you like the last one did. That's hypervigilance talking, not reality. Name it so it doesn't run the show.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: notice how your body feels when you think about dating. Where's the tension?
- Before bed tonight: write down what you want from a partner: not what you should want, what you actually want.
- Tomorrow: do one brave thing. Respond to the message. Go on the date. Say one honest thing.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits addiction / recovery life.

## Worksheet 1: Dating and Hypervigilance Self-Assessment

Goal: Name how trauma and hypervigilance show up in your dating life so you can manage the pattern instead of being run by it.

Prompts:
- When you meet someone new, what does your body do? (scan for exits, analyze their every word, feel nothing, feel everything at once?)
- What's your pattern? Do you move too fast, pull away, test people, overshare, or ghost? Name it honestly.
- What's the fear underneath the pattern? (abandonment, being seen, being trapped, being hurt again, not being enough?)
- Describe the last time hypervigilance ruined or damaged a dating moment. What happened? What were you actually reacting to versus what you thought you were reacting to?
- What does "safe" feel like to you? Can you describe it: or has it been so long that you're not sure?
- What thoughts, feelings, or situations around this problem make you want to numb out, hide, or go back to old coping?

## Worksheet 2: Dating Brave Plan

Goal: Build a concrete plan for one brave dating action this week that honors both your healing and your desire for connection.

Prompts:
- One thing I'll tell the next person I date about how my trauma shows up: "Sometimes I ___ and it's not about you. Here's what helps: ___." Practice saying it.
- The hypervigilance pattern I'll watch for this week: ___. When I notice it, I'll: ___ (name it internally, take a breath, stay instead of bolting).
- One brave dating action I'll take this week: ___ (respond to the message, go on the date, say one honest thing, let someone see me).
- My boundary I'll hold: ___. My boundary I'll soften: ___.
- The person I'll talk to about my dating fears: ___. What I'll say: "I want to date but my nervous system makes it hard. Can I talk through this with you?"

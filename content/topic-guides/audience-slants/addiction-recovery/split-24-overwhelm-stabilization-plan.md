## What This Helps With

Everything needs attention and you can't move. Not lazy: overloaded. Here's how to pick one thing and start without needing to fix it all.
This version is written for people dealing with addiction, compulsive coping, sobriety, recovery, relapse fear, secrecy, and the work of building a life that doesn't depend on numbing.

## What Is Happening

Addiction and recovery change how mental health problems feel because the urge to numb, escape, control, or disappear is always close by. Even if substances or compulsions aren't the main issue today, recovery thinking, shame, secrecy, and relapse fear can still shape the problem hard.

Overwhelm isn't about having too much to do: it's about your brain losing the ability to prioritize. Everything feels equally urgent and equally impossible. So you freeze. You stop moving. And the pile grows, which makes the overwhelm worse, which makes you freeze harder.

This is a nervous system response, not a character flaw. When threat signals overwhelm your executive function, your brain stops being able to sort "urgent" from "important" from "can wait." The result is paralysis that looks like laziness but feels like drowning.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this issue is waking up urges to numb, isolate, hide, or tell yourself one slip doesn't matter.
2. Stop trying to see the whole pile. Pick ONE thing from it. Do that thing.
3. Write the three things that feel most urgent. Cross out the one that can actually wait.
4. Do the remaining two: one before noon, one after. That's the whole plan.

## What To Say

- This is the kind of moment that can push me toward old coping, so I need to move early.
- I don't need to handle this alone or in secret.
- I can't do all of this. I'm going to do one thing and then decide what's next.
- Everything feels urgent right now and I need help sorting it.
- I'm not lazy. I'm overloaded and I'm starting with one thing.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Waiting until the urge is loud before using support, structure, or accountability.
- Trying to fix everything at once, which guarantees you'll finish nothing.
- Making a massive to-do list that becomes its own source of overwhelm.
- Waiting until the panic passes to start: it won't pass until you start.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: pick one thing from the pile. Just one. Do it, then stop.
- Before bed tonight: write the three things that feel most urgent. Cross out the one that can actually wait.
- Tomorrow: do the remaining two. One before noon, one after. That's the whole day.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits addiction / recovery life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map the overwhelm: what's in the pile and what's actually urgent.

Prompts:
- List everything that feels urgent right now. All of it. Get it out of your head.
- Now circle the three things that actually have a deadline or consequence this week.
- What are you avoiding that's making the pile bigger?
- When did the overwhelm start? Was there a tipping point?
- What would "managed" (not perfect) look like right now?
- What thoughts, feelings, or situations around this problem make you want to numb out, hide, or go back to old coping?

## Worksheet 2: Action Builder

Goal: Do one thing. Then decide the next one.

Prompts:
- The one thing I'll do first right now: ______. It'll take ______ minutes.
- The two things I'll do tomorrow (one before noon, one after): ______ and ______.
- One thing I'll cancel, delegate, or let wait: ______.
- One person I'll tell "I'm overwhelmed" to: ______.
- By tomorrow night, I'll know this worked if:

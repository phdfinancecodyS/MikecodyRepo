## What This Helps With

You spent four hours on your phone and feel worse than when you started. This is the recovery plan: because the scroll isn't just wasting time, it's wrecking you.
This version is written for people dealing with addiction, compulsive coping, sobriety, recovery, relapse fear, secrecy, and the work of building a life that doesn't depend on numbing.

## What Is Happening

Addiction and recovery change how mental health problems feel because the urge to numb, escape, control, or disappear is always close by. Even if substances or compulsions aren't the main issue today, recovery thinking, shame, secrecy, and relapse fear can still shape the problem hard.

Doom-scrolling isn't a moral failing. It's your nervous system's way of managing anxiety by staying hyper-aware of threat. But it keeps your threat system locked on, which makes anxiety worse. Breaking the cycle means replacing the compulsion with something that actually calms your nervous system.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this issue is waking up urges to numb, isolate, hide, or tell yourself one slip doesn't matter.
2. Delete apps or move them to a screen folder so they aren't one-tap access.
3. When you want to scroll, do one 60-second body-based alternative instead (walk, shake, breathe, cold water).
4. At night, put your phone in another room one hour before bed.

## What To Say

- This is the kind of moment that can push me toward old coping, so I need to move early.
- I don't need to handle this alone or in secret.
- I'm using my phone as an anxiety management tool and it's making anxiety worse.
- I'm breaking the one-tap access so I've time to choose something better.
- The first few days are hardest and then my nervous system recalibrates.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Waiting until the urge is loud before using support, structure, or accountability.
- Deleting apps in a moment of frustration and reinstalling them 24 hours later. That's a cycle, not a fix.
- Blaming the phone instead of understanding what you're scrolling to avoid. The trigger matters more than the screen.
- Going on a "digital detox weekend" and bingeing twice as hard on Monday. Sustainable boundaries beat dramatic gestures.
- Using Screen Time limits you tap through every time. If the override is easier than the limit, the limit isn't working.
- Treating one good day as proof you're fixed and one bad day as proof you're hopeless. This is a pattern: patterns shift over weeks, not hours.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: set your most-used app to a 30-minute daily limit. Just one app.
- Before bed tonight: charge your phone outside the bedroom tonight. Not next to the bed: another room.
- Tomorrow: replace your first 15 minutes of scrolling with something that uses your hands: coffee, stretching, cooking. Retrain the morning.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits addiction / recovery life.

## Worksheet 1: Doom-Scroll Pattern Tracker

Goal: Map when, where, and why you scroll so you can interrupt the cycle at the right point.

Prompts:
- When do you doom-scroll most? (morning in bed, after work, can't sleep, during breaks?) List your top three scroll windows.
- What are you usually feeling right before you pick up the phone? (anxious, bored, lonely, wired, avoiding something?)
- How long does a typical scroll session last? How do you feel after: better, worse, or numb?
- What apps or content do you gravitate to? What specifically hooks you: news, outrage, comparison, true crime, feeds?
- What has the scrolling cost you? (sleep, time with people, focus, mood, productivity?) Be specific about what you've lost.
- What thoughts, feelings, or situations around this problem make you want to numb out, hide, or go back to old coping?

## Worksheet 2: Scroll Replacement Plan

Goal: Build three concrete alternatives to scrolling that actually work for your nervous system.

Prompts:
- My #1 scroll trigger is: ___. When I notice that trigger, instead of picking up my phone, I'll: ___ (60-second body reset: walk, cold water, stretch).
- My phone setup change this week: ___ (move apps to second screen, delete one app, set a 30-minute daily timer on one app).
- My new bedtime protocol: Phone goes in ___ (specific room/location) at ___ pm. I'll do ___ instead for the last hour.
- My morning replacement: Instead of scrolling for the first 15 minutes, I'll ___ (make coffee, stretch, go outside, anything with my hands).
- By this time next week, I'll know this is working if: ___.

## What This Helps With

When a grocery store feels like a combat zone and normal noise hits like an assault: that's your nervous system, not weakness. Here's how to get through it.
This version is written for people dealing with addiction, compulsive coping, sobriety, recovery, relapse fear, secrecy, and the work of building a life that doesn't depend on numbing.

## What Is Happening

Addiction and recovery change how mental health problems feel because the urge to numb, escape, control, or disappear is always close by. Even if substances or compulsions aren't the main issue today, recovery thinking, shame, secrecy, and relapse fear can still shape the problem hard.

Crowds and public spaces trigger your nervous system because you can't predict threat from many directions at once. Loud noises startle you. Close bodies press against your defenses. You can't see exits clearly. Your nervous system locks into threat-scan mode and exhausts you fast.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this issue is waking up urges to numb, isolate, hide, or tell yourself one slip doesn't matter.
2. Plan public outings with an exit route already known (aisle seat, door nearest you, clear path out).
3. Go to the same places repeatedly so your nervous system can downregulate recognition.
4. Wear noise-dampening headphones or earplugs to reduce sensory overload.

## What To Say

- This is the kind of moment that can push me toward old coping, so I need to move early.
- I don't need to handle this alone or in secret.
- I need to be positioned where I can see an exit.
- Going to familiar places helps my nervous system feel safer.
- I'll take breaks when sensory overload hits.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Waiting until the urge is loud before using support, structure, or accountability.
- Ignoring physical warning signs until they take over the whole day.
- Pushing through symptoms as if force will solve a nervous-system problem.
- Treating body symptoms like a character problem instead of a signal.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: identify one sensory tool that helps (earplugs, sunglasses, a grounding object in your pocket).
- Before bed tonight: plan one outing for tomorrow with a time limit and an exit plan.
- Tomorrow: go, use the tool, leave when you hit your limit: no guilt. Endurance comes later.
- Audience-lens support step: use one support, person, community, or routine that fits addiction / recovery life.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Map your sensory triggers: what overwhelms you and when.

Prompts:
- Which environments hit hardest: grocery stores, restaurants, traffic, events?
- What's the first sensory signal that you're overloading (sound, light, closeness, heat)?
- How long can you usually tolerate a crowded space before you need to leave?
- What do you do when you can't leave: shut down, dissociate, get irritable?
- What tool or habit has helped even slightly (earplugs, sunglasses, aisle seat, exit plan)?
- What thoughts, feelings, or situations around this problem make you want to numb out, hide, or go back to old coping?

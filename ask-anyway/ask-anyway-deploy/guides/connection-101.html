Connection 101

What to Say When You're Worried About Someone

You've noticed something's off. Maybe they've been quieter, canceling plans, or said something that didn't quite sit right. This guide walks you through exactly what to say - and what not to say.

✍️
Written by a Licensed Clinical Social Worker and someone who lost a best friend to suicide - because the world is full of people who care deeply but feel completely frozen around this one conversation. That's not a character flaw. It's a skill gap. Skills can be learned.
Step 1

The Problem (You Already Know This One)

Your gut says something's wrong. And then your brain says: What if I ask and make it worse? What if I say the wrong thing? What if I'm overreacting?

So you don't ask. You tell yourself you'll bring it up later. Or you drop a vague text. Or you mention it to someone else instead.

Here's what we know: In almost every case where someone dies by suicide, people around them saw warning signs they didn't know how to approach. Not because they didn't care. Because they didn't know how.
Step 2

Why Being Indirect Doesn't Work

When we're scared, we soften the conversation. We hope they'll pick up what we're putting down. We ask "Are you okay?" and hope they'll volunteer the truth.

That's not how this works. People who are struggling are expert at saying "I'm fine." They've practiced it. It's the default away-message for pain.

The indirect approach gives people an out. You need a different strategy. You need to ask directly.

Step 3

The Words - Ready to Use

You'll use three sets of words. Here they are.

Opening the door

Find a private moment. Sit. Look at them. "I've noticed you seem [different / withdrawn / not yourself] lately, and I care about you. I want to ask you something, and I need you to know I'm asking because I care, not because I'm judging. Can I ask you something real?"

Wait for them to nod or say yes.

The direct ask

"Are you thinking about suicide?"

Or if that feels impossible:
"Are you thinking about hurting yourself?"
"Have you thought about ending your life?"

If they say yes

Your only job is to not end the conversation. Stay.

"Thank you for telling me. I'm really glad you did. I'm not going anywhere. Can you tell me more about what's been going on?"

Then listen. Don't try to fix. Don't argue. Just listen and stay present.
Step 4

What Kills the Conversation

Here's what NOT to say - and why it backfires.

What you might say What actually works
"You have so much to live for" "Tell me what's been making you feel like this"
"You should just talk to someone" "I'm here. Can we figure out professional support together?"
"Other people have it worse" Say nothing. Just listen.
"I had no idea you felt this way" "I'm really glad you told me."
"Promise me you won't do anything" "I want to help you stay safe. Can we talk about that?"
"Maybe you just need to think positive" Stay silent and listen.
The pattern: The things that feel helpful are often the things that stop the conversation. The things that actually help are: staying present, validating their experience, and listening without trying to solve it immediately.
Step 5

If They Confirm - Your Next 3 Steps

1
Stay and listen - don't transfer responsibility
Your job is NOT to be their therapist. Your job is to not end the conversation and help them connect to professional support. Ask: "How long have you been feeling this way? What's been happening?" Let them talk.
2
Understand how serious it is
You don't need to diagnose anything. Ask: "Do you have a plan for how you would do it?" and "Are you planning to act on this soon?" Their answers tell you whether this is today-level or this-week-level urgency.
3
Connect them to help - together
Don't just say "call this number." If it's active crisis, call 988 together from your phone, or go to the nearest ER together. Don't leave them alone. If it's ongoing struggle, help them make the call or appointment. Check in tomorrow. Check in next week.

Closing

You're Not Responsible for Saving Them

You asked. You stayed. You listened. You helped them connect to help. That took courage.

You are not responsible for making them better. Your job was to have a real conversation and help them connect to professional support.

But your conversation mattered. From here, the actual healing work can begin.

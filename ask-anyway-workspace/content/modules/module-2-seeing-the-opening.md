# MODULE 2 — Seeing the Opening: When and How to Initiate

## Why This Module Exists

Module 1 broke down why you freeze. You looked at the myths. You felt the resistance. And you decided: *I'm done letting fear run the show.*

**Good. But deciding to ask isn't the same as knowing when to ask.**

Because timing matters. Context matters. Where you are, who else is around, what your face looks like when you say it, whether they just walked in from a terrible shift or just sat down with a beer — all of it matters.

This module teaches you to see the opening. To recognize it. To create it when one doesn't exist naturally. And to set up the moment so the person across from you has the best possible chance of being honest with you.

---

## WHAT YOU'RE ACTUALLY LOOKING FOR

### Warning signs aren't flashing red lights. They're whispers.

Most people think a person who's suicidal will do something dramatic. They'll announce it. They'll leave clues like a movie character. They'll write a note or make a grand goodbye.

**That almost never happens.**

What actually happens is quieter. More subtle. More ordinary. And that's exactly why people miss it.

Here's the thing most people get wrong: **Warning signs aren't signs that someone's about to die. They're invitations to be asked.**

When someone pulls away, cancels plans, stops texting back, starts giving things away, makes a comment about being a burden — they're not writing a screenplay. They're communicating the only way they know how.

**Your job is to hear the whisper before it becomes a scream.**

---

## THE THREE TYPES OF OPENINGS

### Opening Type 1: They say something

Verbal cues are the most direct. But they're easy to miss because they rarely sound like what you expect.

**What you might hear:**

- "I don't think I can do this anymore."
- "What's the point?"
- "Everyone would be better off without me."
- "I'm so tired of everything."
- "I just want it to stop."
- "You won't have to worry about me much longer."
- "I wish I could just disappear."

**What most people do when they hear this:** Change the subject. Laugh nervously. Say "don't talk like that." Assume it's just venting.

**What you should do:** File it. You just got an invitation. Maybe not for right this second — but it happened. If you hear anything in that list, treat it like the person just handed you a note that says *I need someone to ask me the real question.*

---

**Less obvious verbal cues:**

- Sudden interest in what happens after death, afterlife talk, philosophical comments about life being meaningless
- Talking about being a burden — "You'd be better off," "I'm just dragging everyone down"
- Making arrangements — "If anything ever happens to me, I want you to have this"
- Dark humor about dying — "At least I won't have to deal with this much longer" followed by a laugh that doesn't reach their eyes
- Saying goodbye differently — texts that feel final, calls that feel like closure

**These aren't just "dark moods."** These are someone testing whether anyone's paying attention.

---

### Opening Type 2: Their behavior changes

This one's harder because it requires you to actually know what someone's baseline looks like.

**Behavioral shifts to notice:**

- **Withdrawal.** They stop showing up. Cancel plans. Stop returning calls. Go quiet on group chats. Disappear from their usual routines.
- **Sleep changes.** Sleeping way more than usual or almost not at all. You notice dark circles. They mention being exhausted even after a day off.
- **Giving things away.** Not spring cleaning — this is handing over possessions that matter to them. Guitars, watches, favorite books. If someone gives away something they love with a calm detachment, that's a red flag.
- **Recklessness.** Driving too fast. Drinking more. Taking risks they wouldn't normally take. This can look like "they're just blowing off steam" — sometimes it's someone who's stopped caring about their own safety.
- **Sudden calm after a rough stretch.** This one catches people off guard. Someone who's been miserable for weeks suddenly seems peaceful, lighter, resolved. This can mean they've made a decision. And that calm isn't healing — it's relief that the struggle will be over.
- **Loss of interest.** Things they used to care about — hobbies, kids' events, the gym, their job — suddenly don't register. Not "I'm busy" — more like "it doesn't matter."

**The key question to ask yourself:** *Is this different from how they usually are?*

If the answer is yes — and especially if multiple things on this list are happening at once — you've got an opening.

---

### Opening Type 3: The situation creates it

Sometimes the opening isn't about what they said or did. It's about what happened to them.

**Situations that create risk windows:**

- **A major loss.** Death of a spouse, child, colleague. End of a relationship. Losing a job. Losing custody.
- **A professional crisis.** Under investigation. Facing a lawsuit. Suspended. Forcibly transferred. Given a career-ending diagnosis.
- **A health crisis.** Chronic pain diagnosis. TBI. Loss of mobility or independence. A diagnosis that changes their self-image.
- **Anniversary dates.** The anniversary of a partner's death. The date of a traumatic incident. Holidays when the family was still together.
- **Transition.** Retirement. Separation from military service. Kids leaving home. Moving to a new city with no support network.
- **Exposure to another person's suicide.** Hearing about a colleague, friend, or family member who died by suicide. Contagion is real and well-documented.
- **Access.** If someone who's struggling has access to lethal means — firearms in the home, medications, equipment — the risk goes up. Not because of intent, but because impulsive moments become lethal when the means are close.

**You don't need all of these.** One or two, combined with a gut feeling that something's off, is enough.

## WARNING SIGNS vs. RISK FACTORS (Why This Matters)

People mix these up all the time. Here's the practical difference:

**Risk factors** are background conditions. They increase the likelihood over time.
- History of mental illness
- Previous attempts
- Family history of suicide
- Chronic pain or illness
- Access to lethal means
- Substance use

**Warning signs** are changes you can see right now. They tell you something's happening.
- Talking about being a burden
- Withdrawing from people
- Giving away possessions
- Sudden calm after depression
- Increased substance use
- Sleep disruption
- Saying goodbye

**Why do you care about this difference?**

Because risk factors tell you who to pay attention to. Warning signs tell you *when* to act.

Someone might have risk factors for years and be fine. But when you see warning signs *on top of* risk factors — that's when you step in.

**Don't be the person who memorizes risk factor lists and misses the actual warning signs happening in front of you.** The list doesn't save the person. Noticing does.

---

## THE "I'M NOT SURE" PROBLEM

Here's where most people get stuck:

*"I noticed something felt off. But what if I'm wrong? What if I'm projecting? What if they're just having a bad week? I don't want to insult them."*

**Let me make this simple:**

You don't have to be sure. You don't need evidence. You don't need to diagnose anything.

If something feels off — if your gut says *this person is struggling* — that's enough.

The worst-case scenario if you ask and you're wrong? They say "No, I'm okay. But thanks for asking." Maybe it's slightly awkward for 30 seconds. And then the conversation moves on.

The worst-case scenario if you don't ask and you're right? I don't have to finish that sentence.

**Your gut is a better detector than any checklist.** You know the people in your life. You know when something's different. Trust that.

## CREATING THE RIGHT CONTEXT

### You've spotted the opening. Now what?

You don't just walk up and say "Are you thinking about suicide?" in the break room. Context matters. The way you set up the conversation directly affects whether they'll be honest.

---

### Rule #1: Private, not public

**This conversation happens one-on-one.** Not at dinner with the family. Not in front of coworkers. Not in a group chat.

Pull them aside. Ask them to go for a walk. Show up at their place. Call them when you know they'll be alone.

**Why:** Shame is the biggest barrier to honesty. If there's an audience, shame wins. Every time. Nobody admits to suicidal thoughts with their mother-in-law sitting across the table.

---

### Rule #2: Unhurried, not squeezed in

**Don't ask if you have 5 minutes before you have to leave.**

This conversation deserves real time. Not because it'll necessarily be long — sometimes it's surprisingly short. But they need to feel like you're not going to run. That you have nowhere else to be.

Don't do it right before your shift. Don't do it in the car on the way to drop the kids off. Don't text it while you're multitasking.

**If you can't give them real time right now,** plan it for when you can. It's better to ask tomorrow in a real setting than to rush it today in a parking lot while your phone's buzzing.

---

### Rule #3: Shoulder-to-shoulder works better than face-to-face

**Eye contact is powerful but it's also confrontational.** Especially for people who are already carrying shame.

Some of the best versions of this conversation happen:
- In a car (both looking forward)
- On a walk (side by side)
- Sitting on a porch, not facing each other
- Doing something together — fishing, cooking, working on something

**Shoulder-to-shoulder removes the interrogation feeling.** It makes the conversation feel like something you're doing together, not something you're doing *to* them.

This isn't a rule. Some people do better with eye contact. But if you're not sure, default to a setting where you're next to each other, not across from each other.

---

### Rule #4: Your energy matters more than your words

This is the thing most guides skip.

**If you approach this conversation visibly anxious, panicked, or crying, you've just made it about you.** It doesn't matter if you say the perfect words. If your energy says *I can't handle this,* they'll protect you by lying.

What they need from you:

- **Calm.** Not robotic — human calm. The calm of someone who gives a damn but isn't falling apart.
- **Steady.** You're not going to freak out. You're not going to call their parents. You're not going to disappear.
- **Warm.** Not clinical. Not professional. Not distant. Warm like a person who loves them.

**How to fake it if you're terrified:** Take a breath before you start talking. Unclench your jaw. Drop your shoulders. Speak a little slower than normal. You don't have to feel calm. You just have to present enough calm that they feel safe telling you the truth.

The people who are best at this aren't the bravest. They're the ones who regulate their own nervous system first so the other person feels safe enough to talk.

## REAL-WORLD SCENARIOS

### Scenario 1: The midnight post

Someone you care about posts something at 2 a.m. that feels off. Maybe it's a lyric about disappearing. Maybe it's "I give up." Maybe it's a vague goodbye. Maybe they just posted a photo of something that doesn't make sense at that hour.

**What most people do:** Screenshot it. Show it to someone else. Say "that was weird." Move on.

**What you should do:**

1. Send a private message (not a public comment): *"Hey, I saw your post. I'm thinking about you. Can we talk tomorrow?"*
2. If they don't respond by the next day, call. Don't text again — call or show up.
3. When you talk, mention the post directly: *"I saw what you posted last night. It worried me. I want to ask you something real."*

**Don't pretend you didn't see it.** They know their post was visible. They know people saw it. If nobody says anything, the message they hear is: *Nobody cared enough to ask.*

---

### Scenario 2: The family member who's withdrawing

Someone in your family — sibling, parent, adult child, partner — has been pulling away. Shorter answers. Less eye contact. Fewer calls. They show up to family things but they're not really there.

**What most people do:** Give them space. Say "they're going through something." Wait for them to come back on their own.

**What you should do:**

1. Stop waiting. They're not going to come back on their own. Withdrawal is the strategy, and it's working.
2. Show up physically if you can. Go to their place. Sit with them. Don't demand a conversation — just be there.
3. When the moment feels right — and you'll feel it — say: *"I miss you. I've noticed you seem far away lately. I'm not trying to judge you. Can I ask you something?"*

**Private. Unhurried. Warm.** Let them know you've noticed and you're not going away.

---

### Scenario 3: The coworker with the dark joke

A colleague makes a comment. "Better me than someone else," after a rough shift. Or "I don't know how much longer I can do this." Or they laugh about not making it to retirement. Everyone laughs along because nobody knows what else to do.

**What most people do:** Laugh. Then feel uncomfortable. Then forget about it (or try to).

**What you should do:**

1. Don't address it in the group. Wait for a private moment — after shift, walking to the lot, on a break when nobody else is around.
2. Name it: *"Hey, that thing you said earlier — I don't know if you were joking. I care about you and I want to check."*
3. Let them respond. If they blow it off — "Nah, I was just being stupid" — let it land but stay close: *"Okay. If that ever changes, I'm here. No weird. Just here."*

**The dark joke is a test.** If nobody responds, the person learns: *Nobody takes me seriously. Nobody's going to ask.* If you respond — even awkwardly — they learn: *Someone noticed.*

---

### Scenario 4: The teenager who's unreachable

You're a parent, an uncle, a coach, a teacher. And there's a teenager in your life who's shut down. They're on their phone. They give one-word answers. They used to talk and now they don't. Their grades dropped. They quit the team.

**What most people do:** Blame puberty. Say "they're just a teenager." Assume it's hormones.

**What you should do:**

1. **Don't start with the big question.** Teenagers shut down harder and faster than adults when they feel ambushed.
2. **Start with presence.** Drive them somewhere. Make food. Watch something together. Put yourself in their orbit without making it feel like an interrogation.
3. **Use the "I've been thinking about you" bridge:** *"I don't need you to talk about anything you don't want to. But I've been thinking about you, and I want you to know that I'm noticing you're not yourself lately. If you ever want to talk — or if things feel really heavy — I'm here. Even in the middle of the night. Even if it's something you think I'll be mad about."*
4. **If you decide to ask the direct question,** preface it with: *"I'm going to ask you something because I love you, not because you're in trouble."*

**Teenagers don't respond to authority.** They respond to safety. Your job is to make yourself safe enough that they'd even consider telling you the truth.

## THE "I'VE BEEN THINKING ABOUT YOU" BRIDGE

This is the most versatile tool in this module, and it works for every scenario above.

When you're not sure whether the moment calls for the direct ask yet — when you're not sure if it's a bad day or a dangerous week — the bridge gives you a way in.

> "I've been thinking about you."

That's it. That's the opening line.

**Why it works:**

- It's warm, not clinical
- It's personal — you're not reading from a script
- It doesn't put them on the defensive
- It doesn't require them to explain or confess anything
- It tells them: *You've been on my mind. You matter to me.*

**Then you follow up based on what you've noticed:**

> "I've been thinking about you. I noticed you haven't been around much. I miss you."

> "I've been thinking about you. You seem like you're carrying something heavy. I don't need you to explain. I just want you to know I see it."

> "I've been thinking about you. If there's stuff you can't talk to anyone else about, you can talk to me. Even the scary stuff."

**This bridge gets you into the room. Module 3 teaches you what to say once you're there.**

## WHAT IF YOU MISSED THE OPENING?

It happens. You see a warning sign. You feel the nudge. And you don't act. You freeze, you get distracted, you convince yourself it wasn't that bad.

**Don't beat yourself up. Come back to it.**

There's no expiration date on care. You can bring it up tomorrow. You can bring it up next week. The fact that you missed the first opening doesn't mean you've lost the right to ask.

> "I've been thinking about something from the other day — when you said [that thing]. I should have said something then. I'm saying something now. Can I ask you something?"

**Late is always better than never.** The person who comes back and says "I should have asked sooner" is still the person who asked.

## Your Job in This Module

### WORKSHEET 2: My Scenario Map

**This is the part where theory becomes personal.**

1. **Think of 1–2 people in your life right now who might be struggling.** Don't overthink it. Who comes to mind? You already know. Write their first name or initial.

2. **For each person, answer these:**
   - What have I noticed that concerns me? (Be specific: behavior changes, things they've said, situations they're in)
   - What type of opening am I seeing? (Verbal, behavioral, situational — or a gut feeling I can't explain?)
   - Where would I have this conversation? (Think: private, unhurried, shoulder-to-shoulder)
   - When would I do it? (Not "someday." Pick an actual day or time frame this week.)
   - What's my bridge line? (Write out your version of "I've been thinking about you...")

3. **Acknowledge your resistance.** What's the voice in your head saying right now about why you shouldn't do this? Write it down. Then ask yourself: *Is that reason more important than this person's life?*

4. **Set a deadline.** Not "I'll get to it." Not "when the time feels right." Pick a day. Write it down. Tell someone else about it if you need accountability.

5. **Remember:** You don't need to be perfect. You need to show up. The conversation doesn't have to go smoothly. It just has to happen.

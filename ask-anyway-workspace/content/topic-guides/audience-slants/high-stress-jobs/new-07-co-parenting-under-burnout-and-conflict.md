## What This Helps With

You're both exhausted, you disagree on everything, and the kids are watching. Here's how to co-parent when you can barely co-exist.

This version is written for people whose work life runs hot: high demand, high stakes, low recovery, and pressure that keeps leaking into the rest of life.

## What Is Happening

When work takes a lot out of you, mental health problems often get mislabeled as personality issues or motivation issues. A lot of the strain is cumulative load. Your job may be training your nervous system to stay switched on, rush, perform, and postpone recovery.

Co-parenting while burned out or in conflict usually means you're managing two crises at once: your own depletion and the conflict with the other parent. Kids read both. Your job isn't to fix the co-parent or the conflict instantly—it's to keep yourself functioning and give kids one stable parent.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this pattern gets worse after deadlines, conflict, overload, or long stretches without real recovery.
2. Pick one parenting commitment you'll protect no matter what (bedtime, one meal, one check-in).
3. Take that off the table from conflict negotiations.
4. Keep co-parent communication to logistics only, not emotions.

## What To Say

- Work pressure is shaping this more than I want to admit.
- I need support that works in real life, not advice that assumes I've unlimited downtime.
- I'm running on empty right now and I need to protect my capacity with the kids.
- On this topic, here's what I'm doing regardless. We can talk about the rest when I'm more stable.
- Let's stick to [logistics topic] and I'll keep you in the loop.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Designing a recovery plan that only works on perfect weeks instead of on the weeks you actually live.
- Bringing five old fights into one conversation.
- Trying to win instead of repair.
- Ending without a clear next step.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: decide one thing you and your co-parent can agree on this week. Just one.
- Before bed tonight: write down what the kids need most from both of you right now — not from the best version, the actual version.
- Tomorrow: have one conversation with your co-parent about the kids that stays on the kids. Not the past. The kids.
- Decompression support step: build one deliberate wind-down window into today — not passive (scrolling) but active (a walk, a call, a physical task that clears your head).

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "Co-Parenting Under Burnout and Conflict" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

## What This Helps With

Your chest is tight, you can't breathe, and you're in a grocery store. This is the 90-second plan. Not later. Right now.

This version is written for people whose work life runs hot: high demand, high stakes, low recovery, and pressure that keeps leaking into the rest of life.

## What Is Happening

When work takes a lot out of you, mental health problems often get mislabeled as personality issues or motivation issues. A lot of the strain is cumulative load. Your job may be training your nervous system to stay switched on, rush, perform, and postpone recovery.

Panic in public feels catastrophic because you think everyone is watching and something is medically wrong with you. Neither is true. Your nervous system got a false alarm signal and now it's running a full threat response. You've 90 seconds to interrupt the spiral before it feeds itself.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this pattern gets worse after deadlines, conflict, overload, or long stretches without real recovery.
2. Move to a less-crowded spot if you can, or stay and plant your feet.
3. Do one grounding move: feel your feet on the ground, name five things you see, or do box breathing for 30 seconds.
4. Drink water and text one safe person that you're handling this now.

## What To Say

- Work pressure is shaping this more than I want to admit.
- I need support that works in real life, not advice that assumes I've unlimited downtime.
- My nervous system thinks I'm in danger but I'm not. This will pass.
- I'm using my reset right now and I'm safe.
- I'm in the middle of this and I'll be okay in a few minutes.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Designing a recovery plan that only works on perfect weeks instead of on the weeks you actually live.
- Ignoring early symptoms until they become overwhelming.
- Making big decisions during peak body alarm.
- Skipping follow-up care when patterns repeat.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: practice the 90-second plan — feet flat, cold object in your hand, breathe in for 4 out for 6 — in a safe space so it's ready when you need it.
- Before bed tonight: write a one-sentence card for your wallet: "This is a panic attack. It will pass. Feet, cold, breathe."
- Tomorrow: go somewhere mildly uncomfortable with an exit plan. Practice being in it and leaving when you need to.
- Decompression support step: build one deliberate wind-down window into today — not passive (scrolling) but active (a walk, a call, a physical task that clears your head).

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:

- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

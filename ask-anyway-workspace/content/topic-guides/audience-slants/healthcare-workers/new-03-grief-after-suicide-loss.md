## What This Helps With

If someone you loved died by suicide and you're carrying a grief that nobody around you fully understands — this guide was written for exactly you.

This version is written for healthcare workers who carry clinical pressure, moral stress, compassion fatigue, shift strain, and the weird tension of caring for everybody while neglecting yourself.

## What Is Happening

Healthcare work teaches you to override your own body, emotions, and limits in order to care for other people. That can become a problem fast. You may be good at triaging everyone else while ignoring what's happening in you until it spills over.

Grief after suicide loss carries extra weight: shock, confusion, guilt about things unsaid, and sometimes anger at the person who left. Your brain keeps running counterfactuals—what could have changed this? Nothing you think right now will answer that question. You're in crisis-level grief and your job is survival first, meaning-making later.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether you're applying professional competence to everyone except yourself, and what it's costing you.
2. Tell three people what happened so you aren't holding this alone.
3. Get to one grief group or therapist this week (not optional).
4. Do one physical thing every day: walk, cry, write, punch a pillow. Move the grief through your body.

## What To Say

- I know how to care for people. I'm not doing a good job of applying that same honesty to myself right now.
- This isn't just stress. It's accumulation, moral strain, and not enough recovery.
- I lost someone to suicide. That's what happened.
- I need help carrying this and I'm not going to pretend I'm fine.
- I don't know why they made that choice and right now I just need to get through each day.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using clinical knowledge to explain your own problem while still avoiding the practical steps you would recommend to someone else.
- Waiting for the perfect wording while risk rises.
- Debating whether pain is serious enough before offering support.
- Putting paid resources ahead of immediate safety actions.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: let yourself feel whatever you're feeling without judging it. There's no right way to grieve this.
- Before bed tonight: say their name out loud. To yourself, to someone, to the room. They existed.
- Tomorrow: connect with one person who understands this kind of loss — a support group, a counselor, someone who's been here.
- Boundary support step: use one hard boundary between patient care and your own recovery space today — even imperfect counts, and your health isn't less important than your patients'.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "Grief After Suicide Loss" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

## What This Helps With

When nothing feels like it's going to work and you can't see a reason to keep trying — this guide doesn't pretend it's easy. It just gives you the next step.

This version is written for single-parent life, where mental health problems hit in the middle of responsibility, no backup, and the pressure to keep functioning even when you're depleted.

## What Is Happening

Single-parent stress changes the shape of a problem because there's less margin. You may not get to collapse, sleep in, or hand things off. That means symptoms can get hidden behind competence, and small problems can feel huge because there isn't much buffer.

This topic sits in the safety-first lane: when pain gets loud, your first job is connection and immediate support.
People often wait for certainty before acting. In this lane, you act early and keep someone connected.
You aren't trying to be perfect. You're trying to keep the next hour safer.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask what this problem costs you as a parent, and where you need support before it becomes a crisis of exhaustion or disconnection.
2. Name what you aren'ticing with direct and calm language.
3. Ask one clear safety question and stay present for the answer.
4. Connect to crisis support now (988/741741/911 as needed) and don't leave them isolated.

## What To Say

- I'm carrying a lot with very little margin, and that changes what support needs to look like.
- I don't need judgment. I need practical help and a realistic plan.
- I care about you, and I want to ask something directly so we can keep you safe.
- Are you thinking about suicide right now?
- We can take one step together right now. I'm staying with you while we reach support.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Setting a support plan that assumes you've extra time, extra childcare, or extra energy that you don't actually have.
- Waiting for the perfect wording while risk rises.
- Debating whether pain is serious enough before offering support.
- Putting paid resources ahead of immediate safety actions.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: do one thing — any thing — in the next hour. Brush your teeth. Make the bed. Drink water. Just prove that you can still act.
- Before bed tonight: text one person: "Today was hard." You don't have to explain.
- Tomorrow: do it again. One action, one contact. That's the whole plan until it gets easier.
- Solo support step: identify one 5-to-10 minute window today that belongs only to you — no child need, no task list, no obligation — and protect it the same way you would protect something for your kid.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "Hopelessness First Aid" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

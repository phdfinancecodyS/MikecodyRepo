## What This Helps With

You woke up shaking, sweating, heart pounding — and now you're afraid to go back to sleep. This is the plan for right now, tonight.

This version is written for military and veteran life, where mission, hypervigilance, identity, loss of tribe, and carrying the past all shape how a problem lands.

## What Is Happening

Military life trains your body and mind around vigilance, control, performance, and mission. Even after service, those patterns don't disappear just because the environment changed. A lot of the struggle isn't weakness. It's training, injury, loss, and identity still showing up in civilian life.

Sleep disruption turns up reactivity, worry, and decision fatigue fast.
Most people chase a perfect night. The better move is a repeatable wind-down pattern.
Small consistency beats occasional heroic fixes.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this reaction belongs to your current environment or to training and survival patterns that are still firing after the mission changed.
2. Pick one fixed anchor time for wind-down or wake-up.
3. Run a 20-minute downshift routine without screens.
4. Write tomorrow's first task so your brain has a landing point.

## What To Say

- Part of this is service-related, and I'm not going to shame myself for the fact that it still shows up.
- I need support that respects the way training and loss changed me.
- I'm running on low sleep and I need to simplify tonight.
- I'm doing my reset routine now so tomorrow is easier.
- I'm not fixing everything tonight; I'm building one better night.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Expecting civilian routines, relationships, or workplaces to make sense without accounting for service, transition, or loss of tribe.
- Using late-night scrolling as your off switch.
- Changing your whole routine every day.
- Ignoring caffeine timing when sleep is unstable.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: if you just woke up from one — lights on, feet on the floor, cold water on your face. Orient to the room.
- Before bed tonight: write down one safe image you can redirect to when you wake from a nightmare. Practice visualizing it.
- Tomorrow: tell one person what's happening at night. Nightmares lose power when they're spoken.
- Peer support step: lean on one person from your unit, your post, a vet peer support network, or your VA team today — calling for backup isn't a weakness in the field and it isn't one now.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "Nightmare Recovery Plan" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

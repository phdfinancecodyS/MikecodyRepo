## What This Helps With

You're trying to be a good parent while running on fumes. If you feel like you're failing at the thing that matters most, this guide is honest about what helps.

This version is written with neurodivergent life in mind, including overwhelm, sensory load, executive function strain, rejection sensitivity, masking, and burnout.

## What Is Happening

A lot of mental health advice quietly assumes a nervous system and executive function style that you may not have. If you're neurodivergent, the same problem can hit harder because of sensory overload, task switching, time blindness, social effort, or the cost of masking. That doesn't mean you're doing it wrong. It means the plan has to fit your brain.

Parenting on empty means you're out of fuel. Patience is gone. Presence is impossible. You're in survival mode. Your kids read your absence and they act out more because they feel the disconnection. You feel guilt about not being the parent you want to be.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being intensified by sensory overload, masking, executive friction, or the cost of trying to function in a way that doesn't fit you.
2. Pick one non-negotiable parenting anchor (bedtime, one meal together, morning check-in) and protect only that.
3. Release guilt about everything else. You can't do all things when you're depleted.
4. Get help with the non-anchors (school pickup, supper prep, bedtime on your off-nights).

## What To Say

- I need a plan that fits how my brain actually works, not advice built for someone else’s system.
- Some of this isn't laziness or avoidance. It's executive and sensory load.
- I'm running on empty and I can only show up for [one specific thing].
- I need help with [specific tasks] so I can protect my presence for what matters most.
- When I come back to capacity, I'll be more available. Right now, this is what I can do.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using shame to force yourself into systems, routines, or expectations that are already failing your nervous system.
- Trying to fix the whole relationship in one conversation.
- Having the conversation when neither person is regulated enough for it.
- Skipping clear repair, boundaries, or follow-up after the hard moment.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: give yourself one 15-minute break today. Not when the kids are asleep — before that. Let something be messy.
- Before bed tonight: name one thing you did right as a parent today, even if it was small.
- Tomorrow: ask for one specific help from one specific person. Not "I need help" — "can you take them for an hour?"
- Brain-friendly support step: use a structure that works with how your brain actually operates — a visual reminder, a body double, a voice memo, or a low-barrier check-in that doesn't require sitting down and writing.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "Parenting on Empty" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

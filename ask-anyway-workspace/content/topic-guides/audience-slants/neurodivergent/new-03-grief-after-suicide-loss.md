## What This Helps With

If someone you loved died by suicide and you're carrying a grief that nobody around you fully understands — this guide was written for exactly you.

This version is written with neurodivergent life in mind, including overwhelm, sensory load, executive function strain, rejection sensitivity, masking, and burnout.

## What Is Happening

A lot of mental health advice quietly assumes a nervous system and executive function style that you may not have. If you're neurodivergent, the same problem can hit harder because of sensory overload, task switching, time blindness, social effort, or the cost of masking. That doesn't mean you're doing it wrong. It means the plan has to fit your brain.

Grief after suicide loss carries extra weight: shock, confusion, guilt about things unsaid, and sometimes anger at the person who left. Your brain keeps running counterfactuals—what could have changed this? Nothing you think right now will answer that question. You're in crisis-level grief and your job is survival first, meaning-making later.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask whether this problem is being intensified by sensory overload, masking, executive friction, or the cost of trying to function in a way that doesn't fit you.
2. Tell three people what happened so you aren't holding this alone.
3. Get to one grief group or therapist this week (not optional).
4. Do one physical thing every day: walk, cry, write, punch a pillow. Move the grief through your body.

## What To Say

- I need a plan that fits how my brain actually works, not advice built for someone else’s system.
- Some of this isn't laziness or avoidance. It's executive and sensory load.
- I lost someone to suicide. That's what happened.
- I need help carrying this and I'm not going to pretend I'm fine.
- I don't know why they made that choice and right now I just need to get through each day.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using shame to force yourself into systems, routines, or expectations that are already failing your nervous system.
- Waiting for the perfect wording while risk rises.
- Debating whether pain is serious enough before offering support.
- Putting paid resources ahead of immediate safety actions.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: let yourself feel whatever you're feeling without judging it. There's no right way to grieve this.
- Before bed tonight: say their name out loud. To yourself, to someone, to the room. They existed.
- Tomorrow: connect with one person who understands this kind of loss — a support group, a counselor, someone who's been here.
- Brain-friendly support step: use a structure that works with how your brain actually operates — a visual reminder, a body double, a voice memo, or a low-barrier check-in that doesn't require sitting down and writing.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "Grief After Suicide Loss" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?

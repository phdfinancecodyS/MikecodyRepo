## What This Helps With

Yesterday was bad. Today you feel like you got hit by a truck. This is the day-after plan — because nobody talks about what to do the morning after a hard one.

This version is written for educators dealing with emotional labor, performance pressure, burnout, care overload, and the reality of trying to keep showing up while running low.

## What Is Happening

Education work asks for constant emotional output, patience, attention, and management of other people’s needs. That can make mental health problems feel invisible because you're still performing. But being able to teach or lead a room doesn't mean you're okay.

The day after an incident, your nervous system crashes. Adrenaline is gone, emotions hit, and your body feels wrecked. This isn't weakness—this is what happens after intense activation. The crash is part of the cycle and managing it well prevents worse spirals later.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this pattern is tied to the emotional labor of always being on, available, and responsible for the tone of the room.
2. Move slowly and don't schedule anything heavy for the next 24 hours.
3. Eat and hydrate like you're recovering from the flu.
4. Lower your bar for today; one small win is enough.

## What To Say

- I can still do the job and still be struggling.
- I need support that accounts for emotional labor, not just workload.
- Today I'm recovering, not performing.
- My body is telling me it needs downtime and I'm listening.
- I made it through yesterday. Today is about stability.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using your ability to stay functional in front of others as proof that nothing needs attention.
- Doing nothing while waiting to feel perfect.
- Trying to fix everything in one conversation.
- Skipping follow-up after the first step.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: hydrate, eat something, and do the minimum. Today is a recovery day, not a performance day.
- Before bed tonight: tell one person what happened yesterday — not the whole story, just enough to break the silence.
- Tomorrow: check in with yourself. If you're still in the crash, that's okay. If you're surfacing, pick up one thing.
- Boundary support step: use one habit that genuinely separates your teacher identity from your recovery space today — no lesson plans, no parent emails, no student problems — even for 30 minutes.

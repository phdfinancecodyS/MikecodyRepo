## What This Helps With

Your teammate is struggling and you want to say something but you don't know how. This gives you the words — not clinical ones, real ones.

This version is written for educators dealing with emotional labor, performance pressure, burnout, care overload, and the reality of trying to keep showing up while running low.

## What Is Happening

Education work asks for constant emotional output, patience, attention, and management of other people’s needs. That can make mental health problems feel invisible because you're still performing. But being able to teach or lead a room doesn't mean you're okay.

Peer support means you aren't a therapist and you aren't responsible for fixing them. Your job is to listen, name what you see, and connect them to actual help. Many peer supporters burn out because they absorb the emotional weight of the problem.

## What To Do Now

1. Ask how much of this pattern is tied to the emotional labor of always being on, available, and responsible for the tone of the room.
2. Listen to what they say without trying to solve it in one conversation.
3. Name back what you heard so they feel seen.
4. Name one concrete resource and leave it with them rather than trying to make them use it.

## What To Say

- I can still do the job and still be struggling.
- I need support that accounts for emotional labor, not just workload.
- I hear you. That sounds really hard.
- Here's what I see: [what they're up against]. And here's where you get actual professional help.
- I'm here to check in with you, and I'm also not the person who fixes this.

## Common Mistakes To Avoid

- Using your ability to stay functional in front of others as proof that nothing needs attention.
- Doing nothing while waiting to feel perfect.
- Trying to fix everything in one conversation.
- Skipping follow-up after the first step.

## 24-Hour Action Plan

- Right now: think about the teammate you're worried about. Decide if you're going to approach them today.
- Before bed tonight: practice your opener: "I've noticed something and I want to check on you. Can we talk?"
- Tomorrow: find a private moment and ask. You're not their therapist — you're their teammate showing up.
- Boundary support step: use one habit that genuinely separates your teacher identity from your recovery space today — no lesson plans, no parent emails, no student problems — even for 30 minutes.

## Worksheet 1: Pattern Finder

Goal: Spot the pattern — when it shows up, what triggers it, and what you notice first.

Prompts:
- When did "Peer-Support Conversations for Teammates" last show up for you? What was the situation?
- What did you notice first — in your body, your thoughts, or your behavior?
- What makes this pattern worse? What conditions or stressors fuel it?
- What have you tried before, and what actually helped (even a little)?
- Who in your life knows about this? Who doesn't — and why?
